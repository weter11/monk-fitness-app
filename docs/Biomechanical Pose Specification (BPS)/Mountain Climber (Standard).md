# Mountain Climber (Standard)

## 1. Exercise Overview
The Mountain Climber is a dynamic plank-based conditioning exercise that adds a running-like knee drive to a stable plank. From a plank position (straight arms or forearms), the athlete alternately drives each knee toward the chest in a continuous, controlled motion, then returns the foot to the floor. It blends trunk rigidity with hip-flexion mobility and cardiovascular demand. The canonical sub-pose is the "knee-driven-up" position — one knee tucked toward the chest while the other leg remains extended and the trunk holds a rigid plank. The center of mass shifts slightly as the legs alternate, but the trunk stays level.

## 2. Pose Purpose
- Develop trunk (core) rigidity under dynamic limb movement.
- Improve hip-flexion mobility and hip-flexor strength (iliopsoas, rectus femoris).
- Elevate heart rate for conditioning (a metabolic/calisthenic drill).
- Train coordination of alternating contralateral/ipsilateral limb patterns.
- Reinforce a neutral-spine plank under load and motion.

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment
- Plank: a straight line from head to heels (straight-arm or forearm plank).
- The trunk is rigid — no sagging at the hips, no hiking.
- As one knee drives up, the hips stay level (no rotation or hiking on the driving side).
- The movement is at the hip joints; the spine does not move.
- Pace is controlled-to-brisk; the trunk remains a fixed platform.

## 4. Head
- Head is in neutral alignment with the spine, eyes down/forward.
- The neck is long; the head does not drop (which would break the plank line) or crane up.
- The cervical spine stays in line with the thoracic plank.

## 5. Spine
- **Whole spine:** held in a neutral, rigid plank — a straight line from the crown of the head through the thoracic and lumbar to the pelvis.
- There is no spinal flexion, extension, or rotation during the knee drive; the core braces to prevent any movement.
- The lumbar stays neutral (not arched, not rounded); the thoracic stays long.

## 6. Upper Body
- Straight-arm version: hands under shoulders, arms extended, shoulders depressed and stable (scapular set).
- Forearm version: forearms on the floor, elbows under shoulders.
- The shoulders are the stable base; they do not shrug or protract excessively.
- The chest is open; the scapular stabilizers (serratus, trapezius) hold the shoulder girdle fixed so the trunk stays rigid.

## 7. Lower Body
- **Driving leg:** hip flexed, knee tucked toward the chest; the foot may be lifted just off the floor or lightly tapping. The knee drives roughly under or toward the chest, not splayed out to the side.
- **Support leg:** extended, hip extended, quadriceps and glute engaged to hold the plank line.
- Hips stay level — the driving side does NOT hike up; the pelvis does not rotate.
- Feet alternate; the non-driving foot stays planted on the floor until it drives.
- Knees track in line with the hips, not collapsing inward.

## 8. Contacts
- Hands (palms) on the floor under the shoulders (straight-arm) OR forearms on the floor (forearm plank).
- The support foot/feet on the floor (toes or ball of foot).
- The driving foot is off the floor (or lightly tapping) — it is not a contact in the driven position.

## 9. Range of Motion
- Driving-hip flexion: from extended (plank) to ~90–110° (knee tucked toward chest).
- Support-hip extension: maintained near the plank's neutral extension.
- Knee flexion of the driving leg: to bring the heel toward the glute/foot toward chest.
- The motion is repetitive/cyclic; tempo varies from slow-controlled to fast-conditioning.

## 10. Muscle Intent
- Core (rectus abdominis, obliques, transversus): brace the trunk rigid against movement.
- Hip flexors (iliopsoas, rectus femoris): drive the knee up.
- Glutes and quadriceps of the support leg: hold the extended plank line.
- Shoulder stabilizers (serratus anterior, trapezius, deltoid, triceps): hold the upper body fixed.
- Hip extensors of the driving side: control the return to plank.

## 11. Visual Checkpoints
- [ ] The body forms a straight plank line from head to heels.
- [ ] The trunk is rigid — no sagging or hiking at the hips.
- [ ] One knee is clearly driven toward the chest in the canonical sub-pose.
- [ ] The support leg is extended and engaged.
- [ ] Hips stay level — the driving side does not hike up.
- [ ] The pelvis does not rotate as the knee drives.
- [ ] Shoulders are stable and down, not shrugged.
- [ ] The head is neutral, in line with the spine.
- [ ] Knees track in line with the hips, not splayed outward.
- [ ] The driving foot is off the floor (or lightly tapping).
- [ ] Breathing is rhythmic, matching the drive tempo.
- [ ] Each drive returns the foot to the floor before the other drives.
- [ ] The spine does not flex, extend, or twist during the movement.
- [ ] Movement is controlled, not flailing.
- [ ] Both sides drive symmetrically.

## 12. Common Mistakes
- Hiking the hips up (turning it into a downward-dog pike) on the driving side.
- Letting the low back sag (loss of plank rigidity).
- Rotating the pelvis as the knee drives.
- Splaying the driving knee way out to the side.
- Dropping the head, breaking the plank line.
- Shrugging the shoulders up toward the ears.
- Bouncing/flailing with no trunk control.

## 13. Acceptance Criteria
- [ ] The trunk holds a rigid, neutral-spine plank throughout the cyclic movement.
- [ ] Each knee drive brings the knee clearly toward the chest with the support leg extended.
- [ ] The hips remain level and the pelvis does not rotate.
- [ ] The shoulders stay stable and depressed and the head stays in neutral alignment.
- [ ] The movement is controlled and symmetrical side-to-side with rhythmic breathing.
