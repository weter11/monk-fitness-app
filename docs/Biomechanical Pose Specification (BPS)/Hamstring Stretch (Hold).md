# Hamstring Stretch (Hold)

## 1. Exercise Overview
The Hamstring Stretch is a static flexibility hold that lengthens the posterior thigh musculature — the semimembranosus, semitendinosus, and biceps femoris (the hamstrings), which span the hip and knee. It is most commonly performed seated on the floor with one leg extended straight and the other bent, hinging forward at the hip; it may also be performed standing with one heel propped on a low surface. The canonical position is the held end-range of a hip hinge: the extended leg is straight, the knee locked, the foot dorsi-flexed or pointed, and the trunk folds forward from the hip joint (not by rounding the spine). The stretch sensation is felt along the back of the thigh.

## 2. Pose Purpose
- Lengthen the hamstrings and the posterior-chain fascia of the thigh.
- Improve hip flexion range of motion with the knee extended (a distinct demand from bent-knee hip flexion).
- Reduce posterior-thigh tightness that limits bending, reaching, and athletic movement.
- Teach the distinction between hinging at the hip and rounding the low back.
- Provide a calm, sustained mobility hold (typically 20–60 seconds).

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment
- Seated version: sit on the floor (or a low bench), one leg extended, the other bent with foot tucked toward the inner thigh.
- Standing version: stand with one heel elevated on a low step/prop, standing leg slightly bent.
- The trunk folds forward by rotating the pelvis on the femur (hip hinge), keeping the spine long.
- The chest stays open; the fold comes from the hip, not spinal flexion.
- Weight is balanced; the sit bones (seated) or standing foot remain grounded and stable.

## 4. Head
- Head is carried in line with the spine as it hinges forward — a continuation of the long trunk, not dropped heavily or cranked up.
- The neck is neutral; the gaze is downward/forward, not strained.
- The head does not lead the movement; it follows the thoracic hinge.

## 5. Spine
- **Lumbar + thoracic:** maintained in a long, neutral-to-slightly-flexed line that mirrors the hip hinge. The spine should be straight (a "flat back"), NOT rounded segment by segment.
- The flexion occurs at the hip joint (pelvis tilting forward over the femur), not by crumbling the vertebra.
- Cervical spine follows the thoracic line gently.
- A "rounded back" indicates the stretch is being faked through spinal flexion rather than true hamstring length.

## 6. Upper Body
- Arms reach toward the extended foot (seated) or rest on the propped leg / reach forward (standing).
- Shoulders are relaxed and down, not shrugged.
- The chest stays open and the upper back long rather than collapsed forward.
- Hands may hold the shin, ankle, or foot — whatever is reachable without rounding the back.

## 7. Lower Body
- **Extended leg:** knee fully straight (locked), quadriceps engaged; the stretch is specifically a knee-extended hamstring lengthening.
- Foot of the extended leg: dorsi-flexed (toes toward shin) to add a mild calf/soleus component, or pointed — consensus favors dorsi-flexion for a cleaner hamstring line, but both are acceptable.
- **Bent leg (seated):** knee flexed, foot tucked in; this leg is not stretched.
- **Standing version:** standing knee soft (slightly bent) to protect the joint; the stretched leg's knee is straight.
- Pelvis stays square; the extended-leg hip does not rotate outward.

## 8. Contacts
- Seated: the ischial tuberosities (sit bones) and the bent-leg foot contact the floor; the extended heel rests on the floor.
- Standing: one foot on the floor, the other heel on a low prop/step.
- Hands may contact the shin/foot but are not bearing the body's weight.

## 9. Range of Motion
- Hip flexion (with knee extended): from neutral to the end range where the hamstring tension is felt — typically 70–100°+ depending on flexibility.
- Knee extension of the stretched leg: full (0°).
- Ankle: neutral to dorsi-flexed.
- The end range is a comfortable tension, not a sharp pain; hold 20–60 s, repeat 2–4 times.

## 10. Muscle Intent
- Hamstrings (semitendinosus, semimembranosus, biceps femoris): the primary lengthened (stretched) tissues.
- Gluteus maximus and erector spinae: lightly engaged to keep the pelvis hinged and the spine long (protective tension, not maximal contraction).
- Quadriceps of the extended leg: contracted to keep the knee straight.
- Gastrocnemius/soleus: gently lengthened if the ankle is dorsi-flexed.
- Deep core: lightly braced to support the neutral spine through the hinge.

## 11. Visual Checkpoints
- [ ] The stretched knee is fully straight (not bent).
- [ ] The spine is long and flat (a single line), not rounded segment by segment.
- [ ] The forward fold comes from the hip (pelvis tipping forward), not spinal crumbling.
- [ ] The chest is open and reaching, not collapsed.
- [ ] Shoulders are relaxed and down, not shrugged.
- [ ] The extended foot is dorsi-flexed or pointed consistently.
- [ ] The pelvis stays square, not rotating open on the stretched side.
- [ ] The head follows the line of the spine, not dropped or cranked.
- [ ] The non-stretched (bent) leg is comfortable and stable.
- [ ] Tension is felt along the back of the thigh, not in the low back or knee.
- [ ] Breathing is slow and easy through the hold.
- [ ] The position is held statically, not bouncing.
- [ ] Weight is balanced on the sit bones / standing foot.
- [ ] The reach (arm/hand) is a consequence of the hinge, not a pull that rounds the back.
- [ ] No sharp knee or low-back pain.

## 12. Common Mistakes
- Rounding the spine to reach farther (faking the stretch through the back, not the hamstring).
- Bending the stretched knee to get lower.
- Letting the pelvis rotate so the stretched hip turns outward.
- Shrugging the shoulders or collapsing the chest.
- Bouncing at end range.
- Pointing the toes so hard the calf competes and the hamstring line is lost (or conversely, ignoring ankle position).
- Holding the breath.

## 13. Acceptance Criteria
- [ ] The stretched leg's knee is fully extended and the hamstring tension is felt along the posterior thigh.
- [ ] The spine is maintained long and neutral; flexion occurs at the hip joint.
- [ ] The pelvis stays square and the chest reaches forward without rounding.
- [ ] The position is held statically with relaxed breathing for the target duration.
- [ ] No sharp pain is felt in the knee or low back.
