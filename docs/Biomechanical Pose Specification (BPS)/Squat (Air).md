# Biomechanical Pose Specification — Squat (Air)

## 1. Exercise overview
The bodyweight (air) squat is a fundamental closed-chain, bilateral lower-body movement performed
without external load. It is a compound, multi-joint pattern driven primarily by the hip and knee
extensors, with the ankle dorsiflexors and plantarflexors regulating shin angle and balance. The
movement descends through coordinated hip, knee, and ankle flexion and ascends through coordinated
extension. It is the base pattern from which loaded and variant squats are derived.

## 2. Pose purpose
To build squat mechanics, lower-body strength, and coordinated hip/knee/ankle mobility using only
body mass. The pose establishes a stable base of support, a controlled center of mass over the feet,
and a repeatable descent–ascent rhythm. It is used for warm-up, movement literacy, conditioning, and
as the unloaded template for all other squat patterns.

## 3. Whole-body alignment
- Feet roughly hip-to-shoulder width apart, pointed forward or very slightly outward.
- Center of mass stays within the base of support, tracking vertically over the midfoot.
- Head, trunk, and pelvis move as a relatively integrated unit; the trunk leans forward as the hips
  drop so the combined center of mass remains over the feet.
- At the top, the body is fully extended: hips, knees, and ankles straight, trunk vertical.
- Symmetry: both limbs share load; the pelvis stays level (no hip drop to either side).

## 4. Head
- Cervical spine in neutral alignment with the trunk; gaze directed forward and slightly down.
- No excessive neck flexion, extension, or lateral tilt.
- The head should not lead the movement or jut forward; it follows the thoracic line.

## 5. Spine
- Lumbar and thoracic spine maintain a neutral, braced posture (a natural, not exaggerated, curve).
- The trunk flexes forward at the hips as depth increases — this is hip-driven trunk lean, not spinal
  rounding.
- Avoid lumbar flexion (rounding) or excessive extension (arching). The spine angles forward from
  vertical to roughly 25–45° at moderate depth, more as depth increases.
- Abdominal and paraspinal muscles co-contract to resist unwanted spinal movement (bracing).

## 6. Upper body
- Arms hang naturally; many trainees extend the arms forward at shoulder height for counterbalance,
  which helps keep the chest up and the center of mass shifted rearward over the midfoot.
- Shoulders stay down and back (scapulae stable), not shrugged.
- The upper body does not collapse; the chest remains open as the trunk leans.

## 7. Lower body
- Descent sequence: hip flexion leads, followed by knee flexion, then ankle dorsiflexion; the hips
  sit back and down.
- Knees track in the same direction as the toes, no inward valgus collapse.
- Descend until the thighs are at least parallel to the floor (hip crease at or below the knee).
- At the bottom, the hips are deeply flexed, knees flexed, ankles dorsiflexed; heels remain flat.
- Ascent: hips and knees extend together; ankles move from dorsiflexion toward neutral; the chest
  rises and the trunk becomes more vertical.
- At the top: full hip, knee, and ankle extension; glutes and quads engaged.

## 8. Contacts
- Both feet flat on the floor, weight distributed across the whole foot with bias toward the midfoot
  and heel.
- No hand contact with the ground or other support (unless used for balance aid, which is not part of
  the canonical pose).
- The base of support is the two footprints; the center of mass must remain within this base.

## 9. Range of Motion
- Hip flexion: to at least ~100–120° (thigh parallel to floor or lower; greater at deeper depths).
- Knee flexion: to at least ~100–120° (tibiae roughly horizontal at parallel depth).
- Ankle dorsiflexion: sufficient to keep heels down with shins angled forward ~25–45° from vertical.
- Trunk forward lean: ~25–45° from vertical at parallel depth.
- Full extension at the top: 0° at hip, knee, and ankle (neutral standing).
- Individual depth is limited by ankle mobility, hip structure, and trunk-control capacity.

## 10. Muscle intent
- Primary movers (concentric on ascent): gluteus maximus and hamstrings (hip extensors); quadriceps
  (knee extensors); soleus/gastrocnemius (ankle plantarflexors at the top of the stand).
- Eccentric control on descent: same muscles lengthening under load.
- Stabilizers: erector spinae and abdominal wall (spinal bracing); gluteus medius and tensor
  fasciae latae (pelvic and knee-valgus control); tibialis anterior (ankle control);
  intrinsic foot muscles (arch support).

## 11. Visual checkpoints
- [ ] Feet flat, roughly hip-to-shoulder width, toes forward or slightly out.
- [ ] Weight centered over midfoot/heel, not on the toes.
- [ ] Hips begin to move back and down before the knees lead.
- [ ] Knees track directly over the toes, no inward collapse.
- [ ] Trunk leans forward from the hips, not rounded in the back.
- [ ] Chest stays open and lifted.
- [ ] Shoulders relaxed, not shrugged.
- [ ] Arms forward for counterbalance or hanging naturally.
- [ ] Thighs reach at least parallel to the floor.
- [ ] Heels remain in contact with the floor at the bottom.
- [ ] Pelvis stays level, no side-to-side drop.
- [ ] Head neutral, gaze forward.
- [ ] Spine stays braced and neutral through the descent.
- [ ] Ascent drives through the full foot, then heel.
- [ ] Hips and knees extend together, not knees-first.
- [ ] At the top, hips fully extended, not tucked under.
- [ ] Breathing controlled; brace on the way down, exhale on the way up.
- [ ] Movement is smooth and controlled, no bouncing at the bottom.
- [ ] Symmetry between left and right limb contribution.
- [ ] Full standing lockout at the top with neutral spine.

## 12. Common mistakes
- Knees caving inward (valgus) — weak glute medius, poor ankle/knee tracking.
- Heels lifting off the floor — limited ankle dorsiflexion or shifting weight forward.
- Rounding the lower back (butt-wink / lumbar flexion) — excessive depth beyond tissue capacity or
  poor bracing.
- Leading with the knees instead of the hips — shifts pattern toward a knee-dominant squat.
- Shifting weight onto the toes — destabilizes and stresses the knees.
- Excessive forward trunk lean from spinal flexion rather than hip hinge.
- Not reaching depth (parallel) when depth is the goal.
- Asymmetric weight shift, causing one hip to drop.

## 13. Acceptance criteria
- [ ] Both feet remain flat with weight over the midfoot/heel throughout.
- [ ] Descent reaches at least thigh-parallel to the floor.
- [ ] Knees track over the toes with no valgus collapse.
- [ ] Spine remains neutral and braced; trunk lean originates at the hips.
- [ ] Full hip/knee/ankle extension achieved at the top.
- [ ] Movement is bilateral, symmetric, and controlled.
- [ ] Center of mass stays within the base of the feet.
- [ ] No heel lift, no spinal rounding, no knee cave.
