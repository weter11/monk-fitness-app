# Pelvic Tilt (Standard)

## 1. Exercise Overview
The Pelvic Tilt is a foundational core and lumbar-control drill performed supine (lying on the back) with the knees bent and feet flat on the floor. It teaches conscious control of the pelvis and the connected lumbar spine by moving between two end ranges: the posterior pelvic tilt (the pelvis rotates so the low back flattens toward the floor — often called an "imprint") and the anterior pelvic tilt (the pelvis rotates the opposite way so the low back arches away from the floor). The canonical sub-poses are these two held end ranges, performed slowly and with control. It is a low-load movement literacy exercise used in Pilates, rehab, and general training.

## 2. Pose Purpose
- Develop conscious, isolated control of the pelvis and lumbar spine.
- Teach the difference between posterior and anterior pelvic tilt and their effect on the low back.
- Improve lumbo-pelvic stabilization and awareness (the basis for safe hinging, lifting, and bracing).
- Mobilize the sacroiliac and lumbosacral junctions through a small, controlled range.
- Prepare the body for more complex core and hip exercises.

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment
- Supine: back on the floor, knees bent ~90°, feet flat hip-width apart.
- The upper body stays relaxed; arms may rest at the sides or on the abdomen.
- Motion is isolated to the pelvis and low back — the trunk above the lumbar region and the legs stay quiet.
- Movement is slow and breathed; tilt on exhale, return on inhale.
- The rib cage and shoulders stay soft on the floor.

## 4. Head
- Head rests on the floor, neutral, in line with the spine.
- The cervical spine does not move relative to the thorax during the tilt.
- No craning or lifting of the head.

## 5. Spine
- **Posterior tilt (imprint):** the pelvis rotates posteriorly, the lumbar spine flattens toward the floor (reduced lordosis), the sacrum presses down.
- **Anterior tilt (arch):** the pelvis rotates anteriorly, the lumbar lordosis increases (low back lifts off the floor).
- The thoracic spine stays in gentle contact with the floor; the motion is concentrated at the lumbopelvic junction.
- The transition between the two is the drill; neither end should be forced into discomfort.

## 6. Upper Body
- Shoulders relaxed on the floor, not braced or shrugged.
- Arms resting; hands may rest on the lower abdomen to feel the tilt.
- The chest stays soft; no gripping in the neck or shoulders.
- No pushing through the arms to create the motion.

## 7. Lower Body
- Knees bent, feet flat, hip-width apart, stable.
- The tilt is driven by the abdominal wall and glutes/hamstrings, NOT by lifting the feet or sliding the feet.
- Hips stay level side-to-side; one side should not tilt more than the other.
- The legs remain quiet; only the pelvis rotates.

## 8. Contacts
- The posterior trunk (head, upper back, sacrum/low back depending on tilt direction) contacts the floor.
- Both feet flat on the floor.
- The backs of the shoulders/upper arms rest on the floor.

## 9. Range of Motion
- Pelvic rotation: posterior tilt to anterior tilt, a small arc (often only a few degrees of true pelvic rotation, with the lumbar spine moving through its lordosis range).
- Lumbar flexion/extension: from flattened (posterior tilt) to gently arched (anterior tilt).
- Performed as slow reps (10–20) or as held end-range holds (5–10 s each).

## 10. Muscle Intent
- **Posterior tilt:** abdominal wall (rectus abdominis, obliques) and gluteals/hamstrings draw the pelvis into posterior rotation; the low back flattens.
- **Anterior tilt:** hip flexors (iliopsoas) and lumbar erector spinae gently arch the low back; the abdominal wall yields.
- Pelvic floor and deep core (transversus abdominis) engage to stabilize.
- Quadriceps/feet: remain quiet, just grounding.

## 11. Visual Checkpoints
- [ ] Body is supine, knees bent, feet flat and hip-width apart.
- [ ] In posterior tilt the low back flattens fully toward the floor.
- [ ] In anterior tilt the low back arches, lifting slightly off the floor.
- [ ] The motion is isolated to the pelvis/low back, not the whole trunk.
- [ ] Feet stay flat and still (not sliding or lifting).
- [ ] Knees stay level, not dropping side to side.
- [ ] Shoulders remain relaxed on the floor, not shrugged.
- [ ] Head stays neutral on the floor.
- [ ] The tilt is slow and controlled, not ballistic.
- [ ] Breathing is smooth — exhale into the tilt.
- [ ] One side of the pelvis does not tilt more than the other.
- [ ] The upper back stays in soft contact with the floor.
- [ ] No butt-clenching beyond what drives the tilt.
- [ ] The range is comfortable, not forced into pain.
- [ ] Arms stay relaxed at the sides.

## 12. Common Mistakes
- Lifting the feet or sliding them to force the tilt.
- Using the shoulders/arms to rock the body instead of the pelvis.
- Over-arching the neck or lifting the head.
- Tilting one side more than the other (asymmetry).
- Holding the breath.
- Forcing the anterior tilt so far the ribs flare and the low back compresses.
- Bracing the glutes so hard the pelvis can't move.

## 13. Acceptance Criteria
- [ ] The pelvis rotates between a clear posterior-tilt (low back flattened) and anterior-tilt (low back arched) end range.
- [ ] The motion is isolated to the lumbopelvic region with the legs and upper body quiet.
- [ ] Feet remain flat and stable, knees level, throughout.
- [ ] The movement is slow, controlled, and breathing-paced.
- [ ] No asymmetry, neck tension, or low-back discomfort is present.
