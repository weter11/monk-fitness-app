# Biomechanical Pose Specification — Couch Stretch (Hold)

> **Canonical reference.** This document defines what a couch (or "couch"
> hip-flexor) stretch should look like from a strict biomechanics, anatomy, and
> physical-therapy standpoint. It is implementation-independent and describes
> human movement only. It must remain valid even if the rendering system behind
> it is rewritten from scratch.
>
> Sources: sports biomechanics, physical-therapy literature, strength &
> conditioning practice, and human anatomy (notably hip-flexor anatomy: psoas
> major, iliacus, rectus femoris). Where expert opinion differs, the consensus is
> stated and the alternatives are noted.

---

## 1. Exercise Overview

**Movement goal.** The couch stretch is a static hip-flexor stretch performed in
a half-kneeling position with the rear shin pinned vertically against a wall,
bench, or similar prop (the rear foot is up, ankle dorsiflexed, foot against the
surface) and the front foot planted forward. The pelvis is tucked under (posterior
pelvic tilt) to bias the stretch toward the rear hip flexors (psoas major,
iliacus, and especially the two-joint rectus femoris). The torso is upright.

**Training goal.** Lengthen the anterior hip structures of the rear (kneeling)
side — primarily the psoas/iliacus and the rectus femoris (which also crosses the
knee) — and improve anterior hip/thalmic mobility and pelvic positioning. It is a
mobility/ flexibility hold, not a strength movement.

**Intended muscles (stretched).**

- Rear side: psoas major, iliacus (collectively iliopsoas), rectus femoris
  (also knee-extensor head of the quadriceps), and to a lesser degree the
  sartorius and tensor fasciae latae.
- Front side: anterior hip structures are shorter/less loaded but still in a
  neutral-to-slightly-open position.

**Intended muscles (stabilizing / engaged).**

- Gluteus maximus and abdominal wall (posterior pelvic tilt / pelvic tuck).
- Quadriceps of the front thigh (knee extension hold).
- Trunk: erector spinae, obliques (upright posture), gluteus medius (pelvic
  leveling).

**Common execution style.** Facing away from a wall, the athlete places the rear
shin vertically against the wall with the rear foot up (toes pointing up, ankle
dorsiflexed) and drops into a half-kneel: the rear knee on the floor, the front
foot planted forward with the front knee at ~90°. The pelvis is tucked under
(posterior tilt) to drive the stretch into the front of the rear hip. The torso
is tall. The hold is typically 30–120 s per side, breathing steadily.

---

## 2. Pose Purpose

This specification treats the **held bottom position** of the couch stretch as
the canonical audited pose.

**Held position.** The athlete is in a half-kneel: the rear knee rests on the
floor directly under (or slightly behind) the rear hip, and the rear shin is
vertical against the prop with the rear foot up (ankle dorsiflexed, toes
pointing upward). The front foot is planted forward, the front knee flexed to
~90° and tracking over the front toe. The pelvis is in a deliberate posterior
tilt ("tucked under") so the anterior superior iliac spines rotate back and down,
maximizing the stretch through the rear iliopsoas and rectus femoris. The trunk
is upright. Weight is shared between the front foot and the rear knee/shin
contact.

**Rear shin against prop.** The rear shin is flush against the wall/bench; the
rear foot is elevated (not on the floor). This traps the rear knee in deep
flexion and the hip in extension, isolating the hip flexors.

**Pelvic tuck.** The key distinguishing feature vs a generic half-kneel: the
pelvis is posteriorly tilted to increase the anterior-hip stretch. The lumbar
spine is therefore flattened (not arched).

**Hold.** Static. No bouncing. The stretch is felt at the front of the rear hip
and, for the rectus femoris, the front of the rear thigh.

---

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment

**Center of mass.** The whole-body COM sits over the base formed by the front
foot and the rear knee/shin contact. It is roughly centered between them, leaning
slightly forward over the front foot. It must remain within this support region.

**Balance.** Load is shared: the front foot bears the larger share (the working
leg), and the rear knee/shin contact bears the rear share and stabilizes the
rear-hip extension. The body is balanced in the sagittal plane.

**Symmetry.** Not mirror-symmetric (half-kneel). The pelvis is level side-to-side
(no contralateral hip drop) and square (facing forward, not rotated). The
shoulders are level and square to the pelvis.

**Posture.** Upright trunk. The pelvis is tucked (posterior tilt) so the lumbar
spine is flat. The front thigh is roughly horizontal; the rear thigh is vertical
(below the rear hip, with the shin up the prop).

**Visual appearance.** From the side, the body shows an upright trunk over a
front shin-vertical leg and a rear knee on the floor with the rear shin pinned up
the wall. From the front, the pelvis is level, the front knee tracks over the
front toe, and the shoulders are square.

---

## 4. Head

**Neck.** The cervical spine is a neutral continuation of the (flattened) thoracic
spine. No excessive flexion or extension.

**Gaze.** The eyes look forward, toward the horizon or slightly down. The gaze is
level, relaxed.

**Chin.** Neutral and slightly packed; ears roughly above the shoulders from the
side. The head does not jut forward.

**Cervical alignment.** The cervical curve is preserved but not exaggerated; one
gentle, unbroken line. No gooseneck, no turtle tuck.

---

## 5. Spine

**Lumbar.** Flattened by the deliberate posterior pelvic tilt. The natural
lumbar arch is reduced (not excessively flexed into a round back). The lumbar
spine is long, not arched (this is intentional for the stretch) and not rounded
into flexion.

**Thoracic.** Neutral-to-slightly-extended and tall. The upper back is flat and
broad; no rounding. The rib cage is stacked over the (tucked) pelvis with the
anterior core engaged.

**Rib cage.** Ribs down, anterior abdominal wall braced. Chest open, not flared.
The thoracic cage moves as a unit with the trunk.

**Pelvis.** Posterior tilt ("tucked under") — the hallmark of the couch stretch.
The anterior superior iliac spines rotate posteriorly and inferiorly. The pelvis
is level side-to-side and square (no rotation). The gluteals and abdominals
co-contract to hold the tuck.

**State summary.** Lumbar: flattened (by posterior tilt, not rounded). Thoracic:
neutral/tall. Pelvis: posteriorly tilted, level, square. The posterior tilt is
intentional and is the mechanism of the stretch.

---

## 6. Upper Body

**Shoulders (glenohumeral).** Level, square to the pelvis, relaxed away from the
ears. Arms may rest on the front thigh, be held at the chest, or reach overhead
(common in couch-stretch variations to further open the front of the body). The
humerus is unloaded and does not shrug.

**Scapula.** Flat on the rib cage; no winging or excessive elevation. If the arms
reach overhead, the scapulae elevate/upwardly rotate naturally but should not
wing.

**Clavicles.** Level and symmetric; do not ride up toward the neck.

**Elbows.** Free per the chosen arm style. No forced position required.

**Forearms / wrists / hands.** Relaxed, not clenched. No deviation implying
shoulder compensation.

**Note.** The upper body is postural; it must not collapse forward, twist, or
tilt the trunk. Overhead arms increase the anterior-chain stretch but do not
change the lower-body landmarks.

---

## 7. Lower Body

**Rear hip.** In extension (the rear thigh is below/behind the rear hip, with the
knee on the floor and the shin up the prop). The rear hip flexors are lengthened.
The posterior pelvic tilt increases this lengthening.

**Rear knee.** Flexed to ~90°+ (deep flexion) and resting on the floor. The rear
knee is the rear support point. It sits under or slightly behind the rear hip.

**Rear ankle.** Dorsiflexed with the foot up against the prop; the toes point
upward. The rear foot is elevated, not on the floor.

**Rear shin / prop contact.** The rear shin is flush against the wall/bench,
vertical. This pins the rear lower leg and traps the stretch.

**Rear foot.** Elevated against the prop (toes up); not bearing floor load.

**Front hip.** Flexed to ~90° so the front thigh is roughly horizontal. The front
hip is in a neutral-to-slightly-open (anterior) position, not the stretched side.

**Front knee.** Flexed to ~90° and tracking over the front foot/toe. No valgus,
no varus. Front shin near-vertical.

**Front ankle.** Dorsiflexed; the front foot flat with the heel planted.

**Front foot.** Flat on the floor — heel and toe both down — pointing forward (or
very slightly outward). Primary load-bearing contact (with the rear knee).

**Weight distribution.** Shared between the front foot (larger share) and the
rear knee/shin contact. The rear foot bears no floor load (it is up the prop).

---

## 8. Contacts

**Front foot–floor contact (×1, primary).**

- *Contact surface:* the whole sole — heel and toe/ball — flat on the floor.
- *Expected pressure:* the larger load share; heaviest under the heel/midfoot
  through the toe; knee tracks over this foot.
- *Stability:* fixed. The front foot does not slide or lift.
- *Visual appearance:* flat foot, heel down, toes forward, knee tracking over
  the toes.

**Rear knee–floor contact (×1, primary rear).**

- *Contact surface:* the rear knee (patellar region / prepatellar soft tissue)
  on the floor.
- *Expected pressure:* a meaningful rear share; stabilized by the shin against
  the prop.
- *Stability:* fixed. The rear knee does not slide.
- *Visual appearance:* rear knee on the floor under/behind the rear hip.

**Rear shin–prop contact (×1, stretch anchor).**

- *Contact surface:* the posterior surface of the rear shin/calf flush against
  the wall/bench; the rear foot elevated, toes up.
- *Expected pressure:* light contact pressure holding the shin vertical; the
  prop resists the shin from sliding down/forward.
- *Stability:* fixed by the prop. The rear shin does not slide down the prop.
- *Visual appearance:* rear shin vertical against the wall, rear foot up.

No hand contact with the floor in the canonical couch stretch (hands rest on
thigh or are overhead). Front-knee or torso contact with the floor is absent.

---

## 9. Range of Motion

**Expected ROM.**

- Rear hip: extended (thigh behind/below the pelvis) with the knee flexed to
  deep flexion; combined with posterior pelvic tilt, the iliopsoas and rectus
  femoris are near end-range lengthening.
- Rear knee: deep flexion (~90°+), knee on floor.
- Rear ankle: dorsiflexed, foot up (toes toward ceiling).
- Front hip: flexed ~90° (thigh horizontal).
- Front knee: flexed ~90°, tracking over the toe.
- Front ankle: dorsiflexed, heel down.
- Spine: lumbar flattened by posterior tilt; thoracic tall; little other change.

**Acceptable variation.**

- Front foot further or closer (alters front-hip angle; closer increases the
  rear-hip stretch intensity).
- Rear knee slightly behind the rear hip (common, increases stretch).
- Arms resting on thigh, at chest, or overhead.
- Slight individual variation in how flat the lumbar spine becomes.

**Unacceptable variation.**

- Rear foot sliding down the prop (shin no longer vertical) — loses the stretch
  trap.
- Anterior pelvic tilt / lumbar arch (defeats the couch-stretch intent; the
  pelvis must be tucked).
- Front knee valgus/varus or front heel lifting.
- Rear knee lifting off the floor.
- Pelvis drop, rotation, or side tilt.
- Torso collapsing forward or twisting.
- Weight shifting so the rear foot takes floor load (rear foot must stay up).
- Asymmetric shoulder tilt.

---

## 10. Muscle Intent

Structures that should *visually* appear lengthened / under stretch (rear side):

- **Iliopsoas (psoas major + iliacus)** — the primary hip-flexor stretch at the
  front of the rear hip; tension felt deep in the anterior hip.
- **Rectus femoris** — two-joint muscle; stretched at both the rear hip (extension)
  and rear knee (flexion with foot up); tension felt down the front of the rear
  thigh.
- **Sartorius / tensor fasciae latae** — secondary anterior-hip structures.

Muscles that should *visually* appear engaged (to hold the position):

- **Gluteus maximus (rear side)** — co-contracts to maintain posterior pelvic
  tilt and hip extension.
- **Rectus abdominis / obliques** — posterior pelvic tilt / abdominal bracing.
- **Quadriceps (front thigh)** — knee extension hold of the front leg.
- **Erector spinae** — upright thoracic posture.
- **Gluteus medius (both sides)** — pelvic leveling.

The body reads as "tucked pelvis, upright trunk, rear hip opened, front leg
stacked."

## 11. Visual Checkpoints

An auditor should be able to confirm each of the following from the rendered
pose:

1. ✓ Rear shin is vertical and flush against the prop (wall/bench).
2. ✓ Rear foot is elevated, toes pointing up, ankle dorsiflexed.
3. ✓ Rear knee is on the floor, under or slightly behind the rear hip.
4. ✓ Rear hip is in extension (thigh below/behind the pelvis).
5. ✓ Front foot is flat, heel down, pointing forward.
6. ✓ Front knee flexed ~90°, tracking over the front toe.
7. ✓ Front knee shows no valgus or varus.
8. ✓ Front shin near-vertical; front heel on the floor.
9. ✓ Front thigh roughly horizontal.
10. ✓ Pelvis is tucked under (posterior tilt) — lumbar spine flattened.
11. ✓ Lumbar spine is flat (not arched, not rounded into flexion).
12. ✓ Thoracic spine tall and broad — no rounding.
13. ✓ Rib cage stacked and braced; chest open.
14. ✓ Pelvis level side-to-side — no contralateral hip drop.
15. ✓ Pelvis square to the front — no rotation.
16. ✓ Neck neutral continuation of the spine; chin neutral and packed.
17. ✓ Gaze forward.
18. ✓ Shoulders level and square to the pelvis, not shrugged.
19. ✓ Scapulae flat — no winging.
20. ✓ No trunk twist; shoulders and hips face the same direction.
21. ✓ Front foot does not slide or lift.
22. ✓ Rear knee does not slide; rear shin does not slide down the prop.
23. ✓ Rear foot stays elevated (does not take floor load).
24. ✓ The stretch is visibly at the front of the rear hip / rear thigh (posterior
     tilt driving it).
25. ✓ Torso upright (or very slight lean), not collapsed forward.
26. ✓ Weight shared between front foot and rear knee/shin contact.
27. ✓ Arms positioned consistently (thigh / chest / overhead) without distorting
     the trunk.
28. ✓ Overall silhouette: upright trunk, front shin vertical, rear knee down with
     shin up the wall.
29. ✓ The held position is static (no bouncing).
30. ✓ The rendered pose matches the visual checkpoints in §11.

## 12. Common Mistakes

**Anterior pelvic tilt / arched lumbar ("butt out").** The athlete arches the
lower back and tilts the pelvis anteriorly. *Why wrong:* this is the opposite of
the couch-stretch intent and off-loads the hip flexors (the stretch is lost).
*Visual difference:* the lower back bows, the butt pokes back, the front of the
hip relaxes.

**Rear foot sliding down the prop.** The rear shin slips so the foot lowers.
*Why wrong:* releases the stretch trap; the rear knee straightens and the hip
flexors shorten. *Visual difference:* the rear shin is no longer vertical; the
rear foot drops toward the floor.

**Front heel lifting.** The front heel rises. *Why wrong:* reduces front-leg
stability and shifts balance. *Visual difference:* front foot rolls onto the toe;
front shin pitches forward.

**Front knee valgus / not tracking the toe.** The front knee caves inward.
*Why wrong:* medial knee stress. *Visual difference:* front knee inside the toe
line; ankle rolls in.

**Pelvis drop / rotate.** The trailing-side hip drops or the pelvis rotates.
*Why wrong:* poor pelvic control; the tuck is lost. *Visual difference:* hips not
level/square; one iliac crest lower or rotated forward.

**Rounded upper back / forward collapse.** The torso pitches forward. *Why
wrong:* reduces the anterior-chain stretch and loads the lumbar. *Visual
difference:* shoulders ahead of the hips; upper back rounds.

**Rear knee lifting off the floor.** The rear knee comes up. *Why wrong:* reduces
rear-hip extension and the stretch. *Visual difference:* the rear thigh leaves the
vertical; the body rises.

**Twisting torso / uneven shoulders.** Shoulders rotate or tilt. *Why wrong:*
poor trunk control. *Visual difference:* shoulders not square to the pelvis.

---

## 13. Acceptance Criteria

The couch stretch (held position) is **accepted only if every criterion is
satisfied**:

- [ ] Rear shin vertical and flush against the prop; rear foot elevated, toes up.
- [ ] Rear knee on the floor, under or slightly behind the rear hip.
- [ ] Rear hip in extension; rear hip flexors visibly lengthened.
- [ ] Front foot flat, heel down, forward, not sliding.
- [ ] Front knee flexed ~90°, tracking over the front toe, no valgus/varus.
- [ ] Front shin near-vertical; front heel on the floor.
- [ ] Front thigh roughly horizontal.
- [ ] Pelvis tucked under (posterior tilt); lumbar spine flattened (not arched).
- [ ] Thoracic spine tall; no rounding.
- [ ] Rib cage stacked and braced; chest open.
- [ ] Pelvis level and square; no drop or rotation.
- [ ] Neck continues the spinal line; chin neutral and packed.
- [ ] Shoulders level, square, not shrugged; scapulae flat.
- [ ] No trunk twist; shoulders and hips face the same direction.
- [ ] Gaze forward.
- [ ] Rear foot stays elevated (no floor load); rear shin does not slide down the
      prop.
- [ ] The stretch is driven by posterior pelvic tilt at the front of the rear hip
      / rear thigh.
- [ ] The rendered pose matches the visual checkpoints in §11.
