# Biomechanical Pose Specification — World's Greatest Stretch

> **Canonical reference.** This document defines what the "World's Greatest
> Stretch" should look like from a strict biomechanics, anatomy, and physical-
> therapy standpoint. It is implementation-independent and describes human
> movement only. It must remain valid even if the rendering system behind it is
> rewritten from scratch.
>
> Sources: sports biomechanics, physical-therapy literature (dynamic
> mobility / integrated stretching), strength & conditioning practice, and human
> anatomy. Where expert opinion differs, the consensus is stated and the
> alternatives are noted.

---

## 1. Exercise Overview

**Movement goal.** The World's Greatest Stretch is a dynamic, integrated mobility
sequence performed from a deep forward lunge. From the bottom of a forward lunge,
the lead (front) hand is planted on the floor inside the lead foot, the rear
(opposite) hand reaches up and overhead into thoracic rotation, and the trunk
rotates toward the lead side. The movement then returns and repeats on the other
side. It combines a hip-flexor stretch, hamstring length on the trailing leg,
thoracic rotation, and ankle mobility in one flow.

**Training goal.** Improve integrated mobility across multiple planes at once:
front-of-hip opening (rear hip flexors), posterior-chain length (trailing leg
hamstrings), thoracic spine rotation, and ankle dorsiflexion of the lead foot.
It is a dynamic mobility drill, not a strength movement.

**Intended muscles (lengthened / opened).**

- Rear (trailing) side: iliopsoas (hip extension), hamstrings (trailing leg
  extended), gastrocnemius/soleus (trailing ankle neutral/planted).
- Lead (front) side: anterior ankle structures (dorsiflexion), hip flexors of the
  lead side in a shorter position.
- Thoracic: rotators and extensors opened on the reaching side.

**Intended muscles (engaged / stabilizing).**

- Lead-leg quadriceps and glute (control the deep lunge).
- Trunk: obliques, erector spinae, rectus abdominis (rotation control and
  anti-flexion), gluteus medius (pelvic leveling).
- Shoulder girdle (reaching arm): deltoid, rotator cuff, scapular stabilizers
  (overhead reach and rotation).
- Lead-arm: shoulder girdle stabilizers (planted hand support).

**Common execution style.** From standing, the athlete steps into a deep forward
lunge. At the bottom, the lead hand is planted inside the lead foot; the rear hand
reaches up and across toward the ceiling, rotating the thorax open over the lead
thigh. The athlete holds the rotation briefly, returns the hand, and stands/
switches sides. Breathing: inhale into the rotation, exhale returning. Typically
performed for reps per side as a warm-up flow.

## 2. Pose Purpose

This specification treats the **rotated bottom position** (the key audited pose
of the sequence) as the canonical pose. The sequence also has a deep-lunge entry
and a return, but the rotated hold is the defining posture.

**Deep lunge entry.** As in a forward lunge bottom: lead foot planted, lead hip
and knee flexed (thigh near horizontal), rear leg extended behind with the rear
heel lifted and rear knee lowered toward the floor; torso upright.

**Rotated bottom position (canonical pose).** From the deep lunge, the lead hand
is planted on the floor just inside the lead foot (fingers forward, supporting
the trunk). The trunk rotates toward the lead side: the rear (opposite) hand
reaches up and overhead, the thorax opens, and the gaze follows the reaching hand.
The lead elbow may brush the inside of the lead knee or the lead foot stays planted
with the knee tracking the toe. The rear leg remains extended behind. Weight is on
the lead foot and the planted lead hand.

**Return / switch.** The reaching hand returns to the floor, the trunk de-rotates,
and the athlete drives up and steps through to the other side, repeating the
pattern with the opposite leg leading.

**Thoracic rotation emphasis.** The defining feature is the open thoracic rotation
driven by the reaching arm; the lumbar spine stays relatively stable (rotation is
primarily thoracic, not lumbar).

---

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment

**Center of mass.** The whole-body COM sits over the lead foot and the planted
lead hand, which together form the support base in the rotated bottom position.
It is lowered (deep lunge) and shifted toward the lead side during the rotation.
It must remain within the support polygon of lead foot + lead hand.

**Balance.** Load is shared between the lead foot (primary) and the planted lead
hand (support). The rear toe provides a small rear balance anchor. Balanced in the
sagittal/frontal plane; the rotation is about the vertical axis through the lead
side.

**Symmetry.** Not mirror-symmetric during the rotated hold (one hand down, one
hand up, trunk rotated). The pelvis is level side-to-side and square-ish to the
lead-leg direction (not rotated excessively). The rotation is in the thoracic
spine, not the pelvis.

**Posture.** Deep lunge base: lead thigh near horizontal, rear leg extended. The
trunk is rotated open over the lead thigh, the reaching arm overhead. The neck
continues the rotated thoracic line (gaze up at the reaching hand).

**Visual appearance.** From the side, a deep lunge with one hand on the floor
inside the lead foot and the opposite arm reaching overhead; the thorax is opened.
From the front, the lead knee tracks the toe, the pelvis is level, and one arm is
down (planted) while the other reaches up.

---

## 4. Head

**Neck.** The cervical spine continues the rotated thoracic line. As the thorax
rotates and the gaze follows the reaching hand upward, the neck extends/turns
gently to look up — this is the intended, controlled position, not a strain.

**Gaze.** The eyes follow the reaching (rear) hand up toward the ceiling/overhead.
The gaze is upward and toward the reaching side, relaxed.

**Chin.** Aligned with the rotated head; not jutting forward or tucked to the
chest. The head is the top of the rotated thoracic chain.

**Cervical alignment.** The cervical curve follows the thoracic rotation; no
forced lateral side-bend or excessive extension beyond following the reaching arm.
No "crane" strain.

---

## 5. Spine

**Lumbar.** Relatively stable and neutral. The rotation is driven primarily by
the thoracic spine; the lumbar spine contributes little rotation (this is
anatomically correct — lumbar rotation is limited). The lumbar does not round or
excessively extend. It stays long as the base of the rotated trunk.

**Thoracic.** The primary rotation segment. The thorax rotates open toward the
lead side as the rear hand reaches overhead; the chest opens. The upper back stays
long and extended (no rounding) through the rotation. This thoracic rotation is
the defining mobility target.

**Rib cage.** The rib cage rotates with the thorax; the anterior core on the
rotating side lengthens while the opposite side braces. The chest stays open, not
collapsed.

**Pelvis.** Level side-to-side (no contralateral hip drop) and relatively square
to the lead-leg direction (the rotation is above the pelvis, in the thorax). Not
in excessive anterior or posterior tilt. The gluteals and core stabilize the
pelvis so the lumbar stays quiet.

**State summary.** Lumbar: stable, neutral (minimal rotation). Thoracic: rotated
open toward the lead side, extended, no rounding. Pelvis: level, roughly square,
stable. The rotation is thoracic, not lumbar or pelvic.

---

## 6. Upper Body

**Shoulders (glenohumeral).** Two distinct positions:

- *Planted (lead) arm:* the shoulder is in weight-bearing support; the humerus is
  vertical under the torso, the shoulder stable and depressed (not shrugged),
  supporting part of the trunk load.
- *Reaching (rear) arm:* the shoulder is flexed and rotated so the arm reaches up
  and overhead; the humerus is the top of the rotated chain. The shoulder stays
  centrated (no anterior glide).

**Scapula.** *Planted arm:* the scapula is stable on the rib cage, depressed,
supporting. *Reaching arm:* the scapula upwardly rotates and elevates naturally
with the overhead reach; it should not wing excessively.

**Clavicles.** The reaching-side clavicle is higher (natural with overhead reach);
the planted-side clavicle is stable. Both move as part of the thoracic rotation.

**Elbows.** *Planted arm:* extended (straight) to support the trunk. *Reaching
arm:* extended (straight) overhead, or very slightly soft; not locked harshly.

**Forearms / wrists / hands.** *Planted hand:* flat on the floor, fingers
forward, bearing support load (wrist in neutral extension). *Reaching hand:* open
or relaxed, reaching toward the ceiling.

**Note.** The upper body drives the thoracic rotation (reaching arm) and supports
the trunk (planted arm). It must not shrug the planted shoulder or collapse the
reaching shoulder.

---

## 7. Lower Body

**Lead (front) hip.** Flexed so the lead thigh approaches horizontal at depth
(the deep-lunge base). The lead hip is the loaded joint of the lunge.

**Lead knee.** Flexed and tracking over the lead foot/toe. No valgus, no varus.
Lead shin near-vertical; lead heel down (planted). The lead foot is the primary
support.

**Lead ankle.** Dorsiflexed; the lead foot flat with the heel planted. The ankle
mobility is part of the stretch.

**Lead foot.** Flat on the floor — heel and toe both down — pointing forward (or
slightly outward). Primary load-bearing contact and the rotation axis base.

**Rear (trailing) hip.** Extended (rear thigh behind the pelvis, relative
hyperextension); the rear hip flexors are opened.

**Rear knee.** Flexed and lowered toward the floor (hovers just above in the
standard deep lunge); does not contact the floor.

**Rear ankle.** Plantarflexed so the rear heel lifts; the rear foot rests on the
toe/ball, pointing back. Secondary balance contact.

**Rear foot.** Rests on the toe/ball; heel raised; points straight back. Secondary
anchor.

**Weight distribution.** Predominantly on the lead foot, with the planted lead
hand bearing trunk support; the rear toe provides balance only.

---

## 8. Contacts

**Lead foot–floor contact (×1, primary).**

- *Contact surface:* the whole sole — heel and toe/ball — flat on the floor.
- *Expected pressure:* the primary load; heaviest under the heel/midfoot through
  the toe; knee tracks over this foot.
- *Stability:* fixed. The lead foot does not slide or lift; it is the drive and
  rotation base.
- *Visual appearance:* flat foot, heel down, toes forward, knee tracking over the
  toes.

**Lead hand–floor contact (×1, support).**

- *Contact surface:* the palm flat on the floor, fingers forward, inside the lead
  foot.
- *Expected pressure:* trunk-support load; distributed across the palm; symmetric
  within the hand.
- *Stability:* fixed. The planted hand does not slide or lift during the rotation
  hold.
- *Visual appearance:* flat palm, fingers forward, arm vertical under the torso,
  supporting the trunk.

**Rear foot–floor contact (×1, secondary).**

- *Contact surface:* the toe/ball region (and, per mobility, the dorsal top) of
  the foot on the floor; the heel is raised.
- *Expected pressure:* light, balance-only.
- *Stability:* fixed as a rear anchor; the rear foot does not slide; heel
  intentionally lifted.
- *Visual appearance:* rear heel up, rear foot pointing back, rear knee hovering
  near the floor.

No rear-knee or torso contact with the floor. The reaching hand does not contact
the floor (it is overhead).

---

## 9. Range of Motion

**Expected ROM.**

- Lead knee: flexed ~90° (thigh horizontal) at depth. Lead ankle dorsiflexed, heel
  down.
- Rear hip: extended/hyperextended; rear knee flexed and lowered; rear ankle
  plantarflexed (heel lift).
- Thoracic spine: rotated open toward the lead side (the defining ROM); lumbar
  rotation minimal.
- Reaching shoulder: flexed and rotated overhead; scapula upwardly rotated.
- Planted shoulder: weight-bearing depression/support.
- Spine/trunk: the trunk moves from upright (deep lunge) to rotated-open (canonical
  pose).

**Acceptable variation.**

- Depth from thigh-parallel to slightly above/below parallel.
- Thoracic rotation angle per individual mobility (clear rotation open is the
  target).
- Lead foot turned out up to ~15°.
- Brief hold at the top of the rotation.
- Hand placement just inside the lead foot (close) to slightly wider.

**Unacceptable variation.**

- Lead knee valgus/varus or lead heel lifting.
- Rear knee touching the floor (standard) or rear foot sliding.
- Lumbar rotation substituting for thoracic rotation (pelvis/lower back twisting
  instead of the chest opening).
- Rounded upper back during the rotation (chest collapsed).
- Planted shoulder shrugged toward the ear or planted hand sliding/lifting.
- Pelvis drop, excessive rotation, or tilt.
- Torso collapsing forward (losing the lunge depth) or the reaching arm
  collapsing.
- Asymmetric loss of the lunge base (lead thigh rising off horizontal
  excessively).

## 10. Muscle Intent

Structures that should *visually* appear lengthened / opened:

- **Iliopsoas (rear side)** — opened as the rear hip extends.
- **Hamstrings (rear side)** — lengthened as the rear leg stays extended.
- **Thoracic rotators / extensors (reaching side)** — opened by the overhead
  rotation.
- **Ankle dorsiflexors (lead side)** — lengthened as the lead ankle dorsiflexes.
- **Anterior core (rotating side)** — lengthened as the thorax opens.

Structures that should *visually* appear engaged (to hold the position):

- **Quadriceps / glute (lead leg)** — control the deep lunge.
- **Obliques / erector spinae** — thoracic rotation control and trunk stability.
- **Gluteus medius (both sides)** — pelvic leveling.
- **Deltoid / rotator cuff / scapular stabilizers (reaching arm)** — overhead
  reach and rotation.
- **Shoulder girdle stabilizers (planted arm)** — trunk support.
- **Triceps (both arms)** — planted arm extension / reaching arm extension.

The body reads as "deep lunge base, one hand planted, opposite arm reaching
overhead, thorax rotated open."

## 11. Visual Checkpoints

An auditor should be able to confirm each of the following from the rendered
rotated bottom pose:

1. ✓ Lead foot flat, heel down, pointing forward (≤15° out).
2. ✓ Lead knee flexed ~90° (thigh near horizontal) at depth.
3. ✓ Lead knee tracks over the lead foot, aligned with the second/third toe.
4. ✓ Lead knee shows no valgus or varus.
5. ✓ Lead shin near-vertical; lead heel on the floor.
6. ✓ Lead hip flexed consistent with the knee angle.
7. ✓ Rear leg extended behind; rear hip in extension.
8. ✓ Rear knee flexed and lowered near (not touching) the floor.
9. ✓ Rear heel lifted; rear foot on the toe/ball, pointing back.
10. ✓ Lead hand planted flat on the floor inside the lead foot, fingers forward.
11. ✓ Planted arm vertical/stable under the torso, supporting the trunk.
12. ✓ Rear (reaching) hand reaches up and overhead toward the ceiling.
13. ✓ Thorax is rotated open toward the lead side (chest opens).
14. ✓ Lumbar spine stays relatively stable (rotation is thoracic, not lumbar).
15. ✓ Thoracic spine long/extended through the rotation — no rounding.
16. ✓ Pelvis level side-to-side — no contralateral hip drop.
17. ✓ Pelvis roughly square to the lead-leg direction — no excessive rotation.
18. ✓ Neck continues the rotated thoracic line; gaze follows the reaching hand.
19. ✓ Gaze is up toward the reaching hand (not down or strained).
20. ✓ Planted shoulder depressed, not shrugged; planted hand not sliding.
21. ✓ Reaching shoulder centrated; scapula not excessively winging.
22. ✓ Rib cage rotates with the thorax; chest open, not collapsed.
23. ✓ Lead foot does not slide or lift.
24. ✓ Rear foot does not slide (heel intentionally raised).
25. ✓ No rear-knee floor contact in the standard position.
26. ✓ Weight shared between lead foot and planted lead hand; rear toe balances.
27. ✓ The deep-lunge base is preserved (lead thigh near horizontal) during the

28. ✓ The rotation is clearly thoracic (upper back opens), not a pelvic twist.

     overhead, thorax open.
30. ✓ The rendered pose matches the visual checkpoints in §11.

## 12. Common Mistakes

**Lumbar twist instead of thoracic rotation.** The athlete rotates from the lower
back/pelvis rather than opening the chest. *Why wrong:* the mobility target (thoracic
rotation) is missed and the lumbar is stressed. *Visual difference:* the pelvis and
lower back rotate while the chest stays square; the upper back does not open.

**Lead knee valgus (caving inward).** The lead knee drops toward the midline. *Why
wrong:* medial knee stress; weak glute stabilization. *Visual difference:* lead
knee inside the toe line; ankle rolls in.

**Lead heel lifting.** The lead heel rises. *Why wrong:* reduces stability and
indicates poor ankle dorsiflexion. *Visual difference:* lead foot rolls onto the
toe; shin pitches forward.

**Rounded upper back during rotation.** The chest collapses as the hand reaches.
*Why wrong:* loses the thoracic-extension component. *Visual difference:* the upper
back rounds; the reaching arm is forward rather than overhead.

**Planted shoulder shrugged.** The planted shoulder rides toward the ear. *Why
wrong:* poor shoulder stability and neck strain. *Visual difference:* the planted
clavicle elevates; shortened neck on that side.

**Pelvis drop / excessive rotation.** The trailing-side hip drops or the pelvis
rotates too far. *Why wrong:* poor pelvic control; the lumbar stays quiet in the
correct version. *Visual difference:* hips not level; pelvis rotated under a square
chest.

**Rear knee to floor / rear foot sliding.** The rear knee touches or the rear foot
drifts. *Why wrong:* breaks the lunge base and balance anchor. *Visual difference:*
rear knee contacts floor; rear footprint shifts.

**Torso collapse forward (losing lunge depth).** The trunk pitches forward and the
lead thigh rises. *Why wrong:* reduces the hip and ankle stretch. *Visual
difference:* shoulders well ahead of the lead foot; lead thigh above horizontal.

**Planted hand sliding / lifting.** The support hand moves. *Why wrong:* the
planted hand is a fixed support. *Visual difference:* the hand position relative to
the lead foot changes.

## 13. Acceptance Criteria

The World's Greatest Stretch (rotated bottom position) is **accepted only if every
criterion is satisfied**:

- [ ] Lead foot flat, heel down, forward (≤15° out), not sliding.
- [ ] Lead knee flexed ~90° (thigh near horizontal) at depth.
- [ ] Lead knee tracks over the lead foot, aligned with the second/third toe.
- [ ] No lead-knee valgus or varus.
- [ ] Lead shin near-vertical; lead heel stays on the floor.
- [ ] Rear leg extended behind; rear knee lowered near (not touching) the floor.
- [ ] Rear heel lifted; rear foot on toe/ball, pointing back, not sliding.
- [ ] Lead hand planted flat on the floor inside the lead foot, fingers forward.
- [ ] Planted arm stable under the torso, supporting; shoulder not shrugged.
- [ ] Rear hand reaches up and overhead; thorax rotated open toward the lead side.
- [ ] Lumbar spine relatively stable; rotation is thoracic, not lumbar/pelvic.
- [ ] Thoracic spine long/extended through rotation; no rounding.
- [ ] Pelvis level side-to-side; roughly square to the lead-leg direction.
- [ ] Neck continues the rotated thoracic line; gaze follows the reaching hand.
- [ ] Rib cage rotates with the thorax; chest open.
- [ ] Weight shared between lead foot and planted lead hand; rear toe balances.
- [ ] The deep-lunge base is preserved during the rotation.
- [ ] The rendered pose matches the visual checkpoints in §11.
