# Dead Bug (Standard) — Biomechanical Pose Specification

## 1. Exercise Overview
The Dead Bug is a supine (on-the-back) core exercise emphasizing lumbar neutrality under contralateral limb loading. The individual lies on the back with arms reaching toward the ceiling and knees bent to 90° directly over the hips (shins parallel to the floor). From this position, one arm is lowered overhead toward the floor while the opposite leg is lowered toward the floor — the low back must stay pressed flat against the ground throughout. It trains the anterior and deep core (rectus abdominis, transversus abdominis, obliques) in an anti-extension pattern, with contralateral coordination.

Populations: beginners through advanced; core conditioning and low-back rehabilitation. No equipment required.

## 2. Pose Purpose
- Train lumbar spine anti-extension: keep the low back pinned to the floor while the limbs move away from the body.
- Strengthen the deep core (transversus abdominis, internal obliques) and rectus abdominis as trunk stabilizers.
- Develop contralateral (opposite arm/leg) coordination with a braced trunk.
- Teach pelvic neutrality (neutral tilt, no anterior tilt) under limb-loading.

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment
- Supine on the floor: entire back and pelvis in contact with the ground at the start.
- Start posture: arms vertical toward the ceiling (shoulders at 90° flexion), knees bent 90° over the hips, shins parallel to the floor, feet relaxed.
- During the move, one arm reaches overhead toward the floor (behind the head) while the opposite leg extends and lowers toward the floor — the two move contralaterally.
- The trunk stays long and still; the pelvis does not tilt and the low back does not arch away from the floor.
- The non-moving arm stays vertical and the non-moving shin stays parallel to the floor (the "dead bug" tabletop is preserved on the stationary side).

## 4. Head
- Head rests on the floor; it does not lift (no cervical flexion crunch) and does not press aggressively into the ground.
- The cervical spine stays in its neutral slight curve; the chin is neither jutted nor tucked sharply.
- Gaze is toward the ceiling (or softly forward); the neck is relaxed and aligned with the trunk.

## 5. Spine
- Lumbar spine stays in neutral contact with the floor: the low back is pressed flat (or maintains its natural curve gently loaded) — no arching (anterior tilt) away from the ground.
- The thoracic spine rests on the floor; no rounding up.
- No rotation of the trunk and no lateral flexion — the pelvis and shoulders stay square.
- The pelvis is in neutral tilt: the ASIS and pubic symphysis stay level; the pelvis does not posteriorly tilt (tuck) nor anteriorly tilt (arch).
- The defining test: a hand could slide flat under the low back only with minimal space — the back does not bridge away from the floor.

## 6. Upper Body
- Stationary arms: vertical toward the ceiling, shoulders at ~90° flexion, scapulae resting on the floor (not shrugged).
- Moving arm: lowered overhead toward the floor with the elbow extended and the shoulder in extension; the arm stays close to the floor line without the low back arching.
- The shoulder girdle stays down (no elevation); the moving scapula stays in contact with the floor as the arm reaches back.
- Both shoulders remain level; no rotation of the chest.

## 7. Lower Body
- Stationary leg: hip and knee at 90°, shin parallel to the floor, foot relaxed — a stable "table" leg.
- Moving leg: hip extends and the knee extends partially as the leg lowers toward the floor; the heel approaches the ground without the low back lifting.
- The moving leg reaches from the hip; the thigh and shin lengthen toward the floor in a controlled contralateral pattern.
- The supporting (stationary) hip/knee stays at 90°; the pelvis does not tilt toward the lowering leg.
- The feet are relaxed; no clenching.

## 8. Contacts
- The entire posterior surface of the trunk is in contact with the floor: occipital region of the head, upper back/thoracic spine, lumbar spine, sacrum, and pelvis all rest on the ground.
- The stationary hand (if it drifts) and the stationary foot may also brush the floor, but the key contacts are the back and pelvis.
- During the limb-lowering, the moving hand and moving foot approach the floor but need not touch; the trunk contacts are never broken.
- The floor is the support for the whole back — this is a supine (posterior-contact) exercise.

## 9. Range of Motion
- Shoulder: from 90° flexion (vertical) to ~150–180° (arm overhead toward floor) on the moving side.
- Hip: from 90° flexion (tabletop) toward ~30–45° flexion as the leg lowers; knee extends from 90° toward ~150–180° (nearly straight) on the moving side.
- The moving limbs lower only as far as the low back can stay flat — the limiting factor is lumbar neutrality, not hamstring length.
- Lumbar/pelvic ROM: zero — the spine and pelvis do not move; the exercise is anti-extension (isometric trunk).
- Neck: neutral, no motion.

## 10. Muscle Intent
- Transversus abdominis and internal obliques: deep bracing drawing the navel toward the spine, pinning the low back.
- Rectus abdominis: anterior trunk stabilization against limb pull.
- Pelvic floor and deep hip flexors: assist neutral pelvic control.
- The moving hip flexors (iliopsoas) lengthen as the leg lowers; the moving shoulder flexors/extensors control the arm reach.
- Erector spinae: isometric, keeping the back grounded.
- Gluteus and hamstrings (stationary leg): maintain the 90° tabletop.

## 11. Visual Checkpoints
- [ ] Supine, entire back and pelvis on the floor.
- [ ] Arms vertical toward ceiling at start (shoulders ~90°).
- [ ] Knees bent 90° over hips, shins parallel to floor.
- [ ] Low back pressed to the floor (no arch).
- [ ] Pelvis neutral (no tilt) at start and during movement.
- [ ] Move is contralateral: right arm + left leg, then left arm + right leg.
- [ ] Moving arm lowers overhead toward the floor, straight.
- [ ] Moving leg lowers toward the floor, extending from the hip.
- [ ] Stationary arm stays vertical.
- [ ] Stationary shin stays parallel to the floor.
- [ ] Low back remains flat as limbs lower.
- [ ] Shoulders stay down (no shrug).
- [ ] No rotation or side-bend of the trunk.
- [ ] Head stays on the floor, neck neutral.
- [ ] Breathing steady (no breath-hold).
- [ ] Movement stops before the low back arches.
- [ ] Limbs return to start under control.
- [ ] Both sides worked equally.
- [ ] Feet relaxed, not clenched.
- [ ] The "tabletop" (90/90) is restored between reps.

## 12. Common Mistakes
- Arching the low back off the floor as the leg/arm lower (losing lumbar neutrality) — the most common fault.
- Anteriorly tilting the pelvis (arching) instead of keeping it level.
- Lifting the head or crunching the neck.
- Letting the stationary shin drop out of the 90° tabletop.
- Lowering the limbs too far (prioritizing range over trunk stiffness).
- Rotating or side-bending the trunk toward the moving limbs.
- Shrugging the shoulders toward the ears.
- Holding the breath instead of breathing.
- Using momentum to swing the limbs rather than controlled lowering.

## 13. Acceptance Criteria
- [ ] Supine with the entire back and pelvis in contact with the floor.
- [ ] Start: arms vertical, knees bent 90° over hips, shins parallel to floor.
- [ ] Contralateral limb lowering (opposite arm overhead + opposite leg toward floor) with the stationary side holding tabletop.
- [ ] Lumbar spine and pelvis remain neutral and flat to the floor throughout (no arch, no tilt, no rotation).
- [ ] Head and neck neutral; shoulders down (not shrugged).
- [ ] Movement is controlled and limited by lumbar neutrality, with steady respiration, for the prescribed reps.
