# Thoracic Extension (Reps)

## 1. Exercise Overview
Thoracic Extension is a mobility drill that targets the thoracic spine (the upper and middle region of the vertebral column, roughly T1–T12). The goal is to improve the ability to extend (arching backward) the upper back, an action that is frequently limited in modern populations due to prolonged sitting and forward-head postures. The drill is commonly performed either prone (lying face down) over a supportive prop such as a foam roller or a rolled towel placed under the upper back, or in a seated position. The canonical repeated sub-pose is the extended (arched) upper-back position, held briefly and repeated for reps. It is a low-load, controlled mobility exercise rather than a strength or loaded movement.

## 2. Pose Purpose
- Restore and maintain thoracic spine extension range of motion.
- Counteract the flexed (rounded) upper-back posture associated with desk work and device use.
- Improve chest (pectoral) openness and overhead-reaching mechanics by mobilizing the trunk.
- Teach segmentation of the thoracic spine from the lumbar (lower) spine so the low back remains neutral.
- Provide gentle, supported end-range loading to encourage tissue adaptation in extension.

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment
- Body orientation: prone (face down) over a prop, or seated upright.
- The thorax is the mobile segment; the lumbar spine and pelvis are kept quiet and stable.
- Rib cage lifts (sternum rises) without forcing the low back into excessive arching.
- Breathing is slow through the movement; extension is paired with an exhalation to ease the rib cage.
- The trunk as a whole should look "segmented": upper back moves, lower back stays still.

## 4. Head
- Head is supported and in line with the upper thoracic curve — not cranked back.
- In the prone-over-prop version, the head may rest gently back or be held neutral looking at the floor; avoid jutting the chin.
- The cervical spine follows the thoracic extension softly; the neck is not the prime mover.
- Keep the chin slightly tucked (neutral) so the extension comes from the mid-back, not the neck.

## 5. Spine
- **Thoracic (T1–T12):** actively extended (arched open) around the prop or actively lifted in seated. This is the primary segment of motion.
- **Lumbar (L1–L5):** remains in a neutral, lightly braced position. It must NOT compensate by overarching; the lumbar does not significantly extend during the drill.
- **Cervical:** neutral-to-slightly extended, following the thorax, not leading it.
- The transition between thoracic extension and lumbar neutral is the key control point — the motion stops at the thoracolumbar junction.

## 6. Upper Body
- Shoulders (glenohumeral joints) are relaxed and drawn down away from the ears (scapular depression).
- Arms may rest at the sides, be extended overhead, or be clasped behind the head with elbows wide — the choice modulates intensity.
- Scapulae are not forcibly pinched; they move freely as the chest opens.
- In the seated version the hands may rest on the thighs; in the prone version the arms reach overhead or outward to encourage the chest to lift.

## 7. Lower Body
- Pelvis stays neutral and grounded (prone) or seated evenly on the surface.
- Hips and knees are comfortable: in prone, legs may be extended with toes pointed or knees bent with feet flat; in seated, feet flat on floor hip-width apart.
- The pelvis should not rotate or tilt to "help" the thoracic movement — any pelvic motion indicates the low back is compensating.

## 8. Contacts
- Prone-over-prop: posterior thorax contacts the prop (roller/towel); pelvis and legs contact the floor; head may contact the floor or be supported.
- Seated: the ischial tuberosities (sit bones) bear weight through the pelvis; feet contact the floor.
- No hands required on the floor unless used for support; the movement is passive-to-active around the prop.

## 9. Range of Motion
- Thoracic extension: from neutral to mild-to-moderate end-range extension, limited by comfort at the sternum/rib cage.
- Lumbar extension: minimal — should remain near neutral (≈0° added extension beyond resting lordosis).
- Cervical: small, following the thorax.
- Reps are typically 5–15 holds of 1–3 seconds; progress by moving the prop up/down the thorax, not by forcing more arch.

## 10. Muscle Intent
- Erector spinae (thoracic portion) and multifidus: eccentrically control and isometrically hold the extended position.
- Latissimus dorsi and pectorals: gently lengthen as the chest opens.
- Deep neck flexors: maintain neutral cervical alignment.
- Abdominal obliques: lightly braced to keep the lumbar spine from overarching.
- Scapular depressors (lower trapezius, serratus anterior): keep shoulders down and wide.

## 11. Visual Checkpoints
- [ ] Upper back is visibly arched (chest rising) around/over the prop or lifted in seated.
- [ ] Low back shows no increase in arch compared to rest (lumbar neutral).
- [ ] Shoulders are down and away from the ears, not shrugged.
- [ ] Head is neutral, not cranked backward.
- [ ] Chin is slightly tucked, not jutting forward.
- [ ] Pelvis is level and still, not rotating or tilting.
- [ ] Rib cage lifts (sternum rises) rather than the belly pushing out.
- [ ] Breathing is smooth, extension paired with exhale.
- [ ] Arms (if overhead) are relaxed, not tense or gripping.
- [ ] Neck is soft, not the prime mover.
- [ ] The motion clearly originates at the mid-back, not the neck or low back.
- [ ] Prop (if used) sits at the thoracic region, not the low back or neck.
- [ ] Hips/knees are comfortable and unchanging through the rep.
- [ ] Each rep returns to a calm neutral before extending again.
- [ ] No discomfort or pinching in the low back.
- [ ] Movement is slow and controlled, not ballistic.

## 12. Common Mistakes
- Overarching the lumbar spine to fake thoracic extension ("arching the low back instead of the mid-back").
- Cranking the neck/chin back to create the illusion of extension.
- Placing the prop at the low back or neck rather than the thoracic spine.
- Shrugging the shoulders up toward the ears.
- Holding the breath instead of exhaling into the extension.
- Using momentum or bouncing at end range.
- Letting the pelvis rotate/tilt to assist the movement.

## 13. Acceptance Criteria
- [ ] Thoracic spine is in visible extension while lumbar spine remains neutral.
- [ ] Head and cervical spine are in neutral alignment, not leading the motion.
- [ ] Shoulders remain depressed and wide, not elevated.
- [ ] Pelvis is stable and level throughout the repetition.
- [ ] The drill is performed slowly with controlled breathing and no low-back discomfort.
- [ ] Each repetition isolates the upper-back region as the prime mover.
