# Biomechanical Pose Specification — Step-Up (Standard)

## 1. Exercise overview
The standard step-up is a unilateral (single-limb) closed-chain lower-body movement in which one foot
is placed on a raised box or step and the body is driven upward until the lead leg is fully extended
and the trailing foot leaves the floor. It trains hip/knee/ankle extension of the lead limb under
load, balance on a raised surface, and contralateral limb control. The canonical pose covers both the
lead-leg driving push and the upright top position standing on the box.

## 2. Pose purpose
To build unilateral lower-body strength and balance, emphasizing the lead-leg gluteus maximus,
quadriceps, and calf, and to develop the coordination of driving through the heel. It improves
single-leg hip extension, knee stability, and the ability to control ascent onto and descent from an
elevated surface.

## 3. Whole-body alignment
- Facing the box; one foot planted on the box top, the other on the floor.
- The lead leg does the work; the torso stays upright and stacked over the lead foot.
- At the top, the body stands upright on the box with the lead knee extended and the trailing foot
  lifted or following.
- Center of mass shifts onto the lead foot as the body rises; balance is maintained over the box.
- Symmetry is trained by alternating lead limbs across repetitions (each pose instance shows one
  lead side).

## 4. Head
- Cervical spine neutral, gaze forward.
- Head stays stacked on the upright trunk; no forward jut during the drive.

## 5. Spine
- Neutral, braced lumbar and thoracic posture; natural curves maintained.
- The trunk stays upright (minimal lean) to keep the load over the lead foot; lean originates at the
  hip if any, not from spinal flexion.
- Abdominal and paraspinal muscles brace for balance on the elevated surface.

## 6. Upper body
- Arms hang naturally or swing for balance/assistance; they do not bear load against the box.
- Shoulders down and back; chest open and lifted.
- The upper body remains stable and upright throughout the step.

## 7. Lower body
- Setup: lead foot flat on the box, knee bent; trailing foot on the floor.
- Drive (concentric): the lead leg extends — hip extension, knee extension, and ankle plantarflexion
  through the heel — lifting the body onto the box.
- Knee of the lead leg tracks over the lead toes, no valgus; the femur stays aligned.
- At the top: lead hip, knee, and ankle extended (or near); the trailing foot is lifted off the floor
  (or steps up to meet the box, depending on the variant).
- Top position: upright stance on the box, pelvis level, both hips even.
- Descent (eccentric): reverse the motion, controlling the trailing foot back to the floor via lead-
  leg eccentric control; knee tracks over toes.

## 8. Contacts
- Lead foot: flat on the box top, driving through the heel; this is the primary support during the
  drive and the top hold.
- Trailing foot: on the floor at the start; lifts off as the body rises; returns to the floor on the
  way down.
- No hand contact with the box or floor for support (balance is unaided).
- Base of support transfers from the floor foot to the box foot during the movement.

## 9. Range of Motion
- Lead hip: from flexed (~70–90° at setup) to ~0° (extended at top).
- Lead knee: from flexed (~70–90°) to ~0–10° (near full extension) at the top.
- Lead ankle: from dorsiflexion toward plantarflexion as the heel drives.
- Trailing hip/knee/ankle: flex to lift the foot clear of the floor at the top.
- Trunk: upright, minimal lean (~0–15° from vertical).
- Box height typically places the lead thigh at or above parallel when the foot is placed.

## 10. Muscle intent
- Lead-leg primary movers: gluteus maximus and hamstrings (hip extension); quadriceps (knee
  extension); gastrocnemius/soleus (ankle plantarflexion through the heel).
- Lead-leg stabilizers: gluteus medius/minimus (pelvic level, knee tracking); quadriceps (knee
  control).
- Core/trunk: erector spinae, abdominals (upright bracing and balance).
- Trailing limb: hip flexors and knee flexors lift the foot; it is largely unloaded at the top.

## 11. Visual checkpoints
- [ ] One foot flat on the box, the other on the floor.
- [ ] Drive initiates through the lead heel, not the toe.
- [ ] Lead knee tracks over the lead toes, no valgus.
- [ ] Torso stays upright over the lead foot.
- [ ] Lead hip extends fully, lifting the body onto the box.
- [ ] Trailing foot lifts clear of the floor at the top.
- [ ] Top position: upright stance, pelvis level, both hips even.
- [ ] Chest open, spine braced and neutral.
- [ ] Head neutral, gaze forward.
- [ ] Shoulders relaxed, not shrugged.
- [ ] Arms used only for balance, not support.
- [ ] Weight biased onto the lead (box) foot.
- [ ] Descent is controlled by the lead leg, not a drop.
- [ ] Knee stays tracked over the toe on the way down.
- [ ] No excessive forward trunk fold.
- [ ] Balance maintained without hand contact.
- [ ] Smooth, controlled drive and step-down.
- [ ] Symmetry achieved by alternating lead sides across reps.
- [ ] Box height appropriate (lead thigh ~parallel or above when foot placed).
- [ ] No hip drop on the trailing side at the top.
- [ ] Full lead-leg extension at the top hold.

## 12. Common mistakes
- Pushing off the trailing (floor) foot instead of driving with the lead heel — reduces lead-leg
  loading.
- Leading with the toe / heel lifting on the box, shifting load forward.
- Knee caving inward (valgus) on the lead leg.
- Excessive forward trunk lean, losing the upright posture and balance.
- Using the hands on the box or a rail for support.
- Letting the trailing hip drop (pelvic tilt) at the top.
- Dropping (not controlling) back down instead of eccentric lead-leg control.
- Box too high, forcing compensatory lean or knee stress.

## 13. Acceptance criteria
- [ ] Lead foot flat on the box; drive through the heel.
- [ ] Lead knee tracks over the toe with no valgus.
- [ ] Torso upright, spine braced and neutral.
- [ ] Body reaches a balanced top stance on the box, trailing foot lifted.
- [ ] Pelvis level, both hips even at the top.
- [ ] Descent controlled by the lead leg; no hand support.
- [ ] Movement unilateral, balanced, and symmetric across alternating reps.
