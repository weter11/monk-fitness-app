# Biomechanical Pose Specification — Cossack Squat

> **Canonical reference.** This document defines what a Cossack squat should look
> like from a strict biomechanics, anatomy, and strength-and-conditioning standpoint.
> It is implementation-independent and describes human movement only. It must remain
> valid even if the rendering system behind it is rewritten from scratch.
>
> Sources: sports biomechanics, physical-therapy literature, strength & conditioning
> practice, and human anatomy. Where expert opinion differs, the consensus is stated
> and the alternatives are noted.

---

## 1. Exercise Overview

**Movement goal.** A Cossack squat is a frontal-plane, single-leg-loaded squat. From
standing with feet wide, the athlete shifts weight onto one leg, flexing that hip and
knee deeply (the working leg) while the opposite leg stays straight and long, its foot
flat and pointing forward (or very slightly out). The pelvis drops on the working side;
the trunk stays upright. The movement alternates sides each repetition.

**Training goal.** Develop unilateral frontal-plane strength, hip adductor/abductor
mobility, and ankle dorsiflexion, with primary emphasis on the working-leg gluteals,
quadriceps, and adductors, plus eccentric demand on the straight-leg adductors.
Secondary trunk and ankle stability.

**Intended muscles.**

- Working leg: gluteus maximus/medius (hip control), quadriceps (knee flexion under
  load), adductors (hip adduction/descent control and stretch).
- Straight leg: adductors (eccentric lengthening), hamstrings and gastrocnemius (knee/
  ankle extension hold).
- Trunk: rectus abdominis, obliques, erector spinae, quadratus lumborum (upright,
  level pelvis, anti-side-bend).
- Supporting: tibialis anterior / ankle evertors-invertors (foot control), hip
  abductors (pelvic leveling).

**Common execution style.** From a wide stance, the athlete sits the working hip back
and down, tracking the working knee over the working toe, while the straight leg
remains long and the straight foot stays flat and forward. The athlete then drives
through the working foot to stand and repeats to the other side. Breathing: inhale on
the descent, exhale on the drive up.

---

## 2. Pose Purpose

This specification treats the **bottom (deep) position** of the Cossack squat as the
canonical audited pose, with the working leg deeply flexed.

**Bottom position (deep Cossack squat).** One foot (working) is planted wide to the
side, flat, the working hip and knee flexed so the working thigh drops toward horizontal
and the working knee tracks over the working toe. The opposite (straight) leg is fully
extended: straight-leg hip abducted, straight-leg knee fully extended, straight foot
flat and pointing forward. The pelvis has dropped on the working side (hip-driven, not
spinal side-bend). The trunk is upright, facing forward. Weight is on the working foot;
the straight foot provides a secondary anchor.

**Working knee tracking.** The working knee travels out over the working foot and in
line with the second/third toe — no valgus collapse inward, no varus bow outward.

**Straight leg.** Stays straight and long; the straight foot remains flat (heel down),
pointing forward — not pivoted to the side, not lifted.

**Return.** The athlete drives through the working foot to stand, then shifts to the
other side. The described pose is the deep bottom hold on one side; the animation
alternates sides across the cycle.

---

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment

**Center of mass.** The whole-body COM sits over the base defined by the two feet (wide
frontal-plane base) and shifts toward the working foot as depth increases. It must
remain within the support polygon.

**Balance.** Load is carried predominantly on the working foot; the straight foot
provides a long secondary anchor. The body is balanced in the frontal plane: no
forward/backward tipping beyond a slight lean.

**Symmetry.** The two legs are deliberately asymmetrical (one bent, one straight), but
the trunk must stay square to the working-leg direction (facing forward, not rotated).
The shoulders are level and square to the pelvis. The pelvis is level front-to-back (no
rotation) but is intentionally lowered on the working side — it is the movement, not a
fault.

**Posture.** The trunk is upright over the working hip. The straight leg is a straight
line from hip to ankle. The working shin is near-vertical; the working thigh approaches
horizontal.

**Visual appearance.** From the front, the body shows a clear "A": two legs spread, one
bent (working) and one straight, trunk upright in the middle. From the side (along the
straight leg), the working thigh drops and the trunk stays tall.

---

## 4. Head

**Neck.** The cervical spine is a neutral continuation of the thoracic spine. No
excessive flexion or extension.

**Gaze.** The eyes look forward, toward the horizon or the floor ahead. Level, not
tilted.

**Chin.** Neutral and slightly packed; ears roughly above the shoulders from the side.
No jut or lateral tilt.

**Cervical alignment.** The cervical curve is preserved but not exaggerated; one gentle,
unbroken line. No gooseneck, no turtle tuck, no lateral side-bend.

---

## 5. Spine

**Lumbar.** Neutral. The natural lumbar arch is maintained. The pelvis drops between the
feet by hip adduction/abduction, **not by side-bending (lateral flexion) of the lumbar
spine**.

**Thoracic.** Neutral-to-slightly-extended. The upper back is flat and broad; no
rounding. The rib cage is stacked over the pelvis with the anterior core engaged.

**Rib cage.** Ribs down, anterior abdominal wall braced. Chest open, not flared or
collapsed.

**Pelvis.** Neutral in tilt, but lowered on the working side as part of the movement.
Square (no rotation), level front-to-back. The gluteals, adductors, and core
co-contract to control the drop.

**State summary.** Lumbar: neutral (no side-bend). Thoracic: neutral (slight extension
acceptable). Pelvis: square, no rotation; lowered on the working side as intended. The
spine stays long and upright; depth comes from the hips, not spinal side-bending.

---

## 6. Upper Body

**Shoulders (glenohumeral).** Level, square to the pelvis (facing forward along the
working-leg line), relaxed away from the ears. Arms hang at the sides or are held in a
chosen counterbalance position (both arms extended forward, or hands at the chest). The
humerus is unloaded and does not shrug.

**Scapula.** Flat on the rib cage; no winging or excessive elevation.

**Clavicles.** Level and symmetric; do not ride up.

**Elbows.** Free per the chosen arm style.

**Forearms / wrists / hands.** Relaxed, not clenched (unless a load is held).

**Note.** The upper body is postural and stabilizing; it must not side-bend, twist, or
tilt the trunk. Any held load increases trunk-stability demand but does not change the
lower-body landmarks.

---

## 7. Lower Body

**Working hip.** Flexed and adducted (the working thigh drops toward horizontal and the
pelvis lowers on this side). The adductors and glute control the descent.

**Working knee.** Flexed to track over the working foot, in line with the second/third
toe. No valgus, no varus. Working shin near-vertical (≈0–20° from vertical). The foot
points forward or slightly outward (≤15–30° common for comfort).

**Working ankle.** Dorsiflexed; the working foot flat, heel planted. The heel does not
lift at the bottom.

**Working foot.** Flat on the floor — heel and toe both down — pointing forward (or very
slightly outward). Primary load-bearing contact.

**Straight hip.** Abducted (the straight thigh is extended out to the side from the
pelvis). The straight adductors are eccentrically lengthened.

**Straight knee.** Fully extended (straight), not bent. The straight leg is the "long"
leg.

**Straight ankle.** Neutral; the straight foot stays flat (heel down), pointing forward
— not pivoted sideways.

**Straight foot.** Flat on the floor, pointing forward (toes ahead, not turned out to the
side). Secondary anchor. The heel stays down.

**Weight distribution.** Predominantly on the working foot (the bent side). The straight
foot keeps contact and provides a long balance base but carries less load.

---

## 8. Contacts

**Working foot–floor contact (×1, primary).**

- *Contact surface:* the whole sole — heel and toe/ball — flat on the floor.
- *Expected pressure:* the primary load; heaviest under the heel/midfoot through the toe;
  the knee tracks over this foot.
- *Stability:* fixed. The working foot does not slide or lift; it is the drive anchor.
- *Visual appearance:* flat foot, heel down, toes forward/slightly out, knee tracking over
  the toes.

**Straight foot–floor contact (×1, secondary).**

- *Contact surface:* the whole sole flat on the floor; heel down; the foot points straight
  forward (not pivoted to the side).
- *Expected pressure:* light, balance-only; not a driving load.
- *Stability:* fixed as a long side anchor; the straight foot does not slide or lift (the
  knee stays straight).
- *Visual appearance:* straight leg, flat foot pointing forward, heel down.

No knee, hand, or hip contact with the floor in the canonical Cossack squat.

---

## 9. Range of Motion

**Expected ROM.**

- Working knee: flexes to roughly 90° (thigh horizontal) at a standard deep Cossack;
  deeper acceptable with mobility.
- Working hip: flexes/adducts as the pelvis drops; adductors lengthen on the working side.
- Working ankle: dorsiflexes to keep the shin near-vertical and the heel down.
- Straight knee: stays extended (~0°); straight-leg hip abducts/extends; straight-leg
  adductors lengthen.
- Spine/trunk: minimal change; stays long and upright (no lateral flexion).

**Acceptable variation.**

- Depth from thigh-parallel to slightly above/below parallel on the working side.
- Slight forward trunk lean (from the hip) up to ~20–30°.
- Working foot turned out up to ~15–30° for comfort.
- Hand position per chosen style.

**Unacceptable variation.**

- Working knee valgus (collapsing inward) or varus.
- Working heel lifting at the bottom.
- Straight knee bending (the straight leg must stay straight).
- Straight foot pivoting/rolling to the side (heel lifting, toes turning out).
- Pelvis rotation or front-to-back tilt (the drop on the working side is fine; rotation is
  not).
- Lumbar side-bending (lateral flexion) instead of hip-driven drop.
- Torso collapsing forward or twisting.
- Weight shifting off the working foot onto the straight foot excessively.
- Asymmetric shoulder tilt / trunk rotation.

---

## 10. Muscle Intent

Muscles that should *visually* appear engaged:

- **Adductors (working side)** — control and are stretched by the hip drop.
- **Gluteus maximus / medius (working side)** — hip control of the bent side.
- **Quadriceps (working side)** — knee flexion under load.
- **Adductors (straight side)** — eccentrically lengthened; visible long inner-thigh line.
- **Hamstrings / gastrocnemius (straight side)** — keep the straight leg long.
- **Gluteus medius (both sides)** — pelvic leveling and control.
- **Rectus abdominis / obliques / quadratus lumborum** — upright, level-pelvis trunk
  control (no side-bend).
- **Erector spinae** — posterior trunk rigidity.
- **Tibialis anterior (working side)** — foot dorsiflexion control.

The body reads as "one leg loaded and bent, one leg long and straight, trunk tall between
them."

## 11. Visual Checkpoints

An auditor should be able to confirm each of the following from the rendered pose:

1. ✓ Working foot flat, heel down, pointing forward (≤30° out).
2. ✓ Working knee flexed ~90° (thigh near horizontal) at depth.
3. ✓ Working knee tracks over the working foot, aligned with the second/third toe.
4. ✓ Working knee shows no valgus or varus.
5. ✓ Working shin near-vertical; working heel remains on the floor.
6. ✓ Working hip flexed/adducted; pelvis dropped on the working side.
7. ✓ Straight leg is straight (knee fully extended).
8. ✓ Straight foot flat, heel down, pointing straight forward (not pivoted).
9. ✓ Straight adductors visibly lengthened (long straight leg).
10. ✓ Feet are spread wide, defining a stable frontal-plane base.
11. ✓ Weight predominantly on the working foot.
12. ✓ Torso upright or slight hip-hinge lean; not collapsed forward.
13. ✓ Lumbar spine neutral — no lateral side-bend.
14. ✓ Thoracic spine flat/broad — no kyphosis.
15. ✓ Rib cage stacked and braced; chest open and tall.
16. ✓ Pelvis square (no rotation); lowered on the working side as intended.
17. ✓ Neck neutral continuation of the spine; chin neutral and packed.
18. ✓ Gaze forward.
19. ✓ Shoulders level and square to the pelvis, not shrugged.
20. ✓ Scapulae flat — no winging.
21. ✓ No trunk twist; shoulders face the same direction as the pelvis.
22. ✓ Working foot does not slide or lift.
23. ✓ Straight foot does not slide or lift; knee stays straight.
24. ✓ No working-knee floor contact; the bend is at the hip/knee, not a fall.
25. ✓ Depth created by hip adduction/flexion, not by rounding or side-bending the spine.
26. ✓ Loaded working leg reads engaged; straight leg reads long and stable.
27. ✓ Overall silhouette: upright trunk, one bent leg, one straight leg ("A" shape).
28. ✓ No trunk tilt to one side (the pelvis drop is hip-driven, not spinal).
29. ✓ The stance width is sufficient to keep the trunk over the working hip.
30. ✓ The rendered pose matches the visual checkpoints in §11.

## 12. Common Mistakes

**Working knee valgus (caving inward).** *Why wrong:* medial knee-joint stress. *Visual:*
working knee points inward vs the foot; ankle rolls in.

**Working heel lifting.** *Why wrong:* shifts load forward, reduces stability, indicates
poor ankle dorsiflexion. *Visual:* working foot rolls onto the toe; shin pitches forward.

**Straight knee bending.** *Why wrong:* defeats the lengthening/adductor stretch intent.
*Visual:* both legs bent; no long straight leg.

**Straight foot pivoting/rolling out.** *Why wrong:* reduces the stable base and the
adductor lengthening. *Visual:* straight toes point sideways; heel off floor.

**Pelvis rotation / tilt.** *Why wrong:* indicates poor hip control; the deliberate
working-side drop is fine but rotation is not. *Visual:* hips not square; one side rotated
forward.

**Lumbar side-bend (lateral flexion).** *Why wrong:* off-loads the hips and strains the
lumbar spine. *Visual:* the trunk leans sideways; the lower back curves laterally.

**Rounded upper back / forward collapse.** *Why wrong:* changes the movement. *Visual:*
shoulders well ahead of the working hip; steep trunk angle.

**Weight shifting to the straight foot.** *Why wrong:* off-loads the target working leg.
*Visual:* COM over the straight foot; working heel may lift.

**Twisting torso / uneven shoulders.** *Why wrong:* poor trunk control. *Visual:*
shoulders not square to the pelvis; one shoulder drops.

## 13. Acceptance Criteria

The Cossack squat (deep bottom position, one side loaded) is **accepted only if every
criterion is satisfied**:

- [ ] Working foot flat, heel down, forward (≤30° out), not sliding.
- [ ] Working knee flexed ~90° (thigh near horizontal) at depth.
- [ ] Working knee tracks over the working foot, aligned with the second/third toe.
- [ ] No working-knee valgus or varus.
- [ ] Working shin near-vertical; working heel stays on the floor.
- [ ] Working hip flexed/adducted; pelvis dropped on the working side.
- [ ] Straight leg straight (knee fully extended), not bending.
- [ ] Straight foot flat, heel down, pointing forward, not pivoted.
- [ ] Weight predominantly on the working foot.
- [ ] Torso upright or slight hip-hinge lean; not collapsed forward.
- [ ] Lumbar spine neutral — no lateral side-bend.
- [ ] Thoracic spine neutral; no rounding.
- [ ] Rib cage stacked and braced; chest open.
- [ ] Pelvis square (no rotation); working-side drop is hip-driven.
- [ ] Neck continues the spinal line; chin neutral and packed.
- [ ] Shoulders level, square, not shrugged; scapulae flat.
- [ ] No trunk twist; shoulders and hips face the same direction.
- [ ] Gaze forward.
- [ ] The rendered pose matches the visual checkpoints in §11.
