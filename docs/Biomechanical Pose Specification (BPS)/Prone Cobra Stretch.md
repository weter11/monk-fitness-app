# Prone Cobra Stretch

## 1. Exercise Overview
The Prone Cobra (sometimes called "Prone Cobra" or a prone scapular-retraction hold) is a prone postural-strengthening and mobility hold that emphasizes the posterior shoulder and upper-back muscles. Unlike the pressed-up cobra, the arms are held in a "W" or external-rotation position — elbows bent ~90°, upper arms out to the sides, forearms pointing forward or up with the thumbs rotated upward (external rotation at the shoulder). From lying face down, the chest and arms lift as the shoulder blades squeeze together and down. It is a staple of posture-correction and lower-trapezius activation work. The canonical held position is the lifted chest with arms in the "W," thumbs up, shoulder blades pinched.

## 2. Pose Purpose
- Strengthen the postural muscles of the upper back: lower trapezius, middle trapezius, rhomboids, and the posterior deltoid/rotator cuff.
- Improve scapular retraction and depression (squeezing and lowering the shoulder blades).
- Train shoulder external rotation in a loaded, weight-bearing position.
- Counteract rounded-shoulder and forward-head posture.
- Mobilize the thoracic spine into gentle extension while the arms are externally rotated.

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment
- Prone: stomach and legs on the floor, forehead resting (or lifted slightly) on the floor.
- Arms in a "W": upper arms abducted ~45–90° from the trunk, elbows bent ~90°, forearms forward/up, palms facing inward or thumbs up.
- The chest lifts off the floor as the scapulae retract and depress.
- The lift is driven by the upper back, not by cranking the neck.
- The low back stays long and quiet; the pelvis stays grounded.

## 4. Head
- Head is in line with the gently extended spine; the forehead may rest or lift slightly.
- The cervical spine extends softly as the chest rises — not cranked back.
- Chin is slightly tucked; the neck is long, not strained.

## 5. Spine
- **Thoracic:** gently extended as the chest lifts — the upper back is the prime mover.
- **Lumbar:** neutral and quiet; it does not arch excessively.
- **Cervical:** neutral-to-slightly extended, following the thorax.
- The extension is mild and controlled; this is a posture hold, not a deep backbend.

## 6. Upper Body
- Arms in the "W": shoulders externally rotated (thumbs up), scapulae retracted (squeezed together) and depressed (pulled down).
- The chest lifts; the pectorals lengthen slightly.
- Elbows stay at ~90° and out to the sides (the "W" shape); forearms may point forward or upward.
- The posterior deltoids and rotator cuff work to hold the external rotation against gravity.
- No shrugging — the shoulders stay down away from the ears.

## 7. Lower Body
- Pelvis, thighs, and legs remain flat on the floor.
- Legs may be straight (tops of feet down) or gently squeezed together; glutes softly engaged to keep the pelvis stable without jamming the low back.
- Hips stay grounded; they do not lift.
- The lower body is quiet and stabilizing.

## 8. Contacts
- Abdomen/pelvis and anterior thighs/legs contact the floor.
- Forehead (or chin) may lightly contact the floor; the chest lifts off.
- Tops of the feet contact the floor (legs straight) or toes if flexed.
- The arms are off the floor (lifted in the W) — they are not bearing weight.

## 9. Range of Motion
- Thoracic extension: mild, just enough to lift the chest off the floor.
- Shoulder external rotation: held at ~the W position (upper arm abducted, elbow flexed 90°, forearm forward/up).
- Scapular retraction/depression: end-range pinch-and-down hold.
- Held 10–45 s; repeated for several reps. Depth limited by comfort and the ability to keep the shoulders down.

## 10. Muscle Intent
- Lower and middle trapezius + rhomboids: retract and depress the scapulae (the "pinch and down").
- Posterior deltoid and infraspinatus/teres minor (rotator cuff): hold shoulder external rotation (thumbs up).
- Erector spinae (thoracic): lift the chest into gentle extension.
- Glutes and hamstrings: lightly stabilize the pelvis.
- Deep neck flexors: keep the cervical spine aligned.

## 11. Visual Checkpoints
- [ ] Body is prone, pelvis and legs flat on the floor.
- [ ] Arms form a clear "W": elbows bent ~90°, upper arms out to the sides.
- [ ] Thumbs are rotated up (shoulders externally rotated).
- [ ] Shoulder blades are squeezed together AND pulled down (not shrugged up).
- [ ] The chest lifts off the floor via the upper back.
- [ ] The low back is quiet, not over-arched.
- [ ] Head is neutral, not cranked back; chin slightly tucked.
- [ ] Shoulders are down, away from the ears.
- [ ] The neck is long, not strained.
- [ ] The pelvis stays grounded (hips do not lift).
- [ ] Forearms point forward/up consistently in the W.
- [ ] Breathing is smooth; the chest expands.
- [ ] The hold is static, not bouncing.
- [ ] No sharp low-back or neck pain.
- [ ] Both sides are symmetrical (W even on both arms).

## 12. Common Mistakes
- Shrugging the shoulders up toward the ears instead of depressing them.
- Letting the elbows drop back (losing the W / external rotation).
- Cranking the neck back to lift the chest instead of using the upper back.
- Over-arching the low back.
- Lifting the pelvis/hips off the floor.
- Letting one shoulder blade work more than the other (asymmetry).
- Holding the breath.

## 13. Acceptance Criteria
- [ ] The arms hold a symmetrical "W" with shoulders externally rotated (thumbs up) and scapulae retracted and depressed.
- [ ] The chest lifts off the floor via thoracic extension while the lumbar spine stays neutral.
- [ ] The head and neck are in neutral alignment, not cranked.
- [ ] The pelvis remains grounded and the lower body quiet.
- [ ] The position is held statically with relaxed breathing and no neck or low-back pain.
