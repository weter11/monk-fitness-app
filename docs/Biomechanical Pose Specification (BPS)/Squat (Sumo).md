# Biomechanical Pose Specification — Squat (Sumo)

## 1. Exercise overview
The sumo squat is a wide-stance squat variant in which the feet are placed well outside shoulder
width and turned outward, dropping the hips between the knees. It is a closed-chain, bilateral
lower-body movement that biases the adductor (inner-thigh) musculature and the gluteals more than a
narrow-stance squat, while permitting a more upright trunk because the hips start closer to the
feet. It is named for its resemblance to a sumo wrestler's stance.

## 2. Pose purpose
To train the squat pattern with a wide base that emphasizes hip adductor length and strength, gluteal
recruitment, and medial-quadriceps involvement, while reducing forward trunk lean and ankle
demand. The pose is useful for lifters with limited ankle dorsiflexion or long femurs, and for
targeting the inner thigh.

## 3. Whole-body alignment
- Feet set wide — clearly beyond shoulder width — with the toes pointed outward (typically 30–45°,
  sometimes more).
- The hip joints sit between the knees; the femur angles diagonally from hip to knee.
- Center of mass tracks vertically over the midfoot within the wide base.
- Trunk is relatively upright (more so than narrow-stance squats) because the hips are already close
  to the feet.
- Pelvis level; symmetrical loading.

## 4. Head
- Cervical spine neutral, gaze forward and slightly down.
- Head stacked on the upright trunk; no forward jut.

## 5. Spine
- Neutral, braced lumbar and thoracic posture; natural curves maintained.
- Forward lean is minimal — typically less than in a standard or air squat — because the wide stance
  shortens the horizontal distance to the feet.
- Trunk lean, if any, originates at the hips, not from spinal flexion.

## 6. Upper body
- Chest up and open; scapulae stable (down and back).
- Arms hang naturally or are held forward/at the chest for balance; they do not bear load against the
  ground.
- Shoulders relaxed; the upper body remains tall and rigid.

## 7. Lower body
- Stance: wide with external hip rotation so the knees align with the outward-pointing toes.
- Descent: hips drop almost straight down between the knees; knees track outward over the toes.
- Knee valgus is prevented by active external rotation of the hips (gluteus medius/minimus, deep
  rotators).
- Depth commonly reaches at least parallel; the wide stance often allows deeper hip descent without
  excessive trunk lean.
- At the bottom: hips deeply flexed and abducted, knees flexed and tracked outward, ankles
  dorsiflexed.
- Ascent: drive through the full foot, extend hips and knees, adductors and glutes lift the pelvis.

## 8. Contacts
- Both feet flat, set wide, toes turned out; weight distributed across the whole foot biased to
  midfoot/heel.
- No hand or support contact.
- Base of support is the wide, outward-angled footprint pair.

## 9. Range of Motion
- Hip flexion + abduction: deep flexion with significant abduction (femur angled outward ~30–45°).
- Knee flexion: to at least ~100–120° at parallel depth.
- Ankle dorsiflexion: moderate; the wide stance reduces required dorsiflexion versus narrow squats.
- Hip external rotation: increased to keep knees over the turned-out toes.
- Trunk lean: minimal (~10–25° from vertical).
- Full extension at the top: neutral standing.

## 10. Muscle intent
- Primary movers (concentric ascent): gluteus maximus and hamstrings (hip extension); quadriceps,
  especially vastus medialis (knee extension with outward tracking).
- Adductors (adductor magnus, longus, brevis, gracilis): lengthen on the way down and contribute to
  hip extension/adduction on the way up — a hallmark of the sumo stance.
- Stabilizers: gluteus medius/minimus and deep external rotators (outward knee tracking, pelvic
  level); erector spinae and abdominals (upright bracing); tibialis anterior (ankle control).

## 11. Visual checkpoints
- [ ] Feet set wide, well beyond shoulder width.
- [ ] Toes pointed outward, roughly 30–45°.
- [ ] Knees track outward directly over the turned-out toes.
- [ ] Hips drop between the knees, not in front.
- [ ] Trunk stays relatively upright.
- [ ] Chest lifted, spine braced and neutral.
- [ ] Weight centered over midfoot/heel.
- [ ] Heels remain flat.
- [ ] No knee valgus (inward collapse) — knees push outward.
- [ ] Pelvis level, no side drop.
- [ ] Head neutral, gaze forward.
- [ ] Adductors engaged (inner thighs active).
- [ ] Depth reaches at least parallel.
- [ ] Ascent drives through the full foot.
- [ ] Hips and knees extend together.
- [ ] Full lockout, neutral spine at top.
- [ ] Symmetry between left and right.
- [ ] Smooth, controlled descent and ascent.
- [ ] No bouncing at the bottom.
- [ ] Shoulders relaxed, not shrugged.

## 12. Common mistakes
- Toes not turned out enough, forcing knees inward (valgus) against the stance.
- Knees not tracking over the toes — collapsing inward due to weak external rotators.
- Stance too narrow, losing the sumo biomechanical advantage.
- Rounding the back from poor bracing.
- Heels lifting despite the reduced ankle demand (weight too far forward).
- Excessive trunk lean defeating the upright purpose.
- Asymmetric stance width or depth.

## 13. Acceptance criteria
- [ ] Wide stance with toes turned out 30–45°.
- [ ] Knees track outward over the toes with no valgus.
- [ ] Hips descend between the knees.
- [ ] Trunk upright, spine braced and neutral.
- [ ] Heels flat, weight over midfoot/heel.
- [ ] Depth reaches at least parallel.
- [ ] Full extension at the top.
- [ ] Symmetric, controlled movement.
