# Thoracic Rotations (Reps)

## 1. Exercise Overview
Thoracic Rotations are a quadruped mobility drill performed on the hands and knees. The exercise isolates rotation (axial twisting) of the thoracic spine, a movement commonly restricted relative to the more mobile lumbar and cervical regions. The classic expression is the "thread the needle" pattern: from a neutral quadruped position, one hand lifts and the same-side shoulder drops toward the floor as the arm reaches underneath the body, then the arm sweeps open and up toward the ceiling, rotating the thorax the opposite way. Alternating sides produces a rhythmic rotation through the mid-back. The canonical repeated sub-pose is the "opened" position — one arm reaching up toward the ceiling with the chest rotated and opened.

## 2. Pose Purpose
- Improve thoracic spine axial rotation range of motion.
- Promote independent movement between the thoracic spine and the (relatively stable) lumbar spine and pelvis.
- Mobilize the scapulothoracic region and improve shoulder articulation.
- Reduce rotational stiffness that limits throwing, swinging, and rotational lifting patterns.
- Provide a low-load, controlled rotational warm-up for the trunk.

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment
- Starting position: quadruped — hands under shoulders, knees under hips, neutral spine (tabletop).
- The pelvis and lumbar spine remain relatively quiet and level; the rotation is created at the thorax.
- The supporting arm stays extended and stable; the moving arm drives the rotation.
- Trunk stays long; the rib cage rotates as a unit rather than collapsing.
- Movement is slow and breathed; rotation paired with exhale.

## 4. Head
- Head follows the rotation of the cervical spine, which tracks the thorax.
- In the "opened" position the eyes track the reaching hand up toward the ceiling.
- The neck is not cranked independently; it moves as the top of the thoracic rotation.
- Avoid jutting the chin or letting the head drop passively.

## 5. Spine
- **Thoracic (T1–T12):** primary segment of axial rotation; rotates around the long axis of the body.
- **Lumbar (L1–L5):** stays near neutral with minimal rotation and minimal side-bending; the pelvis does not rotate significantly with the thorax.
- **Cervical:** rotates with the thorax, eyes leading toward the upward hand in the open position.
- The key control is dissociation: the low back and pelvis stay still while the upper back turns.

## 6. Upper Body
- Supporting (down) arm: extended, shoulder stable, scapulae set (not shrugged).
- Moving arm: reaches under the body (threading) then sweeps overhead/upward, driving thoracic rotation.
- Shoulder girdle moves freely on the rib cage (scapulothoracic mobility) as the arm opens.
- The chest rotates and "opens" toward the ceiling in the end range; the upper arm externally rotates as it reaches up.

## 7. Lower Body
- Knees under hips, shins on the floor; feet relaxed.
- Pelvis level and still — it does NOT rotate with the thorax (this is the dissociation target).
- Hips stay square to the floor; one hip does not hike or drop more than the other.
- The lumbar spine stays long and quiet.

## 8. Contacts
- Hands (palms) on the floor under the shoulders.
- Knees and shins on the floor under the hips.
- In the "thread" position the moving shoulder/upper arm may lightly brush the floor; the reaching hand is off the floor.

## 9. Range of Motion
- Thoracic axial rotation: from neutral through comfortable end-range rotation toward each side, alternating.
- Lumbar rotation: minimal (the lumbar spine rotates far less than the thorax — this is the point of the drill).
- Shoulder: full reach from under-body to overhead.
- Typically 5–10 slow rotations per side; progress by increasing the openness of the chest, not by forcing the neck.

## 10. Muscle Intent
- Thoracic erector spinae and multifidus: control and assist axial rotation.
- Obliques (internal/external) and quadratus lumborum: create and limit rotation, promoting dissociation.
- Scapular stabilizers (serratus anterior, trapezius) and rotator cuff: manage the moving shoulder as it opens.
- Supporting-side latissimus dorsi and triceps: stabilize the supporting arm.
- Deep neck flexors: keep the cervical spine aligned with the thorax.

## 11. Visual Checkpoints
- [ ] Starting position is a clean quadruped: hands under shoulders, knees under hips.
- [ ] Rotation originates in the mid-back, not the low back or pelvis.
- [ ] The pelvis stays level and does not rotate with the thorax.
- [ ] The supporting arm is straight and stable.
- [ ] The moving arm threads under then sweeps clearly overhead.
- [ ] The chest opens toward the ceiling in the end range.
- [ ] Eyes track the upward hand (head follows the rotation).
- [ ] Shoulders are not shrugged toward the ears.
- [ ] The lumbar spine stays long, not side-bent or twisted excessively.
- [ ] Hips remain square to the floor.
- [ ] Breathing is smooth, rotation on exhale.
- [ ] Movement is slow and controlled, alternating sides evenly.
- [ ] The neck is not cranked independently of the thorax.
- [ ] Each rep returns to neutral tabletop between sides.
- [ ] No pinching or discomfort in the low back.

## 12. Common Mistakes
- Rotating the pelvis and low back instead of isolating the thorax.
- Hiking or dropping one hip (loss of pelvic level).
- Shrugging the shoulders up toward the ears.
- Cranking the neck to "reach" rather than rotating the chest.
- Collapsing the rib cage instead of keeping the trunk long.
- Rushing the rotation with momentum.
- Letting the supporting elbow bend and collapse.

## 13. Acceptance Criteria
- [ ] Thoracic spine rotates through a clear range while the lumbar spine and pelvis remain stable and level.
- [ ] The chest visibly opens toward the ceiling in the end-range position.
- [ ] The head and neck track the thoracic rotation without independent craning.
- [ ] Supporting arm remains extended and shoulders depressed.
- [ ] Movement is slow, breathing-paced, and symmetrical side-to-side.
- [ ] No low-back compensation or discomfort is present.
