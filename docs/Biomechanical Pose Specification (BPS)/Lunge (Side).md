# Biomechanical Pose Specification — Lunge (Side)

> **Canonical reference.** This document defines what a lateral (side) lunge
> should look like from a strict biomechanics, anatomy, and strength-and-
> conditioning standpoint. It is implementation-independent and describes human
> movement only. It must remain valid even if the rendering system behind it is
> rewritten from scratch.
>
> Sources: sports biomechanics, physical-therapy literature, strength &
> conditioning practice, and human anatomy. Where expert opinion differs, the
> consensus is stated and the alternatives are noted.

---

## 1. Exercise Overview

**Movement goal.** A lateral (side) lunge is a frontal-plane, closed-chain
movement in which one foot steps out to the side while the stepping leg's hip and
knee flex and the other (trailing) leg stays extended straight. The pelvis drops
between the feet, loading the adductors and gluteals of the working side. The
trunk stays upright.

**Training goal.** Develop frontal-plane strength, hip mobility, and groin
(adductor) flexibility, with primary emphasis on the adductor complex and
gluteus maximus/medius of the stepping leg, and eccentric demand on the
adductors of the trailing leg. Secondary trunk and ankle stability.

**Intended muscles.**

- Stepping (working) leg: adductor longus/brevis/magnus (hip adduction control
  and stretch), gluteus maximus and medius (hip extension/abduction control),
  quadriceps (knee flexion under load).
- Trailing (straight) leg: adductors (eccentric lengthening), hamstrings and
  gastrocnemius (knee/ankle extension hold).
- Trunk: rectus abdominis, obliques, erector spinae, quadratus lumborum
  (upright, level pelvis, anti-side-bend).
- Supporting: tibialis anterior / ankle evertors-invertors (foot control), hip
  abductors (pelvic leveling on the straight side).

**Common execution style.** From standing with feet together, the athlete steps
one foot directly out to the side, keeping the trailing leg straight and the
trailing foot flat and pointing forward. The stepping knee flexes and tracks over
the stepping toe while the pelvis drops between the feet. The athlete then drives
off the stepping foot to return to standing. Breathing is typically inhale on
the step out, exhale on the drive back.

---

## 2. Pose Purpose

This specification treats the **bottom (deep) position** of the lateral lunge as
the canonical audited pose.

**Bottom position (deep lateral lunge).** One foot is planted out to the side
(the working foot), flat and pointing forward (or very slightly outward). The
working hip and knee are flexed so the working thigh drops toward horizontal and
the working knee tracks over the working toe. The trailing leg is straight:
trailing hip extended/abducted, trailing knee fully extended, trailing foot flat
and pointing forward. The pelvis has dropped between the feet (the working hip
lowers). The trunk is upright, with a controlled slight forward lean from the
hip acceptable but no side-bending. Weight is on the working foot; the trailing
foot provides a secondary anchor.

**Working knee tracking.** The working knee travels out over the working foot
and in line with the working second/third toe — no valgus collapse inward, no
varus bow outward.

**Trailing leg.** Stays straight and long; the trailing foot remains flat (heel
down), pointing forward — not pivoted to the side.

**Return.** The athlete drives through the working foot to stand back up and
brings the feet together. The described pose is the bottom hold.

---

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment

**Center of mass.** The whole-body COM sits over the working (stepping) foot and
drops vertically between the two feet as the athlete lowers. It must remain
within the support polygon defined by the two feet (wide base in the frontal
plane).

**Balance.** Load is carried predominantly on the working foot; the trailing
foot provides a long secondary anchor keeping the body from tipping. The body is
balanced in the frontal plane: no forward/backward tipping beyond a slight lean.

**Symmetry.** The two legs are deliberately asymmetrical (one bent, one
straight), but the trunk must stay square to the working-leg direction (facing
forward, not rotated). The shoulders are level and square to the pelvis. The
pelvis is level front-to-back (no rotation) but is intentionally lowered on the
working side — it is the movement, not a fault.

**Posture.** The trunk is upright over the working hip. The trailing leg is a
straight line from hip to ankle. The working shin is near-vertical; the working
thigh approaches horizontal.

**Visual appearance.** From the front, the body shows a clear "A": two legs
spread, one bent (working) and one straight (trailing), trunk upright in the
middle. From the side (viewed along the trailing leg), the working thigh drops
and the trunk stays tall.

---

## 4. Head

**Neck.** The cervical spine is a neutral continuation of the thoracic spine. No
excessive flexion or extension.

**Gaze.** The eyes look forward, toward the horizon or the floor ahead. The gaze
is level, not tilted.

**Chin.** Neutral and slightly packed; ears roughly above the shoulders from the
side. The head does not jut forward or tilt to the side.

**Cervical alignment.** The cervical curve is preserved but not exaggerated; one
gentle, unbroken line. No gooseneck, no turtle tuck, no lateral side-bend of the
neck.

---

## 5. Spine

**Lumbar.** Neutral. The natural lumbar arch is maintained without excessive
extension or flexion. The pelvis drops between the feet by hip adduction/abduction
at the hips, not by side-bending (lateral flexion) of the lumbar spine.

**Thoracic.** Neutral-to-slightly-extended. The upper back is flat and broad;
no rounding. The rib cage is stacked over the pelvis with the anterior core
engaged.

**Rib cage.** Ribs down, anterior abdominal wall braced. Chest open, not flared
or collapsed. The thoracic cage moves as a unit with the trunk; no lateral
side-bend.

**Pelvis.** Neutral in tilt, but lowered on the working side as part of the
movement. It stays square (no rotation) and level front-to-back. The gluteals,
adductors, and core co-contract to control the drop.

**State summary.** Lumbar: neutral (no side-bend). Thoracic: neutral (slight
extension acceptable). Pelvis: square, no rotation; lowered on the working side
as intended. The spine stays long and upright; depth comes from the hips, not
spinal side-bending.

---

## 6. Upper Body

**Shoulders (glenohumeral).** Level, square to the pelvis (facing forward along
the working-leg line), relaxed away from the ears. Arms hang at the sides or are
held in a chosen position (hands clasped at the chest, one arm reaching forward,
or both arms extended for counterbalance). The humerus is unloaded and does not
shrug.

**Scapula.** Flat on the rib cage; no winging or excessive elevation. If arms
reach or extend, the scapulae track naturally without shrugging.

**Clavicles.** Level and symmetric; do not ride up toward the neck.

**Elbows.** Free per the chosen arm style. No forced position required.

**Forearms / wrists / hands.** Relaxed, not clenched (unless a load is held).
No deviation implying shoulder compensation.

**Note.** The upper body is postural and stabilizing; it must not side-bend,
twist, or tilt the trunk. Any held load increases trunk-stability demand but
does not change the lower-body landmarks.

---

## 7. Lower Body

**Working hip.** Flexed and adducted (the working thigh drops toward horizontal
and the pelvis lowers on this side). The adductors and glute control the descent.

**Working knee.** Flexed to track over the working foot, in line with the
second/third toe. No valgus, no varus. Working shin near-vertical (≈0–20° from
vertical). The foot points forward or slightly outward (≤15–30° is common for
comfort).

**Working ankle.** Dorsiflexed; the working foot flat with the heel planted. The
heel does not lift at the bottom.

**Working foot.** Flat on the floor — heel and toe both down — pointing forward
(or very slightly outward). Primary load-bearing contact.

**Trailing hip.** Extended and abducted (the trailing thigh is straight out to
the side from the pelvis). The trailing adductors are eccentrically lengthened.

**Trailing knee.** Fully extended (straight), not bent. The trailing leg is the
"long" leg.

**Trailing ankle.** Neutral; the trailing foot stays flat (heel down), pointing
forward — not pivoted sideways.

**Trailing foot.** Flat on the floor, pointing forward (toes ahead, not turned
out to the side). Secondary anchor. The heel stays down.

**Weight distribution.** Predominantly on the working foot (the bent side). The
trailing foot keeps contact and provides a long balance base but carries less
load.

---

## 8. Contacts

**Working foot–floor contact (×1, primary).**

- *Contact surface:* the whole sole — heel and toe/ball — flat on the floor.
- *Expected pressure:* the primary load; heaviest under the heel/midfoot through
  the toe; the knee tracks over this foot.
- *Stability:* fixed. The working foot does not slide or lift; it is the drive
  anchor.
- *Visual appearance:* flat foot, heel down, toes forward/slightly out, knee
  tracking over the toes.

**Trailing foot–floor contact (×1, secondary).**

- *Contact surface:* the whole sole flat on the floor; heel down; the foot
  points straight forward (not pivoted to the side).
- *Expected pressure:* light, balance-only; not a driving load.
- *Stability:* fixed as a long rear/side anchor; the trailing foot does not
  slide or lift (the knee stays straight).
- *Visual appearance:* straight trailing leg, flat foot pointing forward, heel
  down.

No knee, hand, or hip contact with the floor in the canonical lateral lunge.

---

## 9. Range of Motion

**Expected ROM.**

- Working knee: flexes to roughly 90° (thigh horizontal) at a standard deep
  side lunge; deeper acceptable with mobility.
- Working hip: flexes/adducts as the pelvis drops; adductors lengthen on the
  working side.
- Working ankle: dorsiflexes to keep the shin near-vertical and the heel down.
- Trailing knee: stays extended (~0°); trailing hip abducts/extends; trailing
  adductors lengthen.
- Spine/trunk: minimal change; stays long and upright (no lateral flexion).

**Acceptable variation.**

- Depth from thigh-parallel to slightly above/below parallel on the working side.
- Slight forward trunk lean (from the hip) up to ~20–30°.
- Working foot turned out up to ~15–30° for comfort.
- Hand position per chosen style.

**Unacceptable variation.**

- Working knee valgus (collapsing inward) or varus.
- Working heel lifting at the bottom.
- Trailing knee bending (the trailing leg must stay straight).
- Trailing foot pivoting/rolling to the side (heel lifting, toes turning out).
- Pelvis rotation or front-to-back tilt (the drop on the working side is fine;
  rotation is not).
- Lumbar side-bending (lateral flexion) instead of hip-driven drop.
- Torso collapsing forward or twisting.
- Weight shifting off the working foot onto the trailing foot excessively.
- Asymmetric shoulder tilt / trunk rotation.

---

## 10. Muscle Intent

Muscles that should *visually* appear engaged:

- **Adductors (working side)** — control and are stretched by the hip drop;
  visible tension along the inner thigh.
- **Gluteus maximus / medius (working side)** — hip control of the bent side.
- **Quadriceps (working side)** — knee flexion under load; front of the thigh.
- **Adductors (trailing side)** — eccentrically lengthened; visible long inner
  thigh line.
- **Hamstrings / gastrocnemius (trailing side)** — keep the straight leg long.
- **Gluteus medius (both sides)** — pelvic leveling and control.
- **Rectus abdominis / obliques / quadratus lumborum** — upright, level-pelvis
  trunk control (no side-bend).
- **Erector spinae** — posterior trunk rigidity.
- **Tibialis anterior (working side)** — foot dorsiflexion control.

The body reads as "one leg loaded and bent, one leg long and straight, trunk
tall between them."

## 11. Visual Checkpoints

An auditor should be able to confirm each of the following from the rendered
pose:

1. ✓ Working foot flat, heel down, pointing forward (≤30° out).
2. ✓ Working knee flexed ~90° (thigh near horizontal) at depth.
3. ✓ Working knee tracks over the working foot, aligned with the second/third
     toe.
4. ✓ Working knee shows no valgus or varus.
5. ✓ Working shin near-vertical; working heel remains on the floor.
6. ✓ Working hip flexed/adducted; pelvis dropped on the working side.
7. ✓ Trailing leg is straight (knee fully extended).
8. ✓ Trailing foot flat, heel down, pointing straight forward (not pivoted).
9. ✓ Trailing adductors visibly lengthened (long straight leg).
10. ✓ Feet are spread wide, defining a stable frontal-plane base.
11. ✓ Weight predominantly on the working foot.
12. ✓ Torso upright or slight hip-hinge lean; not collapsed forward.
13. ✓ Lumbar spine neutral — no lateral side-bend.
14. ✓ Thoracic spine flat/broad — no kyphosis.
15. ✓ Rib cage stacked and braced; chest open and tall.
16. ✓ Pelvis square (no rotation); lowered on the working side as intended.
17. ✓ Neck neutral continuation of the spine; chin neutral and packed.
18. ✓ Gaze forward.
19. ✓ Shoulders level and square to the pelvis, not shrugged.
20. ✓ Scapulae flat — no winging.
21. ✓ No trunk twist; shoulders face the same direction as the pelvis.
22. ✓ Working foot does not slide or lift.
23. ✓ Trailing foot does not slide or lift; knee stays straight.
24. ✓ No working-knee floor contact; the bend is at the hip/knee, not a fall.
25. ✓ Depth created by hip adduction/flexion, not by rounding or side-bending
     the spine.
26. ✓ Loaded working leg reads engaged; trailing leg reads long and stable.
27. ✓ Overall silhouette: upright trunk, one bent leg, one straight leg ("A"
     shape from the front).
28. ✓ No trunk tilt to one side (the pelvis drop is hip-driven, not spinal).
29. ✓ The stance width is sufficient to keep the trunk over the working hip.
30. ✓ The rendered pose matches the visual checkpoints in §11.

## 12. Common Mistakes

**Working knee valgus (caving inward).** The working knee drops toward the
midline. *Why wrong:* medial knee-joint stress; weak glute stabilization. *Visual
difference:* working knee points inward vs the foot; ankle rolls in.

**Working heel lifting.** The working heel rises at the bottom. *Why wrong:*
shifts load forward, reduces stability, indicates poor ankle dorsiflexion. *Visual
difference:* working foot rolls onto the toe; shin pitches forward.

**Trailing knee bending.** The "straight" leg flexes. *Why wrong:* defeats the
lengthening/adductor stretch intent of the movement. *Visual difference:* both
legs bent; no long straight leg.

**Trailing foot pivoting/rolling out.** The trailing foot turns out to the side
or the heel lifts. *Why wrong:* reduces the stable base and the adductor
lengthening. *Visual difference:* trailing toes point sideways; heel off floor.

**Pelvis rotation / tilt.** The pelvis rotates or tilts front-to-back. *Why
wrong:* indicates poor hip control; the deliberate working-side drop is fine but
rotation is not. *Visual difference:* hips not square; one side rotated forward.

**Lumbar side-bend (lateral flexion).** The athlete bends the spine sideways to
drop. *Why wrong:* off-loads the hips and strains the lumbar spine. *Visual
difference:* the trunk leans sideways; the lower back curves laterally.

**Rounded upper back / forward collapse.** The torso pitches far forward. *Why
wrong:* changes the movement and reduces frontal-plane demand. *Visual
difference:* shoulders well ahead of the working hip; steep trunk angle.

**Weight shifting to the trailing foot.** The athlete leans onto the straight
leg. *Why wrong:* off-loads the target working leg. *Visual difference:* COM over
the trailing foot; working heel may lift.

**Twisting torso / uneven shoulders.** Shoulders rotate or tilt. *Why wrong:*
poor trunk control. *Visual difference:* shoulders not square to the pelvis; one
shoulder drops.

## 13. Acceptance Criteria

The lateral (side) lunge (bottom position) is **accepted only if every criterion
is satisfied**:

- [ ] Working foot flat, heel down, forward (≤30° out), not sliding.
- [ ] Working knee flexed ~90° (thigh near horizontal) at depth.
- [ ] Working knee tracks over the working foot, aligned with the second/third
      toe.
- [ ] No working-knee valgus or varus.
- [ ] Working shin near-vertical; working heel stays on the floor.
- [ ] Working hip flexed/adducted; pelvis dropped on the working side.
- [ ] Trailing leg straight (knee fully extended), not bending.
- [ ] Trailing foot flat, heel down, pointing forward, not pivoted.
- [ ] Weight predominantly on the working foot.
- [ ] Torso upright or slight hip-hinge lean; not collapsed forward.
- [ ] Lumbar spine neutral — no lateral side-bend.
- [ ] Thoracic spine neutral; no rounding.
- [ ] Rib cage stacked and braced; chest open.
- [ ] Pelvis square (no rotation); working-side drop is hip-driven.
- [ ] Neck continues the spinal line; chin neutral and packed.
- [ ] Shoulders level, square, not shrugged; scapulae flat.
- [ ] No trunk twist; shoulders and hips face the same direction.
- [ ] Gaze forward.
- [ ] The rendered pose matches the visual checkpoints in §11.
