# Biomechanical Pose Specification — Lunge (Reverse)

> **Canonical reference.** This document defines what a reverse lunge should look
> like from a strict biomechanics, anatomy, and strength-and-conditioning
> standpoint. It is implementation-independent and describes human movement only.
> It must remain valid even if the rendering system behind it is rewritten from
> scratch.
>
> Sources: sports biomechanics, physical-therapy literature, strength &
> conditioning practice, and human anatomy. Where expert opinion differs, the
> consensus is stated and the alternatives are noted.

---

## 1. Exercise Overview

**Movement goal.** A reverse lunge is a split-stance, closed-chain movement in
which one foot steps *backward* to a planted rear position while the front foot
stays fixed. The body lowers by flexing the front hip and knee; the rear knee
descends toward the floor behind the body, and the rear heel lifts. The torso is
held upright. Compared with the forward lunge, the rear (stepping) leg bears the
eccentric load of the step and the glute/hamstring of the rear hip are
emphasized on the return.

**Training goal.** Develop unilateral lower-body strength and posterior-chain
emphasis, with primary demand on the quadriceps of the front (planted) leg and
the gluteus maximus and hamstrings of the rear (stepping) leg during the step
and drive. Secondary trunk anti-rotation/anti-flexion demand.

**Intended muscles.**

- Front (planted) leg: quadriceps (knee extensor under load), gluteus maximus
  and hamstrings (front-hip control).
- Rear (stepping) leg: gluteus maximus and hamstrings (eccentric step-back and
  drive), gastrocnemius/soleus (rear heel lift).
- Trunk: rectus abdominis, obliques, erector spinae (upright posture and
  anti-rotation), gluteus medius (pelvic leveling).
- Supporting: tibialis anterior (front-foot control), hip abductors/adductors
  (pelvic stability).

**Common execution style.** From standing, the athlete steps one foot straight
back, landing the rear foot on the toe/ball with the heel lifted and the rear
knee bending to lower toward the floor. The front foot stays planted and the
front thigh works to control the descent. The torso stays tall. From the bottom,
the athlete drives through the front foot (and pushes off the rear toe) to step
the rear foot forward and return to standing. Breathing is typically inhale on
the step back, exhale on the drive up.

---

## 2. Pose Purpose

This specification treats the **bottom position** of the reverse lunge as the
canonical audited pose.

**Bottom position (deep lunge).** The front foot is planted flat and fixed. The
front hip and knee are flexed so the front thigh approaches horizontal (or as
deep as mobility allows) and the front shin is near-vertical. The rear foot is
planted behind on the toe/ball with the heel lifted; the rear knee is flexed and
lowered toward (but not touching) the floor, and the rear hip is extended behind
the pelvis. The torso is upright (a slight forward lean from the hip is
acceptable but minimal). Weight is carried on the front foot, with the rear toe
as a balance anchor.

**Front knee tracking.** The front knee travels forward over the front foot and
in line with the second/third toe — no valgus collapse, no varus.

**Torso.** Upright, tall through the trunk. Any forward lean is generated at the
hip, not by rounding the spine.

**Return.** The athlete drives through the front foot and pushes off the rear
toe to bring the rear foot forward to standing. The described pose is the bottom
hold.

**Distinction from forward lunge.** The foot *motion* is backward, and the rear
(stepping) leg is the one that trails and is emphasized eccentrically; the front
foot never leaves the floor and the front thigh is the prime loaded segment at
the bottom.

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment

**Center of mass.** The whole-body COM sits over the front (planted) foot. It
descends vertically as the rear foot steps back and rises on the drive out. It
must remain within the support polygon defined primarily by the front foot, with
the rear toe providing a secondary rear anchor.

**Balance.** Load is carried predominantly on the front foot. The rear toe/ball
provides balance and a small support share but is not the primary driver. The
body is balanced in the sagittal plane: no lateral tipping.

**Symmetry.** Not mirror-symmetric (split stance), but each side internally
correct. The pelvis is level (no drop) and square (facing forward, not rotated).
The shoulders are level and square to the pelvis.

**Posture.** A line from the crown of the head through the thorax and pelvis
stays upright (or leans slightly forward from the hip). The rear leg extends
straight back; the front shin is near-vertical and the front thigh approaches
horizontal at depth.

**Visual appearance.** From the side, the body shows an upright trunk over a
bent front leg, with the rear leg trailing and the rear knee lowered. From the
front, the front knee tracks over the front foot, the pelvis is level, and the
shoulders are square.

---

## 4. Head

**Neck.** The cervical spine is a neutral continuation of the thoracic spine. No
excessive flexion or extension.

**Gaze.** The eyes look forward and slightly down, toward the horizon or the
floor ahead. The gaze is level.

**Chin.** Neutral and slightly packed; ears roughly above the shoulders from the
side. The head does not jut forward.

**Cervical alignment.** The cervical curve is preserved but not exaggerated; one
gentle, unbroken line from skull through cervical and thoracic spine. No
gooseneck, no turtle tuck.

---

## 5. Spine

**Lumbar.** Neutral-to-slightly-extended. The natural lumbar arch is maintained
without excessive extension or flexion. If a slight forward trunk lean is used,
it comes from the hip, not lumbar flexion.

**Thoracic.** Neutral-to-slightly-extended. The upper back is flat and broad;
no rounding. The rib cage is stacked over the pelvis with the anterior core
engaged.

**Rib cage.** Ribs down, anterior abdominal wall braced. Chest open, not flared
or collapsed. The thoracic cage moves as a unit with the trunk.

**Pelvis.** Neutral and level. Square to the front (no rotation), level
side-to-side (no hip drop). Not in excessive anterior or posterior tilt. The
gluteals and anterior core co-contract to hold the pelvis stable and the trunk
upright.

**State summary.** Lumbar: neutral (slight extension acceptable, no flexion).
Thoracic: neutral (slight extension acceptable). Pelvis: neutral, level, square.
The spine stays long; depth is created by the legs.

---

## 6. Upper Body

**Shoulders (glenohumeral).** Level, square to the pelvis, relaxed away from the
ears. The arms hang at the sides or are held in a chosen position (hands rest on
the front thigh, held at the chest, or extend for counterbalance). The humerus
is unloaded and does not shrug.

**Scapula.** Flat on the rib cage; no winging or excessive elevation. If arms
are held up or out, the scapulae track naturally without shrugging.

**Clavicles.** Level and symmetric; do not ride up toward the neck.

**Elbows.** Free per the chosen arm style; lightly flexed if hands rest on the
thigh, straight (not locked) if extended for balance. No forced position.

**Forearms / wrists / hands.** Relaxed, not clenched (unless a load is held).
No pronation/supination deviation implying shoulder compensation.

**Note.** The upper body is postural and stabilizing; it must not collapse
forward, twist, or tilt the trunk. Any held load increases trunk-stability
demand but does not change the lower-body landmarks.

---

## 7. Lower Body

**Front hip.** Flexed so the front thigh approaches horizontal at depth. The
front hip controls the descent and drives the return. Primary loaded joint on
the planted side.

**Front knee.** Flexed to track over the front foot, in line with the
second/third toe. No valgus, no varus. Front shin near-vertical (≈0–20° from
vertical).

**Front ankle.** Dorsiflexed; the front foot flat with the heel planted. The
heel does not lift at the bottom.

**Front foot.** Flat on the floor — heel and toe both down, pointing forward (or
very slightly outward, ≤15°). Primary load-bearing contact.

**Rear hip.** Extended (rear thigh behind the pelvis, relative hyperextension).
The rear glute and hamstring control the step-back and lowering.

**Rear knee.** Flexed and lowered toward the floor; hovers just above in the
standard reverse lunge.

**Rear ankle.** Plantarflexed so the rear heel lifts; the rear foot rests on the
toe/ball, dorsal top often near the floor depending on mobility.

**Rear foot.** Rests on the toe/ball; heel raised; points straight back (or very
slightly outward). Secondary balance contact.

**Weight distribution.** Predominantly on the front foot (roughly 60–80% of
load). The rear toe provides balance only.

---

## 8. Contacts

**Front foot–floor contact (×1, primary).**

- *Contact surface:* the whole sole — heel and toe/ball — flat on the floor.
- *Expected pressure:* the primary load; heaviest under the heel/midfoot,
  distributed forward through the toe.
- *Stability:* fixed. The front foot never leaves the floor during the
  movement; it is the planted anchor and drive surface.
- *Visual appearance:* flat foot, heel down, toes forward, knee tracking over
  the toes.

**Rear foot–floor contact (×1, secondary).**

- *Contact surface:* the toe/ball region (and, per mobility, the dorsal top) of
  the foot on the floor; the heel is raised.
- *Expected pressure:* light, balance-only; not a driving load.
- *Stability:* planted as a rear anchor; the rear foot does not slide, but the
  heel is intentionally lifted.
- *Visual appearance:* rear heel up, rear foot pointing back, rear knee hovering
  near the floor.

No knee, hand, or hip contact with the floor in the canonical reverse lunge.

---

## 9. Range of Motion

**Expected ROM.**

- Front knee: flexes to roughly 90° (thigh horizontal) at a standard deep
  lunge; deeper acceptable with mobility.
- Front hip: flexes to ~90° (or more) consistent with the knee angle.
- Front ankle: dorsiflexes to keep the shin near-vertical and the heel down.
- Rear hip: extends/hyperextends as the rear leg trails; rear knee flexes as it
  lowers.
- Rear ankle: plantarflexes (heel lift).
- Spine/trunk: minimal change; stays long and upright.

**Acceptable variation.**

- Depth from thigh-parallel to slightly above/below parallel.
- Slight forward trunk lean (from the hip) up to ~20–30°.
- Front foot turned out up to ~15°.
- Rear foot turned out slightly for comfort.
- Hand position per chosen style.

**Unacceptable variation.**

- Front knee valgus or varus.
- Front heel lifting at the bottom.
- Front shin pitching far forward (excessive anterior knee translation) with
  poor ankle mobility.
- Rear knee touching the floor (standard) or rear foot sliding.
- Pelvis drop, rotation, or tilt.
- Lumbar flexion / rounded back at the bottom.
- Torso collapsing forward or twisting.
- Weight shifting predominantly onto the rear foot.
- Asymmetric shoulder tilt.

## 10. Muscle Intent

Muscles that should *visually* appear engaged:

- **Quadriceps (front leg)** — dominant knee extensor under load; visible on the
  front of the thigh at the bottom.
- **Gluteus maximus (front leg)** — hip extensor driving the return.
- **Hamstrings (front leg)** — co-contract for knee stability.
- **Gluteus maximus / hamstrings (rear leg)** — eccentric step-back control and
  drive; visible tension along the back of the rear thigh.
- **Gastrocnemius / soleus (rear leg)** — rear heel lift (plantarflexion).
- **Gluteus medius (both sides)** — pelvic leveling.
- **Rectus abdominis / obliques** — anterior trunk rigidity (upright, no
  rotation).
- **Erector spinae** — posterior trunk rigidity.
- **Tibialis anterior (front leg)** — front-foot dorsiflexion control.

The body reads as "loaded on the planted front leg, tall through the trunk, rear
leg a stable trailing anchor."

10. ✓ Rear hip extended behind the pelvis.
11. ✓ Rear foot points straight back (or slightly out).
12. ✓ Weight predominantly on the front foot.

     trailing.

## 12. Common Mistakes

**Front knee valgus (caving inward).** The front knee drops toward the midline.
*Why wrong:* medial knee-joint stress; weak glute stabilization. *Visual
difference:* front knee points inward vs the foot; ankle rolls in.

**Front heel lifting.** The front heel rises at the bottom. *Why wrong:* shifts
load forward, reduces stability, indicates poor ankle dorsiflexion. *Visual
difference:* front foot rolls onto the toe; shin pitches forward.

**Pelvis drop / rotate.** The trailing-side hip drops or the pelvis rotates.
*Why wrong:* weak gluteus medius, reduced trunk stability. *Visual difference:*
hips not level; one iliac crest lower; pelvis off-square.

**Rounded lower back.** The athlete rounds the spine to reach depth. *Why wrong:*
off-loads the legs, strains the lumbar spine. *Visual difference:* lower back
bows forward; chest drops toward the thigh.

**Excessive forward trunk lean.** The torso pitches far forward. *Why wrong:*
changes the movement into a hinge, reduces quadriceps demand. *Visual
difference:* shoulders well ahead of the front foot; steep trunk angle.

**Rear foot sliding / rear knee to floor.** The rear foot drifts or the rear
knee touches down. *Why wrong:* breaks the controlled rear-leg demand. *Visual
difference:* rear footprint shifts; rear knee contacts floor.

**Weight shifting to the rear foot.** The athlete leans back onto the rear leg.
*Why wrong:* off-loads the target front leg. *Visual difference:* COM behind the
front foot; rear heel lowers.

**Twisting torso / uneven shoulders.** Shoulders rotate or tilt. *Why wrong:*
poor anti-rotation control. *Visual difference:* shoulders not square to hips;
one shoulder drops.

## 13. Acceptance Criteria

The reverse lunge (bottom position) is **accepted only if every criterion is
satisfied**:

- [ ] Front foot flat, heel down, forward (≤15° out), never leaving the floor.
- [ ] Front knee flexed ~90° (thigh near horizontal) at depth.
- [ ] Front knee tracks over the front foot, aligned with the second/third toe.
- [ ] No front-knee valgus or varus.
- [ ] Front shin near-vertical; front heel stays on the floor.
- [ ] Rear foot planted behind on toe/ball; rear heel lifted.
- [ ] Rear knee lowered near (not touching) the floor; rear hip extended.
- [ ] Rear foot points back, not sliding.
- [ ] Weight predominantly on the front foot.
- [ ] Torso upright or slight hip-hinge lean; not collapsed forward.
- [ ] Lumbar and thoracic spines neutral; no rounding or kyphosis.
- [ ] Rib cage stacked and braced; chest open.
- [ ] Pelvis level and square; no drop or rotation.
- [ ] Neck continues the spinal line; chin neutral and packed.
- [ ] Shoulders level, square, not shrugged; scapulae flat.
- [ ] No trunk twist; shoulders and hips face the same direction.
- [ ] Gaze forward and slightly down.
- [ ] The rendered pose matches the visual checkpoints in §11.
