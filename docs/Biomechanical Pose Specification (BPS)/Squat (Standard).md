# Biomechanical Pose Specification — Squat (Standard)

## 1. Exercise overview
The standard (loaded back-squat pattern) squat describes the biomechanics of a barbell-style back
squat performed without the bar object itself. It is a closed-chain, bilateral lower-body lift in
which the torso carries an upright-ish, loaded posture and the lower body executes a hip- and
knee-driven descent to parallel or below. The defining feature versus the air squat is the more
vertical, "chest up" trunk demanded by an assumed load on the upper back, and the greater emphasis on
keeping the bar path (here, the head/shoulders) vertical.

## 2. Pose purpose
To represent the loaded squat pattern used in strength training: maximal posterior-chain and
quadriceps recruitment under an assumed axial load, with a stable, vertical bar path and a rigid
trunk. The pose prioritizes a proud chest, a near-vertical trunk, and a secure knee-over-toe track so
the assumed load is supported safely over the midfoot.

## 3. Whole-body alignment
- Feet about shoulder-width apart, toes pointed slightly outward (5–20°).
- Center of mass (and the assumed load) track vertically over the midfoot.
- Trunk is more upright than in the air squat — roughly 10–30° forward lean — to keep the assumed
  load balanced.
- Head, thoracic, and lumbar segments form a rigid, braced column.
- Pelvis level; symmetrical limb loading.

## 4. Head
- Cervical spine neutral; gaze slightly forward and down, in line with the braced trunk.
- The head does not drop or jut; it stays stacked on the rigid thoracic column as if supporting load.

## 5. Spine
- Neutral, aggressively braced lumbar and thoracic posture; natural lordosis maintained, not flattened
  or over-arched.
- Forward trunk lean is limited and originates at the hip joints, not from spinal flexion.
- The erector spinae and abdominal wall create a stiff torso to transmit the assumed axial load.

## 6. Upper body
- "Chest up": the sternum lifts and the scapulae retract slightly and depress to create a stable shelf
  for the assumed load.
- Shoulders blade down and back; upper traps elevate mildly as if anchoring a bar, without shrugging
  the neck forward.
- Arms are fixed and stable (as if gripping a bar), not swinging, supporting the rigid upper back.

## 7. Lower body
- Descent: hips push back and down while knees flex and track forward over the toes; ankle
  dorsiflexion follows.
- Knees track outward over the toes with no valgus; the femur rotates slightly externally in the
  hip socket to accommodate.
- Depth to at least parallel (hip crease at knee) or below, per standard strength prescription.
- At the bottom: deep hip and knee flexion, ankles dorsiflexed, heels flat.
- Ascent: drive through the full foot/heel; extend hips and knees together; trunk rises to near
  vertical; full lockout at the top.

## 8. Contacts
- Both feet flat on the floor, weight biased toward the midfoot and heel, with even left/right
  distribution.
- No hand or support contact; the assumed axial load is borne by the trunk, not the hands on the
  ground.
- Base of support = the two footprints.

## 9. Range of Motion
- Hip flexion: to at least ~110–130° (parallel or below).
- Knee flexion: to at least ~110–130°.
- Ankle dorsiflexion: enough to keep heels down with a moderate shin angle (~20–40° from vertical).
- Trunk lean: limited to ~10–30° from vertical (more upright than air squat).
- Full extension at the top: neutral standing.

## 10. Muscle intent
- Primary movers: gluteus maximus, hamstrings (hip extension); quadriceps (knee extension);
  soleus/gastrocnemius (top-of-stand plantarflexion).
- Trunk: erector spinae, multifidus, abdominal wall (rigid bracing under assumed load).
- Hip stabilizers: gluteus medius, gluteus minimus, deep external rotators (knee tracking, pelvic
  level).
- Upper back: middle trapezius, rhomboids, posterior deltoid, levator scapulae (shelf stability).

## 11. Visual checkpoints
- [ ] Feet shoulder-width, toes slightly out.
- [ ] Weight over midfoot/heel; no toe rise.
- [ ] Chest is proud and lifted throughout.
- [ ] Trunk near-vertical, leaning only slightly forward.
- [ ] Spine braced and neutral, no rounding.
- [ ] Shoulders down and back, stable upper-back shelf.
- [ ] Knees track over toes, no inward collapse.
- [ ] Hips drop back and down, then knees follow.
- [ ] Depth reaches at least parallel.
- [ ] Heels stay flat at the bottom.
- [ ] Head neutral, stacked on the trunk.
- [ ] Ascent drives through the heel/full foot.
- [ ] Hips and knees extend together.
- [ ] Pelvis level, no side drop.
- [ ] Full lockout at top with neutral spine.
- [ ] Arms fixed and stable (as if loaded), not swinging.
- [ ] Smooth, controlled descent and ascent.
- [ ] Load (head/shoulders) travels a near-vertical path.
- [ ] Symmetry between limbs.
- [ ] No bouncing out of the bottom.

## 12. Common mistakes
- Losing the upright chest — excessive forward lean places the assumed load off balance.
- Heels lifting — ankle restriction or weight shift forward.
- Knees caving inward under load.
- Rounding the back (loss of bracing) at depth.
- Not reaching parallel depth.
- Leading with the knees, letting them shoot forward past the toes excessively.
- Asymmetric hip drop.
- Bouncing or "good-morning" style (hip rise faster than shoulders) on the ascent.

## 13. Acceptance criteria
- [ ] Trunk stays near-vertical with chest up throughout.
- [ ] Depth reaches at least parallel with heels flat.
- [ ] Knees track over toes, no valgus.
- [ ] Spine neutral and braced.
- [ ] Weight distributed over midfoot/heel.
- [ ] Full hip/knee/ankle extension at the top.
- [ ] Assumed-load path stays vertical over the midfoot.
- [ ] Movement bilateral, symmetric, and controlled.
