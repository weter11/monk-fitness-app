# Push-Up (Decline) — Biomechanical Pose Specification

## 1. Exercise Overview

- **Movement goal:** A prone, closed-chain horizontal press performed with the feet elevated on a raised surface (box, step, bench) so the body inclines downward from the elevated feet to the floor hands; the press angle is shifted upward.
- **Training goal:** Bias the load toward the clavicular (upper) head of the pectoralis major and the anterior deltoid by increasing shoulder flexion; a progression that also lengthens the lever for greater demand.
- **Intended muscles:** Pectoralis major (clavicular/upper fibers dominant), anterior deltoid (high demand), triceps brachii, serratus anterior, rectus abdominis, transverse abdominis, gluteus maximus, quadriceps (knee hold).
- **Common execution style:** Hands on the floor roughly shoulder to 1.5× shoulder width, feet elevated on a stable box/step behind; the body forms a straight line from the elevated feet down to the hands; the head sits lower than the hips; descent/ascent driven by elbow flexion with the trunk rigid.

## 2. Pose Purpose

- **Why it exists:** The decline push-up is an incline-angle progression: elevating the feet increases shoulder flexion and shifts press torque onto the upper chest and front deltoid while lengthening the body lever (more load) relative to the standard push-up.
- **Canonical sub-poses:**
  - **Top (decline plank):** arms extended, feet elevated, body straight from elevated feet to hands, head lower than hips.
  - **Bottom (depth):** elbows flexed, chest lowered toward floor, trunk rigid.
  - **Transition:** descent/ascent with trunk shape unchanged.
- **Scapular strategy:** Protraction at bottom, retraction at top, depressed and flat; at the increased shoulder-flexion angle the scapulae sit relatively more elevated-tolerant but should still not shrug.

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment

- **Center of mass:** Projected between the floor hand base and the elevated foot contact; because the feet are high, more load shifts toward the hands/upper body than in the standard push-up.
- **Balance:** Stable, bilateral, symmetrical; requires a secure elevated foot surface.
- **Symmetry:** Mirror-symmetric; equal load both hands and both feet.
- **Posture:** Neutral spine; straight line from the elevated ankle/foot through the greater trochanter and acromion down to the hands — a downward-inclined plank with the head lower than the hips.
- **Visual appearance:** A rigid plank tilted so the feet are clearly higher than the head; from the side the body declines from the box to the floor; the upper chest and front shoulders are prominently loaded.

## 4. Head

- **Neck:** Neutral, continuation of the spine; because the head is the low point, the neck continues the downward line without craning.
- **Gaze:** At the floor a short distance ahead of the hands.
- **Chin:** Slightly tucked.
- **Cervical alignment:** Smooth single curve with the spine; no excessive flexion/extension.

## 5. Spine

- **Lumbar:** Neutral, braced.
- **Thoracic:** Neutral to slightly extended; flat.
- **Rib cage:** Closed/down.
- **Pelvis:** Neutral and level (parallel to the floor line of the plank); no anterior/posterior tilt, no rotation.
- **Summary state:** Neutral spine, isometric, unchanged top/bottom.

## 6. Upper Body

- **Shoulders (glenohumeral):** At the top the humerus is flexed more than in a standard push-up (the shoulder is more flexed because the body is inclined); at the bottom the upper arms are ~45–70° from the torso in the frontal plane and the shoulders carry greater flexion torque.
- **Scapula:** Depressed, flat, protract at bottom/retract at top; no winging.
- **Clavicles:** Level, symmetric.
- **Elbows:** Flexed to ~90° at bottom; track ~45–60° from torso; at top extended.
- **Forearms:** Vertical, wrists under elbows.
- **Wrists:** Extended, straight; load through palm.
- **Hands:** Flat, shoulder to 1.5× shoulder width on the floor; fingers forward.
- **Expected visual appearance:** Hands on floor; elbows moderately flared; shoulders broad; the upper chest clearly bears the descent.

## 7. Lower Body

- **Hip:** Extended/neutral; extensors hold the pelvis level with the inclined plank line.
- **Femur:** In line with the trunk; thighs continue the straight decline line to the elevated feet.
- **Knee:** Extended, straight, held by quadriceps.
- **Ankle:** Plantarflexed or neutral as the feet rest on the elevated surface; the foot is on top of the prop.
- **Foot:** The toes/balls (or full foot) rest on the elevated box/step; the foot is elevated, not on the floor.
- **Toe direction:** Pointed back (toward the floor/down the incline) or neutral on the prop.
- **Weight distribution:** Greater share on the hands than a standard push-up (due to the incline); symmetric.

## 8. Contacts

- **Hand contact (×2):** Flat palms/fingers on the floor; even pressure; the floor pivot. Visually: hands flat, wrists under elbows.
- **Foot contact (×2) on prop:** The balls/toes (or full foot) rest on the elevated box/step; stable, secure rear pivot. Visually: feet clearly elevated above the hands; the prop is stable.
- **(Prop):** A stable box/step/bench under the feet; it is equipment, not anatomy, but its stability is part of the pose's integrity.

## 9. Range of Motion

- **Expected ROM:** Elbow flexion top ~180° to bottom ~90°; increased shoulder flexion at top due to incline; chest descends toward floor.
- **Acceptable variation:** Chest within ~10 cm of floor; elbow 70–100°; incline stable.
- **Unacceptable variation:** Feet sliding off the prop; pelvis sagging (lumbar round) due to the harder angle; chest far from floor; unstable prop causing asymmetry.

## 10. Muscle Intent

- **Visibly engaged:** Clavicular pectoralis major (upper chest domes), anterior deltoid (high tension), triceps, serratus anterior (flat scapulae), abdominal wall (braced), gluteals (pelvis level), quadriceps (knees straight).

## 11. Visual Checkpoints

- [ ] Body one straight line from elevated feet through hip to shoulder to hands (head lower than hips).
- [ ] Feet clearly elevated on a stable prop.
- [ ] Hands shoulder to 1.5× shoulder width on the floor.
- [ ] Head neutral, gaze at floor ahead.
- [ ] Neutral spine; pelvis level (no sag, no butt-up).
- [ ] Rib cage closed.
- [ ] Scapulae flat, depressed, no winging.
- [ ] Elbows track ~45–60° from torso.
- [ ] Chest descends near floor at bottom.
- [ ] Elbow angle ~90° at bottom.
- [ ] Knees straight.
- [ ] Feet symmetric on the prop, no sliding.
- [ ] Symmetric load left/right.
- [ ] No torso rotation.
- [ ] Trunk rigid across full ROM.
- [ ] Movement controlled both directions.
- [ ] Prop stable and secure.
- [ ] Neck long.
- [ ] Wrists straight under elbows.

## 12. Common Mistakes

- **Sagging hips (lumbar round):** The harder incline causes the pelvis to drop; visually a dip at the waist. Wrong — lumbar load, lost plank.
- **Feet sliding off the prop:** Unstable surface; visually the feet slip. Wrong — loss of balance, asymmetry.
- **Butt-up:** Pelvis rises above the line; visually inverted-V. Wrong — shortens lever, removes upper-chest bias.
- **Half reps:** Chest stays high. Wrong — fails ROM.
- **Winging scapulae:** Medial borders lift. Wrong — unstable shoulder.
- **Excessive incline (too high a prop):** Shifts load so far onto the deltoid that the chest contribution vanishes and shoulder strain rises; visually a near-vertical press. Wrong for a "push-up" intent.

## 13. Acceptance Criteria

- [ ] Straight decline line from elevated feet to hands; head lower than hips.
- [ ] Feet securely on a stable prop, symmetric.
- [ ] Hands shoulder to 1.5× shoulder width; elbows track ~45–60° from torso.
- [ ] Chest descends to within ~10 cm of floor (~90° elbow).
- [ ] Neutral spine/pelvis; no sag, no butt-up.
- [ ] Knees straight.
- [ ] No torso rotation; balanced load.
- [ ] Scapulae flat/depressed, no winging/shrug.
- [ ] Full, controlled ROM both directions.
