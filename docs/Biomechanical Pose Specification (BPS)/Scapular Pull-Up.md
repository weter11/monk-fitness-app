# Scapular Pull-Up

## 1. Exercise Overview
The scapular pull-up (also called a "dead hang with scapular activation") isolates the first phase of any vertical pull: the downward and inward setting of the shoulder blades. It is performed hanging from a fixed bar with the arms essentially straight throughout—there is minimal or no elbow flexion. The body is raised a few units purely by depressing and retracting the scapulae, moving between a relaxed dead hang and an "active hang." It is a scapular-control and shoulder-health drill as much as a strength exercise.

## 2. Pose Purpose
This document specifies the biomechanics of the scapular pull-up: the oscillation between a dead hang (scapulae elevated/protracted) and an active hang (scapulae depressed/retracted) with the arms staying straight. The movement is driven entirely by the scapulothoracic muscles; the elbow angle changes only a degree or two. It trains the ability to initiate every vertical pull with the shoulder girdle, and it is the diagnostic of proper "scap first" sequencing.

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment
- The body hangs in a vertical line beneath the bar; the hands are fixed and the arms remain straight.
- The trunk is braced and neutral; a slight, controlled forward leg pendulum is permitted but must not swing the torso.
- The midline stays in a single sagittal plane; no rotation.
- The only gross movement is a small vertical rise and fall of the whole body as the scapulae set and release.

## 4. Head
- Cervical spine neutral relative to the thorax; the chin neither tucked nor jutted.
- Gaze is forward and slightly downward; it rises a few units with the body during the active-hang phase.
- The neck is relaxed and long; importantly, the upper trapezius does NOT elevate the shoulders toward the ears (the neck stays long even at the top of the shrug-free elevation).

## 5. Spine
- Thoracic and lumbar spines are neutral and static; the spine does not flex or extend during the drill.
- At the bottom (dead hang) the scapulae are elevated and protracted (relaxed).
- The active phase depresses the scapulae (downward, away from the ears) and retracts them (squeezing toward the spine), raising the body a few units. The thoracic cage rises with the girdle but the vertebral column angle is unchanged.
- The spine's only role is to stay rigid while the scapulothoracic joint does the work.

## 6. Upper Body
- **Grip:** hands fixed on the bar (overhand/neutral/underhand all valid; overhand is canonical). Full grip.
- **Shoulders (glenohumeral):** the humeri remain in near-full elevation with straight arms; the shoulder joint itself barely changes angle. All motion is at the scapulothoracic interface.
- **Elbows:** essentially straight (~170–180°) throughout; flexion is minimal (a degree or two at most) and is not the driver.
- **Wrists/hands:** fixed on the bar; wrists neutral; grip stays secure.
- **Muscles (primary):** lower trapezius and serratus anterior (scapular depression / downward rotation control), rhomboids and middle trapezius (retraction), with the upper trapezius eccentrically controlled (it must NOT simply shrug). The latissimus and biceps remain nearly quiet because the elbows do not flex.

## 7. Lower Body
- Hips and knees extended or slightly flexed; legs hang with a slight controlled forward pendulum.
- Pelvis neutral; glutes lightly engaged to keep the trunk from swaying.
- Feet relaxed, off the ground, not driving the movement.
- The lower body is a stable, non-swinging mass that merely rises and falls a few units with the scapular set.

## 8. Contacts
- **Primary (anterior/upper) contact:** both hands fixed on the horizontal bar. The hands are the only external anchor; the body rises/falls relative to them.
- **Secondary contact:** none. Feet are free.
- **Contact stability:** the grip is secure and static; hands do not slide or regrip.
- **Bottom reference:** the dead-hang extreme—arms straight, scapulae elevated/protracted, feet hanging free.

## 9. Range of Motion
- **Shoulder girdle:** full available depression and retraction at the active-hang top; full elevation and protraction at the dead-hang bottom. The vertical excursion of the body is small (a few units).
- **Glenohumeral:** near-constant high elevation; angle changes only minimally.
- **Elbow:** ~170–180° (essentially straight) at both extremes; no meaningful flexion.
- **Wrist:** neutral throughout.
- **Spine/trunk:** static neutral; no rotation.
- **Vertical travel:** small (the scapular set raises the body a few units only).

## 10. Muscle Intent
- **Depression:** lower trapezius and serratus anterior pull the scapulae down and slightly rotate them to lengthen the neck and lift the rib cage a few units.
- **Retraction:** rhomboids and middle trapezius squeeze the scapulae toward the spine, narrowing the upper back.
- **Control:** the upper trapezius is inhibited from shrugging; the latissimus and biceps stay quiet because the elbows remain straight.
- **Stabilization:** grip and forearm flexors hold the bar; the trunk and scapular stabilizers keep the body rigid during the small rise/fall.

## 11. Visual Checkpoints
- [ ] ✓ Hands fixed on the bar with a secure grip.
- [ ] ✓ Arms essentially straight throughout (no meaningful elbow flexion).
- [ ] ✓ At the bottom (dead hang) the scapulae are elevated and protracted (relaxed shoulders).
- [ ] ✓ The active phase depresses the scapulae—pulling them down away from the ears (neck stays long, no shrug).
- [ ] ✓ The active phase also retracts the scapulae—squeezing them toward the spine.
- [ ] ✓ The body rises only a few units; the movement is small and controlled.
- [ ] ✓ The trunk stays rigid and neutral; no swing, no lumbar arch.
- [ ] ✓ The head is neutral; neck long, not jutted or bundled.
- [ ] ✓ The legs hang with a slight controlled forward pendulum.
- [ ] ✓ Gaze forward/down, rising slightly with the body.
- [ ] ✓ The motion is purely scapulothoracic—the elbows do not bend to "cheat" the rise.
- [ ] ✓ The drill oscillates smoothly between dead hang and active hang.
- [ ] ✓ No thoracic rotation; the body stays in one sagittal plane.
- [ ] ✓ The grip is maintained without sliding or regripping.
- [ ] ✓ Breathing continues calmly through the oscillation.

## 12. Common Mistakes
- **Shrugging:** using the upper trapezius to hike the shoulders toward the ears instead of depressing them—this is the opposite of the intended motion.
- **Bending the elbows:** turning the drill into a mini pull-up by flexing the elbows, which removes the scapular isolation.
- **Swinging:** pumping the legs to create motion instead of controlling the scapulae.
- **Protracting only / no retraction:** only depressing without squeezing the blades together, missing half the scapular set.
- **Arching the low back:** losing trunk rigidity during the small rise.
- **Exaggerating the range:** trying to lift the body too far (which forces elbow flexion), rather than accepting the small few-unit scapular rise.

## 13. Acceptance Criteria
- [ ] The hands are fixed on the bar with a secure grip.
- [ ] The arms remain essentially straight (no meaningful elbow flexion) at both extremes.
- [ ] At the bottom the scapulae are elevated/protracted (dead hang).
- [ ] The active phase depresses AND retracts the scapulae (active hang), with no upper-trap shrug.
- [ ] The body rises only a few units; the motion is small and controlled.
- [ ] The trunk remains rigid and neutral; no swing, no lumbar arch.
- [ ] The feet hang free with at most a slight controlled forward pendulum.
- [ ] The drill oscillates smoothly between dead hang and active hang with continued breathing.
