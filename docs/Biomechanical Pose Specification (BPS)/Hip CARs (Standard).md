# Hip CARs (Standard)

## 1. Exercise Overview
Hip CARs (Controlled Articular Rotations) are a joint-mobility drill in which one hip is taken through its full, slow, controlled circumduction — a continuous circle through hip flexion, abduction, extension, and adduction — while the standing leg and trunk remain perfectly still. CARs are a staple of Functional Range Conditioning: the intent is to explore and own the ENTIRE articular range of the hip joint under control, not to build strength or cardio. The canonical sub-poses are the hip at full flexion (the knee driven up high, thigh vertical) and full extension (the leg swept back behind the body). The movement is performed standing on one leg.

## 2. Pose Purpose
- Explore and maintain the full articular range of motion of the hip joint.
- Improve neuromuscular control at the end ranges of hip flexion, extension, abduction, and adduction.
- Train trunk and standing-leg stability while the moving leg rotates.
- Identify and gradually expand restricted arcs of hip motion.
- Serve as a joint "warm-up" / mobility investment before training.

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment
- Stand on one leg (the stance leg), the other leg is the moving limb.
- The trunk is tall, upright, and braced — it does NOT lean, rotate, or tilt to compensate for the moving leg.
- The moving leg traces a large, slow circle; the motion is at the hip joint of the moving leg.
- The stance foot is planted; the pelvis stays level (no hip drop on the moving side).
- The rotation is slow (several seconds per circle) and deliberate.

## 4. Head
- Head is in neutral, upright alignment with the spine.
- The cervical spine is tall; the chin is level.
- The head does not tilt or rotate as the leg sweeps; it stays centered over the stance foot.

## 5. Spine
- **Whole spine:** neutral and upright, lightly braced — a stable pillar.
- No flexion, extension, rotation, or lateral lean to "help" the moving leg.
- The lumbar and thoracic stay still; the pelvis stays level (no anterior/posterior tilt or drop).

## 6. Upper Body
- Arms may be held out to the sides or in front for balance, or on the hips.
- The shoulders are level and down, not hiking on the moving-leg side.
- The arms assist balance only; they do not pull the trunk into rotation.
- The chest stays open and the upper back long.

## 7. Lower Body
- **Stance leg:** hip, knee, and ankle stable; the glute and quadriceps lightly engage to keep the leg straight and the pelvis level. The knee may be very slightly soft.
- **Moving leg:** the femur rotates in the hip socket through the full circle:
  - **Full flexion (canonical):** thigh drives up toward the chest, knee bent or straight, hip flexed to its end range.
  - **Abduction:** leg sweeps out to the side.
  - **Extension (canonical):** leg sweeps back behind the body, hip extended to end range.
  - **Adduction:** leg returns across/back to center.
- The knee of the moving leg may stay extended (harder) or slightly bent (easier); consensus: keep it as extended as control allows to load the true hip articulation.
- The pelvis stays level — the stance hip does NOT drop.

## 8. Contacts
- The stance foot on the floor (the only lower-body contact).
- No hand/wall contact is required (though a finger-tip touch for balance is an acceptable regression).
- The moving foot is off the floor throughout the circle.
- The exercise is freestanding; no external prop needed.

## 9. Range of Motion
- Hip flexion: to end range (thigh toward chest) — canonical sub-pose.
- Hip extension: to end range (leg behind body) — canonical sub-pose.
- Hip abduction: to end range (leg out to the side).
- Hip adduction: returning across to neutral/front.
- The full circle is one continuous controlled rep; typically 3–5 slow reps per side. The range is the honest articular limit, not forced.

## 10. Muscle Intent
- Moving-leg hip flexors (iliopsoas, rectus femoris): drive and control flexion.
- Moving-leg glutes/hamstrings: drive and control extension.
- Moving-leg abductors (gluteus medius/minimus) and adductors: control the side arcs.
- Stance-leg glute medius and quadriceps: keep the pelvis level and the leg stable.
- Core/erectors: keep the trunk upright and still against the moving limb.

## 11. Visual Checkpoints
- [ ] The athlete stands on one stable leg; the other leg is the mover.
- [ ] The trunk is tall and upright, not leaning or rotating.
- [ ] The pelvis stays level — no drop on the moving-leg side.
- [ ] The moving leg traces a large, slow, continuous circle.
- [ ] At full flexion the thigh is driven up high (knee to chest) — canonical sub-pose.
- [ ] At full extension the leg is swept clearly behind the body — canonical sub-pose.
- [ ] The head stays neutral and centered over the stance foot.
- [ ] The shoulders are level, not hiking.
- [ ] The stance knee is stable (slightly soft, not buckling).
- [ ] The moving knee stays extended or only slightly bent (true hip articulation).
- [ ] The movement is slow and controlled, not swinging with momentum.
- [ ] Both sides perform the same arc (symmetry of range).
- [ ] Breathing is calm through the slow circle.
- [ ] No torso sway or balance grab compensations.
- [ ] The circle is continuous through all four directions (flex/abduct/extend/adduct).

## 12. Common Mistakes
- Leaning the trunk or rotating the pelvis to "reach" the leg higher.
- Dropping the stance-side hip (Trendelenburg-like compensation).
- Swinging the leg with momentum instead of controlled articulation.
- Bending the moving knee excessively (offloading the hip joint).
- Letting the head tilt or the shoulders hike.
- Short-cutting the circle (not reaching true flexion/extension/abduction).
- Using a wall/arm to pull the body out of alignment.

## 13. Acceptance Criteria
- [ ] One leg stands stable while the other traces a slow, continuous full circle through hip flexion, abduction, extension, and adduction.
- [ ] The trunk and pelvis stay level and upright with the head neutral and centered.
- [ ] The canonical end ranges (full hip flexion and full hip extension) are clearly reached under control.
- [ ] The movement is slow, momentum-free, and symmetrical side-to-side with calm breathing.
- [ ] No trunk lean, pelvic drop, or balance-grab compensations are present.
