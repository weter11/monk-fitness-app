# Reverse Snow Angel (Prone) — Biomechanical Pose Specification

## 1. Exercise Overview
The Reverse Snow Angel is a prone (face-down) scapular and thoracic exercise. Lying face down with the arms out to the sides at shoulder height (the "snow angel" start position), the individual slides the arms overhead (up and back along the floor) and returns them, mimicking making snow angels on the ground. The focus is lower-trapezius strength and scapular depression with external rotation of the shoulders, performed while maintaining thoracic extension and a neutral neck. It reinforces posterior shoulder health and counters rounded-shoulder posture.

Populations: shoulder health, posture correction, warm-ups, and upper-back strengthening. No equipment required (a thin mat is typical).

## 2. Pose Purpose
- Strengthen the lower trapezius and serratus anterior for scapular depression and upward rotation.
- Train shoulder external rotation in the prone, arms-abducted position (posterior shoulder integrity).
- Maintain thoracic extension while the arms move (anti-protraction of the chest).
- Improve scapular control without neck strain or upper-trapezius dominance.

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment
- Prone on the floor: chest, abdomen, pelvis, and thighs in contact with the ground.
- Start: arms abducted to ~90° (out to the sides at shoulder height), elbows extended, palms facing down or thumb-up; the arms lie along the floor.
- Motion: the arms slide overhead (toward the head) and back down to the side in a sweeping arc along the floor — the "angel" pattern — repeated as reps.
- The thoracic spine stays extended (chest gently lifted / long through the upper back) throughout; the low back is neutral.
- The movement is symmetric: both arms sweep equally; no rotation or side-bend of the trunk.
- The head and neck stay neutral and off strain; the pelvis stays grounded.

## 4. Head
- Head rests on the floor or is lifted just slightly, gaze directed down toward the floor (not craned to the side or up).
- The cervical spine is in neutral/gentle extension consistent with the thoracic lift; no sharp neck angle.
- The neck does not rotate or side-bend; the chin is not jutted.

## 5. Spine
- Thoracic spine maintained in gentle extension: the chest is long and open, the upper back does not round (no protraction hump).
- Lumbar spine neutral (natural lordosis), pelvis neutral and grounded — no arching or tucking.
- No rotation and no lateral flexion of the trunk during the arm sweeps.
- The extension is a maintained posture, not a dynamic arch; the spine stays steady while the arms move.
- Consensus: a "long spine" cue keeps the thoracic extension subtle and sustainable without rib-flare.

## 6. Upper Body
- Start (arms abducted 90°): shoulders in abduction with the scapulae gently depressed (down, not shrugged); the arms lie along the floor.
- During the sweep overhead: the arms slide along the floor in an arc; the shoulders externally rotate (thumbs rotate up/toward the ceiling) so the movement is driven by the posterior cuff and lower trapezius, not the anterior deltoid.
- Scapulae depress and slightly retract/upwardly rotate as the arms reach overhead; the lower trapezius keeps the shoulder blades pinned down (no elevation toward the ears).
- The elbows stay extended; the arms return to the abducted start under control.
- No upper-trapezius shrug; the neck stays relaxed.

## 7. Lower Body
- Legs extended straight back along the floor, knees extended, feet relaxed or gently pointed.
- The pelvis stays grounded and neutral; the glutes may be gently engaged to keep the thighs down and the pelvis stable.
- The lower body is passive support: it does not lift; the exercise isolates the upper-back/scapular region.
- No hip movement or pelvic tilt during the reps.

## 8. Contacts
- Anterior contacts: chest, abdomen, pelvis, and anterior thighs rest on the floor (prone fulcrum), same as the Superman base.
- The arms move along the floor (sliding contact) rather than lifting away from it — the "angel" is drawn on the ground.
- The head rests on the floor (or just above it); the neck is unloaded.
- No part of the trunk lifts; the only motion is the arms sliding along the floor surface.

## 9. Range of Motion
- Shoulder abduction: from ~90° (arms to the sides) sweeping toward ~150–180° overhead (arms up by the head) and back.
- Shoulder external rotation: maintained/subtly increased through the sweep (thumbs up) to engage the posterior cuff.
- Scapular depression/retraction: the primary controlled ROM — the shoulder blades slide down and together as the arms reach overhead.
- Thoracic extension: a maintained posture (small ROM), held isometrically.
- Elbow: extended (~0°) throughout.
- Neck/cervical: neutral, minimal motion.
- The arm sweep is the moving ROM; the spine is steady.

## 10. Muscle Intent
- Lower trapezius: primary scapular depressor/upward rotator driving the arm sweep.
- Serratus anterior: assists scapular upward rotation and keeps the scapulae flat (no winging).
- Posterior deltoid and infraspinatus/teres minor (rotator cuff): shoulder external rotation and the overhead sweep.
- Middle trapezius: scapular retraction supporting the depressed position.
- Erector spinae (thoracic): maintain the gentle thoracic extension.
- Rhomboids: assist scapular setting.
- Upper trapezius: intentionally quiet (avoided) to prevent neck strain/shrug.

## 11. Visual Checkpoints
- [ ] Prone, chest and pelvis on the floor.
- [ ] Arms start abducted ~90° at shoulder height, along the floor.
- [ ] Arms sweep overhead in an arc along the floor (snow-angel pattern).
- [ ] Both arms sweep symmetrically.
- [ ] Shoulders externally rotate (thumbs up) during the sweep.
- [ ] Scapulae depressed (pinned down, no shrug).
- [ ] No upper-trapezius elevation toward the ears.
- [ ] Thoracic spine stays extended (chest long, no rounding).
- [ ] Lumbar spine neutral, pelvis grounded.
- [ ] Head neutral, gaze down, no neck strain.
- [ ] Elbows extended throughout.
- [ ] Arms slide along the floor, not lifted away.
- [ ] No rotation or side-bend of the trunk.
- [ ] Lower body stays still and grounded.
- [ ] Neck relaxed (no tensing).
- [ ] Return sweep controlled to the start position.
- [ ] Breathing steady (no breath-hold).
- [ ] Both sides work evenly.
- [ ] No rib-flare / excessive arch.
- [ ] Reps are smooth, not jerky.
- [ ] Scapulae do not wing during the sweep.

## 12. Common Mistakes
- Shrugging the shoulders toward the ears (upper-trapezius dominance) instead of depressing the scapulae.
- letting the thoracic spine round (protracted chest hump) instead of staying extended.
- Lifting the arms off the floor (turning it into a raise rather than a slide).
- Internally rotating the shoulders (thumbs down) which stresses the anterior cuff.
- Craning or straining the neck to "help" the arm sweep.
- Letting the lower back arch or the pelvis tilt.
- Asymmetric sweeping (one arm reaching further).
- Holding the breath.
- Jerky, momentum-driven sweeps instead of controlled sliding.
- Winging the scapulae (loss of serratus control).

## 13. Acceptance Criteria
- [ ] Prone with chest, abdomen, and pelvis grounded; arms abducted ~90° at the start.
- [ ] Arms sweep symmetrically overhead along the floor in a snow-angel arc and return.
- [ ] Shoulders maintain external rotation and scapular depression (no upper-trapezius shrug, no winging).
- [ ] Thoracic spine stays extended and lumbar/pelvis neutral with no trunk rotation or side-bend.
- [ ] Head and neck neutral with no strain; movement controlled with steady respiration for the prescribed reps.
