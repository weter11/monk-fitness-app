# Push-Up (Pike) — Biomechanical Pose Specification

## 1. Exercise Overview

- **Movement goal:** A prone, closed-chain press performed from a piked hip position: the hips are driven high so the body forms an inverted "V", with hands and feet on the floor; the descent brings the head/shoulders down toward the floor between the hands while the hips remain elevated.
- **Training goal:** Bias the load toward the anterior deltoid and the shoulder flexors/vertical-press pattern (often called a "shoulder press push-up"); trains shoulder flexion strength and trunk hip-hinge control.
- **Intended muscles:** Anterior deltoid (dominant), pectoralis major (upper/clavicular), triceps brachii, upper trapezius/levator scapulae (assistive elevation control), erector spinae (hip hinge hold), hamstrings (hip flexion/extension hold), gluteus maximus, gastrocnemius/soleus (ankle plantarflexion), rectus abdominis.
- **Common execution style:** From a downward dog–like start, hands and feet on the floor, hips lifted high so the torso and legs form an inverted V with the hips as the apex; the elbows flex to lower the head/shoulders toward the floor between the hands, then extend to press back up, keeping the hips high throughout.

## 2. Pose Purpose

- **Why it exists:** The pike push-up uses a hip-hinged (inverted-V) body geometry to redirect the press into a largely vertical, shoulder-dominant pattern — a bodyweight proxy for overhead pressing — while the hips stay elevated rather than in a flat plank.
- **Canonical sub-poses:**
  - **Top (pike):** hips high, body inverted-V, arms and legs straight, head between the arms.
  - **Bottom (depth):** elbows flexed, head/shoulders lowered toward the floor between the hands, hips still elevated.
  - **Transition:** descent/ascent by elbow flexion/extension with the hip apex maintained.
- **Scapular strategy:** At the bottom the scapulae elevate and protract as the head dips (shoulder-flexion press pattern); at the top they depress and retract; the scapulae may elevate more than in a flat push-up because the movement resembles an overhead press.

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment

- **Center of mass:** Positioned beneath the hip apex, shared between the hand base and the foot base; it shifts forward toward the hands as the head descends.
- **Balance:** Stable, bilateral, symmetrical; the wide hand–foot base supports the inverted V.
- **Symmetry:** Mirror-symmetric; equal load both hands and both feet.
- **Posture:** The spine is in two straight segments meeting at the high hip apex — a straight torso segment (shoulders to hips) and a straight leg segment (hips to ankles) — forming an inverted V; the head is the low point between the arms.
- **Visual appearance:** A clear inverted-V (^) silhouette with the hips as the highest point; the arms and legs are straight at the top; on descent the shoulders dip toward the floor while the hips stay up.

## 4. Head

- **Neck:** Cervical spine in a neutral-to-slightly-flexed position relative to the inclined torso; the head sits between the arms, not craned up or dropped.
- **Gaze:** At the floor between or just ahead of the hands.
- **Chin:** Slightly tucked so the head clears the floor on descent.
- **Cervical alignment:** Smooth continuation of the inclined torso line; no sharp kink.

## 5. Spine

- **Lumbar:** Neutral within the inclined torso segment; braced, not rounded or arched as the hips hinge.
- **Thoracic:** Neutral to slightly extended along the inclined segment; flat upper back.
- **Rib cage:** Closed/down.
- **Pelvis:** Positioned at the apex of the inverted V; the pelvis is lifted high and level (no rotation, no anterior/posterior tilt relative to the torso line).
- **Summary state:** A hip-hinged (piked) posture with a neutral spine along both straight segments; the spine does not round during the descent.

## 6. Upper Body

- **Shoulders (glenohumeral):** Heavily flexed — at the top the arms are angled steeply (the shoulders are well above the hands); at the bottom the shoulders flex further as the head dips, approaching a vertical press. This is the dominant shoulder-flexion pattern.
- **Scapula:** Elevate and protract at the bottom (overhead-press-like), depress and retract at the top; not winged.
- **Clavicles:** Symmetric; may elevate slightly with the shoulder press at the bottom.
- **Elbows:** Flexed at the bottom (~70–100°) to lower the head; at the top extended. They track outward modestly (~30–60° from torso) as the head dips between the hands.
- **Forearms:** Vertical-ish under the shoulders at the top; angle slightly as the head descends.
- **Wrists:** Extended, straight; load through palm.
- **Hands:** Flat on the floor, roughly shoulder-width, fingers forward, placed so the head can descend between them.
- **Expected visual appearance:** Shoulders broad and high; on descent the head lowers between the hands while the hips stay up; the press reads as "upside-down shoulder press."

## 7. Lower Body

- **Hip:** Flexed at the apex of the inverted V (the torso–leg angle is acute, ~60–90°); the hip extensors (gluteals, hamstrings) hold the hinge position isometrically.
- **Femur:** Along the straight leg segment descending from the hip apex to the feet; angled steeply relative to the floor.
- **Knee:** Extended, straight (legs straight) — the pike is a hip hinge, not a knee bend.
- **Ankle:** Plantarflexed so the heels may be lifted or the feet flat with weight through the balls; the calves are engaged.
- **Foot:** Flat or on the balls; stable on the floor.
- **Toe direction:** Pointed straight forward (toward the hands) or natural; no rotation.
- **Weight distribution:** Shared between hands and feet; shifts toward the hands as the head descends; symmetric.

## 8. Contacts

- **Hand contact (×2):** Flat palms/fingers on the floor; stable base for the inverted V; pressure increases as the head descends. Visually: hands flat, under/near the shoulders, head descends between them.
- **Foot contact (×2):** Flat feet or balls of feet on the floor at the rear base of the inverted V; stable. Visually: feet planted, legs straight up to the hip apex.

## 9. Range of Motion

- **Expected ROM:** Shoulder flexion from the high pike to the head-near-floor bottom; elbow flexion ~180° top to ~70–100° bottom; hip stays hinged (minimal change at the hip); the descent is primarily at the shoulder/elbow, not the hip.
- **Acceptable variation:** Head descends to within a few cm of the floor (or the crown approaches it) while hips stay high; elbow 70–100°.
- **Unacceptable variation:** Hips dropping to flatten the body (loses the pike — becomes a standard push-up); knees bending; head far from floor (no depth); asymmetrical hip apex.

## 10. Muscle Intent

- **Visibly engaged:** Anterior deltoid (dominant, domes as the head dips), upper pectoralis (assist), triceps (extension), upper trapezius/levator (controlled scapular elevation), erector spinae and gluteals/hamstrings (hold the hip hinge), calves (ankle plantarflexion), abdominals (brace).

## 11. Visual Checkpoints

- [ ] Body forms an inverted V with the hips as the highest apex.
- [ ] Hips stay elevated throughout (do not drop to flat).
- [ ] Legs straight (knees extended), not bent.
- [ ] Head descends toward the floor BETWEEN the hands on the bottom.
- [ ] Shoulders heavily flexed (steep arm angle).
- [ ] Elbows flex ~70–100° at bottom; extend at top.
- [ ] Hands flat, roughly shoulder-width.
- [ ] Feet flat/ball contact, symmetric, no rotation.
- [ ] Head neutral, gaze at floor, chin tucked.
- [ ] Spine neutral along both straight segments; no rounding.
- [ ] Rib cage closed.
- [ ] Scapulae not winged; elevate/protract at bottom, depress at top.
- [ ] Pelvis level, no rotation.
- [ ] Symmetric load left/right.
- [ ] No torso rotation.
- [ ] Movement controlled both directions.
- [ ] Neck long, no kink.
- [ ] Wrists straight.
- [ ] Calf/ankle engaged, heels lifted or firm.

## 12. Common Mistakes

- **Hips dropping (losing the pike):** The apex falls so the body flattens toward a standard push-up; visually the inverted V collapses. Wrong because it removes the shoulder-press bias and becomes a different exercise.
- **Knee bending:** The hinge shifts to the knees; visually bent legs. Wrong — the pike is a hip hinge; bent knees shorten the lever and change the pattern.
- **Insufficient depth:** Head stays far above the floor; visually a shallow dip. Wrong — fails ROM.
- **Walking the feet/hands asymmetrically:** The apex shifts; visually uneven V. Wrong — asymmetric load.
- **Winging scapulae:** Medial borders lift at the bottom. Wrong — unstable shoulder.
- **Neck kink (head craned up):** The cervicals break from the torso line; visually a sharp neck angle. Wrong — cervical strain.

## 13. Acceptance Criteria

- [ ] Inverted-V posture with hips as the apex, maintained throughout.
- [ ] Legs straight (knees extended); hips do not drop to flat.
- [ ] Head descends toward the floor between the hands (~70–100° elbow).
- [ ] Shoulders in heavy flexion; elbows track modestly outward.
- [ ] Hands flat, feet stable, symmetric, no rotation.
- [ ] Neutral spine along both segments; head neutral, chin tucked.
- [ ] Pelvis level; no torso rotation; balanced load.
- [ ] Scapulae not winged; full, controlled ROM both directions.
