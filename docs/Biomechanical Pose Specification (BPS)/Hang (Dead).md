# Hang (Dead)

## 1. Exercise Overview
The dead hang is a static, closed-chain holding position in which the body is fully suspended from a fixed horizontal bar by the hands, with the arms extended and no pulling effort beyond what is needed to maintain the grip and a safe shoulder position. It is the bottom reference position for all vertical pulls and an isometric scapular/shoulder-girdle exercise in its own right. It trains grip endurance, passive and active shoulder depression, and trunk control under suspension.

## 2. Pose Purpose
This document specifies the biomechanics of a stationary dead hang: the body hangs with the arms fully extended, the shoulders actively depressed (not shrugged), and the feet free below. There is no elbow flexion and no upward pull. A gentle breathing sway and a slight forward pendulum of the legs are permitted and natural. The dead hang is the loading and alignment baseline from which every pull-up/chin-up repetition begins.

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment
- The body hangs in a vertical line beneath the bar; the hands are fixed and the arms are straight.
- The trunk is relaxed but not collapsed: the abdominal wall has gentle tone to prevent excessive lumbar sway, and the pelvis is near neutral.
- A slight forward pendulum of the legs (and a correspondingly slight posterior counter-swing) is natural and permitted; it should remain small and not become a swing.
- The midline stays close to a single sagittal plane; no thoracic rotation.

## 4. Head
- Cervical spine neutral relative to the thorax; the chin is neither aggressively tucked nor jutted.
- Gaze is forward and slightly downward (toward the floor or bar base).
- The neck is relaxed and long; there is no shrugging of the upper trapezius toward the ears.

## 5. Spine
- The thoracic spine is in its natural, relaxed kyphosis; the lumbar spine is in a neutral-to-slightly-relaxed position (a small amount of lordosis is acceptable in a passive hang, but the trunk should not be actively arched or rounded).
- The scapulae are in a depressed and protracted (or neutrally placed) position appropriate to a relaxed hang—importantly, the shoulders are actively depressed (pulled down away from the ears) to protect the glenohumeral joint and lengthen the neck, NOT elevated/shrugged.
- There is no spinal movement in a static hang; the emphasis is on maintaining a safe, long shoulder girdle.

## 6. Upper Body
- **Grip:** the hands are fixed on the bar. The grip style (overhand/neutral/underhand) is exercise-dependent, but for the canonical dead hang an overhand (pronated) or neutral full grip is typical; the grip must be secure.
- **Shoulders (glenohumeral):** the humeri hang in near-full elevation (~160–180°) with the arms straight. The shoulder girdle is actively depressed (scapulae down, away from the ears) and may be slightly protracted.
- **Elbows:** fully extended (~175–180°); there is no elbow flexion in a dead hang.
- **Wrists/hands:** fixed on the bar; wrists neutral. The grip muscles (forearm flexors) are isometrically engaged to maintain the hold.
- **Muscles:** the primary active role is the lower trapezius and serratus anterior (scapular depression), the rotator-cuff and scapular stabilizers (glenohumeral centration), and the forearm/grip flexors (holding the bar). The lats and biceps are relaxed or only lightly engaged.

## 7. Lower Body
- Hips and knees extended or very slightly flexed; the legs hang and may show a slight, natural forward pendulum.
- The pelvis is near neutral; the glutes are relaxed or lightly engaged to avoid excessive sway.
- The feet are relaxed and plantarflexed naturally; they do not touch the ground.
- The lower body's role is to act as a passive, stable mass; the slight leg pendulum is a natural balance response, not a driving motion.

## 8. Contacts
- **Primary (anterior/upper) contact:** both hands fixed on the horizontal bar. The hands are the only external anchor; the rest of the body is suspended freely.
- **Secondary contact:** none. The feet are free and off the ground.
- **Contact stability:** the grip is secure and static; the hands do not slide or regrip during the hold.
- **Bottom reference:** the dead hang IS the bottom reference position—arms fully extended, shoulders depressed, feet hanging free.

## 9. Range of Motion
- **Shoulder girdle:** depressed and (slightly) protracted at rest; the full comfortable depression is maintained.
- **Glenohumeral:** near-full elevation (~160–180°) with straight arms.
- **Elbow:** fully extended (~175–180°); no flexion.
- **Wrist:** neutral (~0–20° extension).
- **Spine/trunk:** maintained within a relaxed neutral range; small breathing/sway motion permitted.
- **Legs:** small forward pendulum permitted (a few degrees of hip/knee motion); not a swing.

## 10. Muscle Intent
- **Active depression:** lower trapezius and serratus anterior pull the scapulae down (away from the ears) to lengthen the neck and center the humeral heads—this is the key "active" component of an otherwise passive hang.
- **Grip/hold:** forearm flexors and grip muscles maintain the fixed hand contact isometrically.
- **Stabilization:** rotator cuff and scapular stabilizers center the glenohumeral joints; light abdominal tone prevents excessive lumbar sway; the grip and shoulder depressors sustain the position against gravity.

## 11. Visual Checkpoints
- [ ] ✓ Hands fixed on the bar; grip secure (full grip recommended).
- [ ] ✓ Arms fully extended (elbows ~straight); no elbow flexion.
- [ ] ✓ Shoulders depressed—pulled down away from the ears, NOT shrugged up.
- [ ] ✓ The neck looks long; the trapezius is not bundled toward the ears.
- [ ] ✓ The body hangs in a vertical line; the trunk is not collapsed or aggressively arched.
- [ ] ✓ The head is neutral, gaze forward/down.
- [ ] ✓ A gentle breathing sway is present but small.
- [ ] ✓ The legs show a slight, natural forward pendulum (not a swing).
- [ ] ✓ The feet are free and off the ground.
- [ ] ✓ The pelvis is near neutral, not sharply anteriorly tilted.
- [ ] ✓ The grip is maintained without sliding or regripping.
- [ ] ✓ No thoracic rotation; the body stays in one sagittal plane.
- [ ] ✓ The position is sustainable (breathing continues).
- [ ] ✓ The scapulae are set down before any pull begins from this position.

## 12. Common Mistakes
- **Shrugging (elevated scapulae):** letting the upper traps pull the shoulders toward the ears—this compresses the neck, stresses the shoulder, and indicates a loss of active depression.
- **Dead weight collapse:** fully relaxing the trunk into excessive lumbar sway or a rounded upper back, rather than maintaining light tone.
- **Swinging:** turning the hang into a pendulum by pumping the legs, which defeats the static control intent.
- **False/grip loss:** a thumbless "false" grip or slipping hands; a full secure grip is safer for a sustained hold.
- **Overarching:** actively arching the low back instead of hanging relaxed-neutral.
- **Holding the breath:** failing to keep a calm breathing rhythm during the hold.

## 13. Acceptance Criteria
- [ ] The hands are fixed on the bar with a secure grip.
- [ ] The arms are fully extended with no elbow flexion.
- [ ] The shoulders are actively depressed (not shrugged toward the ears).
- [ ] The body hangs in a vertical line; the trunk is relaxed-neutral with light tone.
- [ ] The head is neutral and the neck is long.
- [ ] The feet are free and off the ground.
- [ ] At most a small, natural forward leg pendulum and breathing sway are present (no swing).
- [ ] The position is held statically with continued breathing.
