# Superman (Prone) — Biomechanical Pose Specification

## 1. Exercise Overview
The Superman is a prone (face-down) posterior-chain exercise. Lying flat on the floor, the individual simultaneously lifts the chest, arms, and legs off the ground, forming a gentle bow shape with the body. It targets spinal extension (erector spinae), the gluteals, and the hamstrings, and reinforces the lower trapezius and posterior shoulder musculature. The lift is a controlled hover — a strong but restrained arch — not a violent or maximal backbend.

Populations: general fitness, rehabilitation progressions for posterior-chain activation, and beginner spinal-extension work. No equipment required.

## 2. Pose Purpose
- Strengthen the erector spinae and the entire posterior chain in spinal extension.
- Activate the gluteus maximus and hamstrings as hip extensors that support the lifted legs.
- Develop lower-trapezius and posterior-deltoid / rhomboid control for the lifted arm position.
- Teach a controlled, symmetric extension "bow" rather than a compressive, end-range backbend.

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment
- Prone on the floor: pelvis, abdomen, and thighs rest on the ground at the start.
- At the lift, the chest and head rise off the floor, the arms extend forward (or out front) and lift, and the legs extend back and lift — the body forms a shallow, symmetric bow.
- The line of the bow is gentle: the lifted segments clear the floor but the spine is not cranked into maximal extension.
- The movement is symmetric about the midline: right and left sides lift equally; no rotation or side-bend.
- The pelvis stays in contact with the floor (the fulcrum of the bow is the hips/pelvis).

## 4. Head
- Head is a neutral extension of the cervical spine: lifted just off the floor, gaze directed down and slightly forward (not craned up).
- The neck is in gentle extension consistent with the thoracic lift — not over-extended (no "looking at the ceiling" strain).
- The chin is not jutted forward; the cervical curve flows smoothly into the thoracic extension.

## 5. Spine
- Spinal extension concentrated through the thoracic and lumbar regions: the chest lifts, the low back gently arches.
- Extension is moderate and symmetric — the goal is a rounded "bow", not end-range lumbar hyperextension.
- No rotation and no lateral flexion; the spine extends as a single symmetric arch.
- The pelvis remains neutral (not posteriorly tilted/tucked) and stays on the floor; the extension originates from the paraspinals, not from tilting the pelvis.
- Consensus note: some coaching cues emphasize lifting only to where the navel stays soft (avoiding lumbar compression); alternatives allow a slightly higher lift but warn against rib-flare and pelvic tilt.

## 6. Upper Body
- Arms extended forward (overhead, along the floor line) or held out front; they lift off the floor together with the chest.
- Shoulders in flexion (~120–180°) and slight scapular retraction; the lower trapezius and rhomboids depress and retract the scapulae (no elevation/shrug).
- Posterior deltoids assist the lift; the arms are not swung but held steadily.
- The chest (sternum) lifts; the shoulder girdle stays down (no upward shrug toward the ears).

## 7. Lower Body
- Legs extended straight back and lifted off the floor together; knees are extended, not bent.
- Hip extension is driven by the gluteus maximus; the hamstrings assist in maintaining the lifted, extended position.
- The thighs lift as a unit; the inner thighs stay engaged (gentle adduction) to avoid splaying.
- The pelvis remains grounded and neutral; the lift comes from the hips/glutes extending the legs, not from pelvic tilt.
- Feet are relaxed or gently pointed; no excessive plantarflexion strain.

## 8. Contacts
- The pelvic region, lower abdomen, and the anterior thighs (quadriceps/patellar region) remain in contact with the floor — these are the ground fulcrum.
- The chest, head, arms, and legs are OFF the floor during the hold.
- The contact surface is the front of the body (anterior), unlike supine exercises; load is distributed along the lower trunk and thighs.

## 9. Range of Motion
- Spinal extension: moderate (thoracic + lumbar), short of end-range; the chest clears the floor by a few centimeters.
- Cervical extension: ~20–30° to lift the head in line with the thoracic curve.
- Shoulder flexion (arms forward): ~150–180°; arms lifted a few centimeters off the floor.
- Hip extension (legs back): the thighs lift off the floor, knees extended; roughly 10–20° of measurable lift from the prone line.
- Knee: extended (~0°) during the lift; no flexion.
- The pose is a hover: ranges are modest and held isometrically.

## 10. Muscle Intent
- Erector spinae (thoracic and lumbar): primary spinal extensors lifting the chest/head.
- Gluteus maximus: hip extension lifting the legs.
- Hamstrings: assist hip extension and stabilize the extended-leg position.
- Lower trapezius and rhomboids: scapular depression/retraction supporting the lifted arms.
- Posterior deltoid: assists the forward arm lift.
- Quadratus lumborum and gluteus medius: assist symmetric pelvic stabilization.
- Deep neck extensors: support the lifted head.

## 11. Visual Checkpoints
- [ ] Body prone, pelvis and thighs on the floor at start.
- [ ] Chest and head lift off the floor together.
- [ ] Arms extend forward and lift off the floor.
- [ ] Legs extend back and lift off the floor together.
- [ ] Body forms a gentle, symmetric bow.
- [ ] Spine extension is moderate, not cranked.
- [ ] Pelvis stays on the floor and neutral (not tucked).
- [ ] Head gaze is down-forward, not craned up.
- [ ] Shoulders are depressed (not shrugged to ears).
- [ ] Scapulae gently retracted (no winging).
- [ ] Knees are straight, not bent.
- [ ] Legs lift symmetrically (no one side higher).
- [ ] Arms lift symmetrically.
- [ ] No rotation or side-bend of the trunk.
- [ ] Lift is a hover, not a maximal arch.
- [ ] Glutes are engaged (legs lift from the hips).
- [ ] Neck flows into the thoracic curve (no sharp angle).
- [ ] Breathing steady (no breath-hold at the top).
- [ ] Contact remains on pelvis and lower thighs.
- [ ] No rib-flare/over-arching of the low back.
- [ ] Return to floor is controlled, not a drop.

## 12. Common Mistakes
- Craning the head/neck up (looking at the ceiling) instead of a neutral cervical extension.
- Over-arching the low back into lumbar hyperextension / rib-flare (compressive, not a "bow").
- Tilting the pelvis posteriorly (tucking the tail) which disconnects the glutes from the leg lift.
- Shrugging the shoulders toward the ears instead of depressing the scapulae.
- Lifting one side higher than the other (asymmetry / rotation).
- Bending the knees to make the legs "look" lifted rather than extending from the hips.
- Using momentum to throw the body up rather than a controlled hover.
- Holding the breath at the top.
- Dropping abruptly instead of lowering with control.

## 13. Acceptance Criteria
- [ ] Prone position with pelvis and thighs as the grounded fulcrum.
- [ ] Chest, head, arms, and legs lifted off the floor simultaneously into a symmetric, gentle bow.
- [ ] Spinal extension is moderate and symmetric (thoracic + lumbar), not end-range hyperextension.
- [ ] Pelvis remains on the floor and neutral; hip extension driven by the glutes.
- [ ] Knees extended; shoulders depressed and scapulae retracted (not shrugged).
- [ ] Head neutral (gaze down-forward), neck flowing into the thoracic curve.
- [ ] Position held as a controlled hover with steady respiration, then lowered under control.
