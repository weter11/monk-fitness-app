# Biomechanical Pose Specification — Squat (Jump)

## 1. Exercise overview
The squat jump is a plyometric, bilateral lower-body movement combining a bodyweight squat descent
with an explosive concentric triple extension of the hips, knees, and ankles to launch the body off
the ground, followed by a soft, controlled landing that absorbs force by returning to a squat. It is
a dynamic power and reactivity drill, not a slow strength exercise. The pose includes a flight phase
with no floor contact and a landing phase governed by eccentric deceleration.

## 2. Pose purpose
To develop lower-body explosive power, rate of force development, and landing mechanics. The movement
trains the stretch–shortening cycle: a rapid eccentric squat loads the muscles and tendons, then a
fast concentric triple extension produces vertical displacement. The landing teaches force absorption
and rebounds into the next repetition.

## 3. Whole-body alignment
- Stance roughly hip-to-shoulder width, toes slightly out, like an air squat.
- During the descent and the flight apex, the body is compact and aligned; the trunk leans forward
  modestly to balance the dropping hips.
- In flight, the body is extended and taut; limbs are off the ground entirely.
- On landing, the body returns to a squat posture to absorb impact; the center of mass drops back
  over the midfoot.
- Symmetry and bilateral timing are essential for a balanced launch and landing.

## 4. Head
- Cervical spine neutral; gaze forward.
- In flight the head stays aligned with the trunk; on landing the head does not drop or jut forward.

## 5. Spine
- Neutral, braced lumbar and thoracic posture throughout; natural curves maintained.
- Forward trunk lean is hip-driven during the squat portions; the spine does not round.
- During the explosive extension and flight, the trunk stays rigid to transmit power.
- On landing, the spine remains braced to receive the impact.

## 6. Upper body
- Arms swing backward during the descent (loading) and drive forward/up during the takeoff to add
  momentum; on landing they return to a counterbalanced position.
- Chest stays open; shoulders down and back.
- The arms are a timing aid, not a ground contact.

## 7. Lower body
- Descent (loading): a quick but controlled squat — hips back and down, knees flexing and tracking
  over toes, ankles dorsiflexing; stretch the hip/knee/ankle extensors.
- Takeoff (triple extension): explosive concurrent extension of hips, knees, and ankles
  (plantarflexion) launches the body; the thighs leave the ground together.
- Flight: hips, knees, and ankles extended; body airborne; limbs tucked minimally or extended.
- Landing: feet contact simultaneously; ankles, knees, and hips flex immediately to absorb force,
  returning to a squat depth; the movement should be "soft" with minimal noise.
- The next repetition begins from that squat bottom.

## 8. Contacts
- Feet flat on the floor during the squat and landing phases; full-foot contact, weight over
  midfoot/heel.
- During the flight phase there is NO floor contact — the body is entirely airborne.
- On landing, both feet contact at the same time; no hand contact with the ground.
- Base of support = the two footprints during ground phases.

## 9. Range of Motion
- Squat phases: hip flexion to at least ~90–110°, knee flexion to ~90–110°, ankle dorsiflexion
  moderate (~25–40° shin angle).
- Takeoff: hips, knees extend toward 0°; ankles move into plantarflexion (~15–25° beyond neutral).
- Flight: near-full extension of hip/knee/ankle.
- Landing: rapid reabsorption of the same squat ROM; depth may be partial or full depending on
  intent.

## 10. Muscle intent
- Loading (eccentric): quadriceps, gluteus maximus, hamstrings lengthen under load; calf muscles
  lengthen (eccentric dorsiflexion control).
- Takeoff (concentric): gluteus maximus + hamstrings (hip extension), quadriceps (knee extension),
  gastrocnemius + soleus (ankle plantarflexion) — the triple-extensor drive.
- Landing (eccentric deceleration): same extensors absorb force; gluteus medius and core stabilize.
- Arms: deltoids, traps assist the swing for momentum.

## 11. Visual checkpoints
- [ ] Stance hip-to-shoulder width, toes slightly out.
- [ ] Descent is a quick, controlled squat (hips back/down, knees track over toes).
- [ ] Weight over midfoot/heel before takeoff.
- [ ] Takeoff is explosive triple extension (hip + knee + ankle together).
- [ ] Both feet leave the ground simultaneously.
- [ ] Body is extended and taut in flight.
- [ ] Arms swing back to load, drive up to assist takeoff.
- [ ] Landing is soft with both feet contacting at once.
- [ ] Knees track over toes on landing, no valgus.
- [ ] Hips/knees/ankles flex immediately to absorb impact.
- [ ] Landing returns to a squat, not a stiff-legged stop.
- [ ] Spine braced and neutral through all phases.
- [ ] Head neutral, gaze forward.
- [ ] Chest stays open, no rounding.
- [ ] Pelvis level, no side drop.
- [ ] Symmetric power and landing (both sides equal).
- [ ] No excessive forward trunk collapse on landing.
- [ ] Quiet landing (low impact noise).
- [ ] Ready to rebound into the next jump.

## 12. Common mistakes
- Landing stiff-legged (no squat absorption) — high joint impact.
- Asymmetric takeoff/landing — one foot leads, risk of ankle/knee injury.
- Knees caving inward on landing (valgus).
- Insufficient depth on the loading squat, reducing stretch–shortening benefit.
- Not using the arms to aid timing/momentum.
- Letting the chest fold forward instead of bracing the trunk.
- Hard, loud landing indicating poor eccentric control.
- Toe-dominated takeoff (heels early lift) reducing power.

## 13. Acceptance criteria
- [ ] Explosive triple extension launches both feet off the ground together.
- [ ] Flight phase occurs with no floor contact.
- [ ] Landing is simultaneous, soft, and returns to a squat.
- [ ] Knees track over toes with no valgus on landing.
- [ ] Spine remains braced and neutral throughout.
- [ ] Weight over midfoot/heel; heels flat on ground contact.
- [ ] Symmetric, controlled movement with proper arm timing.
