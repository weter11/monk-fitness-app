# Biomechanical Pose Specification — Squat (Deep Hold)

## 1. Exercise overview
The deep squat hold (often called "ass-to-grass") is a static mobility and positional hold performed
at the very bottom of a full-depth squat. The hips drop as low as tissue allows, with the pelvis
lowered close to the heels, the knees fully flexed and tracked outward over the toes, and the ankles
deeply dorsiflexed. Unlike a dynamic squat, the pose is held isometrically to build and demonstrate
ankle, knee, and hip mobility plus trunk control under maximal flexion.

## 2. Pose purpose
To develop and display full squat-depth mobility — particularly ankle dorsiflexion, hip flexion with
external rotation, and knee flexion — while maintaining an upright, braced trunk. It is used as a
mobility drill, a resting position in some training cultures, and a diagnostic of lower-body
flexibility. The emphasis is on a stable, comfortable bottom position rather than on lifting load.

## 3. Whole-body alignment
- Feet about shoulder-width or slightly wider, toes turned slightly outward to permit knee tracking.
- The hips are lowered until they are close to or resting near the heels ("ass-to-grass").
- Center of mass stays over the midfoot; the trunk is as upright as mobility permits to keep balance.
- The pelvis is level; both limbs share the load symmetrically.
- The body is motionless in the held position.

## 4. Head
- Cervical spine neutral, gaze forward and slightly down.
- Head stacked on the trunk; no forward jut or drop.

## 5. Spine
- Neutral, braced lumbar and thoracic posture despite the deep fold; natural curves preserved.
- The trunk leans forward only as much as necessary to balance over the feet; an upright chest is the
  goal.
- The spinal erectors and abdominals brace to hold the posture without rounding.

## 6. Upper body
- Arms typically extended forward at shoulder height (or hands together at the chest) to act as a
  counterbalance that permits a more upright trunk.
- Shoulders relaxed and stable; chest open and lifted.
- The upper body does not collapse forward.

## 7. Lower body
- Maximal hip flexion with some external rotation; the femurs drop nearly horizontal or lower.
- Maximal knee flexion; knees track outward over the toes with no valgus.
- Maximal ankle dorsiflexion; the shins are angled sharply forward but the heels remain flat.
- The pelvis is lowered between the heels; depth is limited only by individual tissue mobility.
- Held isometrically: the hip/knee/ankle extensors and flexors co-contract to maintain the position.

## 8. Contacts
- Both feet flat on the floor, heels down, weight distributed over the midfoot/heel.
- No hand contact with the ground (the holding position is unsupported by the arms).
- Base of support = the two footprints; the center of mass must remain within this base to stay
  balanced.

## 9. Range of Motion
- Hip flexion: maximal — often 120–140°+ depending on individual structure.
- Knee flexion: maximal — commonly 130–150°+.
- Ankle dorsiflexion: maximal for the individual (shin angle can approach 40–60° from vertical).
- Hip external rotation: increased to keep knees over the turned-out toes at depth.
- Trunk lean: kept as small as mobility allows (goal: near-upright).

## 10. Muscle intent
- Held isometrically: quadriceps, gluteals, and hamstrings co-contract to maintain depth.
- Ankle dorsiflexors (tibialis anterior) and calf extensors balance the deep shin angle.
- Gluteus medius/minimus and deep external rotators keep the knees tracked outward and the pelvis
  level.
- Erector spinae and abdominal wall brace the trunk upright.
- Adductors are lengthened and active in maintaining the wide knee track.

## 11. Visual checkpoints
- [ ] Feet flat, heels down, toes slightly out.
- [ ] Hips dropped close to the heels (maximal depth).
- [ ] Knees fully flexed and tracked outward over the toes.
- [ ] No knee valgus (inward collapse).
- [ ] Ankles deeply dorsiflexed with heels still planted.
- [ ] Trunk as upright as mobility allows.
- [ ] Chest open and lifted, spine braced and neutral.
- [ ] Arms forward or at chest for counterbalance.
- [ ] Shoulders relaxed, not shrugged.
- [ ] Head neutral, gaze forward.
- [ ] Pelvis level, no side drop.
- [ ] Weight centered over midfoot/heel.
- [ ] Position held isometrically, no bobbing.
- [ ] Breathing controlled despite the deep fold.
- [ ] Both sides symmetric in depth and knee track.
- [ ] No rounding of the lower back.
- [ ] Feet evenly loaded, left and right equal.
- [ ] Hips between the heels, not tucked under excessively.
- [ ] Stable balance within the base of support.
- [ ] No pain-driven compensations (e.g., lifting a heel to relieve ankle tension).

## 12. Common mistakes
- Heels lifting to achieve depth — indicates insufficient ankle dorsiflexion; compensate by widening
  stance or elevating the heels slightly rather than lifting them.
- Rounding the back to "get lower" — depth should come from the hips/knees, not spinal flexion.
- Knees caving inward under the deep fold.
- Leaning too far forward, losing an upright trunk and balance.
- Asymmetric weight shift, one hip lower than the other.
- Using the hands on the floor for support (defeats the unsupported hold).
- Bouncing or pulsing instead of holding steadily.

## 13. Acceptance criteria
- [ ] Hips lowered to maximal depth with heels flat.
- [ ] Knees fully flexed and tracked outward over the toes, no valgus.
- [ ] Ankles dorsiflexed maximally with heels planted.
- [ ] Trunk upright as mobility allows, spine braced and neutral.
- [ ] Position held isometrically and symmetrically.
- [ ] Weight over midfoot/heel; balance maintained unaided.
- [ ] No spinal rounding, no heel lift, no knee cave.
