# Biomechanical Pose Specification — Leg Raise (Standard)

> **Canonical reference.** This document defines what a standard lying leg raise
> should look like from a strict biomechanics, anatomy, and strength-and-
> conditioning standpoint. It is implementation-independent and describes human
> movement only. It must remain valid even if the rendering system behind it is
> rewritten from scratch.
>
> Sources: sports biomechanics, physical-therapy literature, strength &
> conditioning practice, and human anatomy. Where expert opinion differs, the
> consensus is stated and the alternatives are noted.

---

## 1. Exercise Overview

**Movement goal.** A supine (lying on the back) anterior-core exercise in which
the straight or slightly-bent legs are lifted together off the floor by hip
flexion, then lowered under control without the low back arching.

**Training goal.** Develop the anterior core — primarily the iliopsoas (hip
flexors) working in coordination with the rectus abdominis and obliques to keep
the pelvis posteriorly tilted and the lumbar spine pressed to the floor.

**Intended muscles.**

- Hip flexors: iliopsoas (psoas major + iliacus), rectus femoris (a weaker
  hip-flexor contributor via the quadriceps).
- Anterior core stabilizers: rectus abdominis, internal/external obliques,
  transverse abdominis (posterior pelvic tilt to protect the low back).
- Supporting: quadriceps (knee extension hold), adductors (leg adduction/
  togetherness).

**Common execution style.** The athlete lies on the back with the low back
flattened to the floor (posterior pelvic tilt), hands resting at the sides or
under the hips for support. The legs (typically straight, or with a slight knee
bend for regressions) are lifted to roughly vertical (90° of hip flexion) by
driving the thighs toward the ceiling, then lowered slowly back toward the floor
without letting the lumbar spine lift. Breathing: exhale on the lift, inhale on
the controlled descent.

---

## 2. Pose Purpose

**Bottom / start position.** Legs extended along the floor, low back pinned
flat, pelvis in a slight posterior tilt. This is the reference (ease) position.

**Top / lifted position (canonical audited pose).** The legs are raised together
to about 90° of hip flexion (thighs roughly perpendicular to the floor, or as
high as the individual can lift without the low back arching). The anterior core
holds the pelvis in posterior tilt so the lumbar spine stays in contact with the
floor.

**Transition.** The lift is driven by hip flexion (the thigh rotating toward the
torso at the hip joint), not by swinging the legs with momentum or by arching
the back. The descent is a controlled eccentric lowering; the low back must not
peel off the floor at the bottom of the range.

**Pelvic strategy.** A posterior pelvic tilt (gentle "imprint") is maintained
throughout. The pelvis does not antevert (tip forward) — if it does, the lumbar
spine arches and the exercise loses its core intent and stresses the low back.

---

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment

**Center of mass.** The COM of the upper body stays on the floor; only the legs
move. As the legs rise, the effective COM of the legs shifts upward but the
pelvis/trunk remain anchored to the ground.

**Balance.** This is a supine ground exercise; balance is not a factor. Stability
comes from the posterior chain (back, pelvis, heels) staying in contact with the
floor.

**Symmetry.** Both legs lift together, symmetric and parallel. No leg rotates
outward or crosses the midline; the hips stay level (no hiking of one side).

**Posture.** The trunk is supine and flat. The lumbar spine is in gentle
posterior-tilt contact with the floor (no arch). The rib cage is stacked, not
flared. The head rests neutrally (not craned forward).

**Visual appearance.** From the side, the body is a flat line on the floor with
the two legs rising as a single straight (or lightly bent) unit. From the front,
the legs are parallel and centered; the body reads as stable and controlled, not
swinging.

---

## 4. Head

**Neck.** Neutral, resting on the floor (or on a thin pad). There is no
craning forward (which would engage the sternocleidomastoid and lift the head)
and no excessive tuck.

**Gaze.** Eyes look up toward the ceiling, or softly forward; relaxed, not
strained.

**Chin.** Neutral, neither jutting nor tucked hard. The cervical spine is a
gentle continuation of the (flat) thoracic spine.

**Cervical alignment.** The cervical curve is preserved but the head is not
lifted off the floor. No "ab-crunch neck" strain.

---

## 5. Spine

**Lumbar.** Posterior-tilt contact with the floor. The low back is flattened
(neutral-to-slightly-flexed via pelvic tilt), NOT arched (extended). This is the
single most important spinal criterion — the lumbar must stay down.

**Thoracic.** Flat on the floor, neutral. No rounding or extension; the upper
back remains in contact with the ground.

**Rib cage.** Stacked and slightly depressed ("ribs down") so the anterior core
can engage; not flared open. The thorax moves as part of the anchored trunk.

**Pelvis.** In a slight posterior tilt (anterior superior iliac spines and
pubic symphysis roughly level, with the front of the pelvis tipped slightly up
toward the ribs). The pelvis is the anchor; it does not rotate, hike, or tip
into anterior tilt. The glutes and deep core co-contract to hold this.

**State summary.** Lumbar: neutral / lightly posterior-tilted (flat to floor).
Thoracic: neutral (flat). Pelvis: posterior tilt, level, anchored. None of
these extend or rotate to fake the leg lift.

---

## 6. Upper Body

**Shoulders (glenohumeral).** Relaxed and resting on the floor; not shrugged.
They may be used for light support (hands under the hips/glutes) in regressions,
but they are not the prime movers.

**Scapula.** Flat on the floor, depressed and not elevated. No winging. They
anchor the trunk.

**Clavicles.** Level and resting; not riding up toward the neck.

**Elbows.** Extended and relaxed (arms straight at the sides) in the standard
version; or slightly bent with hands supporting the hips in a regression. Not
the focus.

**Forearms.** Resting on the floor (palms down) or with hands under the hips.
They are passive stabilizers, not engaged lifters.

**Wrists.** Neutral, relaxed; no tension or bending.

**Hands.** Flat on the floor (palms down) beside the hips, or lightly cupping
the glutes/hips for support. Fingers relaxed.

---

## 7. Lower Body

**Hip.** The hip joints are the prime movers: flexion drives the thighs upward
toward the ceiling. At the top, the hips are flexed to roughly 90° (thighs
vertical) or to the individual's maximal honest range without low-back lift. Both
hips flex symmetrically.

**Femur.** The femurs rise together as a unit, parallel to each other and to the
midline. They do not rotate internally or externally out of the sagittal plane.

**Knee.** In the standard straight-leg version, the knees are fully extended
(locked but not hyperextended) and held straight through the lift. In a
regression, a slight knee bend is acceptable (and reduces hamstring tension), but
the bend angle is constant — the knee does not wobble or snap.

**Ankle.** Neutral (natural), or in a slight plantarflexion (toes pointed) which
is the common aesthetic; dorsiflexion (toes toward shins) is also acceptable.
The ankle is not strained.

**Foot.** The feet are the distal end of the lifted unit; they rise with the
legs. If plantarflexed, the toes point away from the body.

**Toe direction.** Toes point straight up/forward (away from the head) in the
standard pointed-toe version, parallel between the two feet. They do not splay
outward or rotate inward.

**Weight distribution.** Load is borne by the posterior chain contact: the back,
pelvis, and (often) heels remain on the floor. The lifted legs are the moving
mass; the trunk anchors the rest. No disproportionate weight shift off the
anchored pelvis.

---

## 8. Contacts

**Back–floor contact (trunk).**

- *Contact surface:* the entire posterior trunk — thoracic spine, lumbar (in
  posterior tilt), and glutes/pelvis — rests on the floor.
- *Expected pressure:* even, distributed along the spine and pelvis; the pelvis
  is the heaviest contact.
- *Stability:* fixed anchor. The trunk does not lift or roll.
- *Visual appearance:* flat, stable torso; low back visibly pressed to the floor.

**Heel–floor contact (optional).**

- *Contact surface:* the backs of the heels may remain in light contact at the
  bottom of the range (more so in regressions).
- *Expected pressure:* minimal; they are a reference point, not a load-bearing
  anchor during the lift.
- *Stability:* the heels may lift as the legs rise; they should not slide.
- *Visual appearance:* heels near the floor at the start; legs clear the floor
  through the lift.

No hand/foot gripping of props is required (though hands may lightly support the
hips). The defining contact is the flat, anchored trunk.

---

## 9. Range of Motion

**Expected ROM.**

- Hip: from ~0° (legs on floor) to ~90° flexion (thighs vertical) at the top,
  or to the maximal honest range the individual can reach with the low back
  remaining flat.
- Knee: 0° (full extension) in the standard; a constant slight bend (e.g.
  20–30°) in regressions.
- Lumbar/pelvis: essentially no change in joint angle — they stay pinned flat.

**Acceptable variation.**

- Top hip flexion between ~70° and 100° (the common standard is thighs to
  vertical, ~90°).
- Slight constant knee bend in regressions.
- Toes pointed or neutral.
- Hands lightly supporting the hips (regression).
- Minor individual variation in how high the legs can rise before the back lifts.

**Unacceptable variation.**

- Low back arching / peeling off the floor at any point (lumbar extension).
- Anterior pelvic tilt (pelvis tipping forward) as the legs rise.
- Swinging or using momentum to fling the legs up.
- One leg higher/rotated/crossing the midline (asymmetry).
- Knee collapsing or bending inconsistently in the straight-leg version.
- Head craning up off the floor or neck straining.
- Shoulders shrugging or lifting off the floor.

---

## 10. Muscle Intent

Muscles that should *visually* appear engaged:

- **Iliopsoas (psoas major + iliacus)** — primary hip flexors lifting the
  thighs; tension visible through the front of the hip/groin.
- **Rectus abdominis / obliques / transverse abdominis** — hold the posterior
  pelvic tilt and keep the low back flat (the "core brace").
- **Quadriceps (rectus femoris)** — assists hip flexion and holds the knee
  straight; visible on the front of the thigh.
- **Adductors** — keep the legs together and parallel; subtle inner-thigh
  tension.
- **Gluteus (light)** — co-contracts to stabilize the posterior-tilted pelvis.

The trunk reads as "glued" to the floor while the legs lift as one controlled
unit.

## 11. Visual Checkpoints

1. ✓ Body lies flat on the floor (supine), trunk stable.
2. ✓ Low back is pressed flat to the floor (no arch) at start and through lift.
3. ✓ Pelvis is in a slight posterior tilt, level, not tipping forward.
4. ✓ Rib cage is stacked/down, not flared.
5. ✓ Head rests neutrally; neck not craned forward.
6. ✓ Gaze is up/forward, relaxed.
7. ✓ Shoulders are flat on the floor, not shrugged.
8. ✓ Scapulae are flat, not winging.
9. ✓ Arms are relaxed at the sides (or lightly supporting hips).
10. ✓ Both legs lift together as a symmetric unit.
11. ✓ Hips flex to roughly 90° (thighs near vertical) at the top.
12. ✓ Knees are extended (or hold a constant slight bend) and straight.
13. ✓ Legs are parallel and centered, not rotated or crossed.
14. ✓ Toes point straight up/forward, parallel between feet.
15. ✓ No low-back lift / arch as the legs rise.
16. ✓ No momentum/swing; the lift is controlled.
17. ✓ The descent lowers legs without the lumbar peeling off the floor.
18. ✓ Pelvis does not hike or rotate side-to-side.
19. ✓ Heels (if in contact) do not slide.
20. ✓ The trunk remains the anchored base; the legs are the only movers.
21. ✓ Both hips rise to the same height (symmetry).
22. ✓ Neck is relaxed, not straining the sternocleidomastoid.
23. ✓ The movement originates at the hip joint, not by swinging the feet.
24. ✓ The overall silhouette is a flat anchored torso with two legs rising as one.

## 12. Common Mistakes

**Arching low back (lumbar extension).** The pelvis tips anteriorly and the
small of the back lifts as the legs rise. *Why wrong:* shifts the load off the
anterior core onto the hip flexors + strained lumbar spine; loses the exercise's
purpose and risks back pain. *Visual difference:* a visible gap under the low
back; the pelvis tilts forward; the legs appear to lift "from the back."

**Swinging / momentum.** The legs are flung up with a bounce. *Why wrong:*
removes the eccentric/concentric control that trains the core. *Visual
difference:* a jerky, ballistic motion; the trunk may rock.

**Asymmetric lift.** One leg higher, rotated, or crossing the midline. *Why
wrong:* indicates core/hip imbalance and compensates with rotation. *Visual
difference:* the legs are not parallel; the pelvis may rotate.

**Neck strain / head crane.** The head lifts and the chin tucks hard. *Why
wrong:* offloads the core onto the neck flexors and strains the cervical spine.
*Visual difference:* the head lifts off the floor; the neck flexes sharply.

**Shoulder shrug / lift.** The shoulders rise off the floor. *Why wrong:*
indicates tension and loss of the anchored base. *Visual difference:* the upper
back lifts; a "hunched" appearance.

**Knee bend wobble (straight-leg version).** The knees bend inconsistently or
collapse. *Why wrong:* reduces the lever and changes the exercise (unless a
deliberate regression). *Visual difference:* the legs are not straight; the
knees flex during the lift.

**Incomplete posterior tilt.** The pelvis stays neutral/anteverted. *Why wrong:*
the low back cannot stay flat and the core does not engage. *Visual difference:*
the pelvis tips forward; an arch forms under the waist.

## 13. Acceptance Criteria

The leg-raise pose is **accepted only if every criterion is satisfied**:

- [ ] The body lies supine, flat, and stable on the floor.
- [ ] The low back is pressed flat to the floor (no arch) at start and through
      the lift.
- [ ] The pelvis is in a slight posterior tilt, level, not tipping forward.
- [ ] The rib cage is stacked/down, not flared.
- [ ] The head rests neutrally; the neck is not craned or strained.
- [ ] The shoulders and scapulae are flat on the floor, not shrugged or winged.
- [ ] The arms are relaxed (or lightly supporting the hips).
- [ ] Both legs lift together as a symmetric, parallel unit.
- [ ] The hips flex to roughly 90° (thighs near vertical) at the top, or to the
      maximal honest range with the back flat.
- [ ] The knees are extended (or hold a constant slight bend) and straight.
- [ ] The legs are parallel and centered, not rotated or crossed.
- [ ] The toes point straight up/forward, parallel between feet.
- [ ] No low back lifts or arches as the legs rise.
- [ ] The lift is controlled, with no momentum/swing.
- [ ] The descent lowers the legs without the lumbar peeling off the floor.
- [ ] The pelvis does not hike or rotate side-to-side.
- [ ] The movement originates at the hip joint, not by swinging the feet.
- [ ] The rendered pose matches the visual checkpoints in §11.
