# Pull-Up (Wide Grip)

## 1. Exercise Overview
The wide-grip pull-up is a closed-chain vertical pull performed hanging from a fixed horizontal bar with an overhand (pronated) grip and the hands placed well outside shoulder width (approximately 2× biacromial shoulder width). Widening the grip increases the degree of shoulder abduction at the hang and forces the elbows to track outward and downward, increasing the vertical distance the shoulders must travel and biasing the movement toward the latissimus dorsi with less biceps contribution. It is used for lat development and for training the wide scapular-adduction path.

## 2. Pose Purpose
This document specifies the biomechanics of a single wide-grip pull-up repetition, from the extended hang to the top. The wider hand placement means the humerus is more abducted; the elbows flare outward and down rather than tucking to the ribs. Vertical shoulder travel is larger (making the movement harder), and the chin-over-bar top is reached with a broader, more lat-dominant pull. The scapulae still initiate the motion (depress + retract) before elbow flexion.

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment
- The body hangs in a vertical line beneath the bar; the wider grip places the hands outside the shoulders, so the upper arms form a wide "V" from the bar.
- The trunk is braced and neutral; the slight forward leg pendulum is controlled and does not swing the torso.
- The midline stays in a single sagittal plane; there is no rotation of the thorax.
- Because the grip is wider, the shoulders are more abducted and the overall posture is broader through the upper back.

## 4. Head
- The cervical spine is neutral relative to the thorax; the chin is neither tucked nor jutted.
- Gaze is forward and slightly downward at the bottom, lifting as the chest rises; the chin clears the bar at the top.
- The neck is relaxed; no elevation of the shoulders toward the ears.

## 5. Spine
- The thoracic spine is neutral to slight natural kyphosis; the lumbar spine is neutral (no arch, no round).
- The pull is initiated by the scapulae: from the relaxed, elevated, protracted hang, the lower trapezius and serratus anterior depress the scapulae while the rhomboids and middle trapezius retract them. The body rises a few units with arms essentially straight (active hang).
- Because the grip is wide, the scapulae retract across a longer distance and the latissimus dorsi is placed under greater stretch at the bottom.
- Continued pulling is driven by shoulder extension/adduction and elbow flexion, with the chest leading up. The spine stays rigid; no flexion or extension beyond neutral.

## 6. Upper Body
- **Grip:** overhand (pronated), palms facing away from the face. Hands well outside the shoulders (≈2× biacromial width). Full grip recommended.
- **Shoulders (glenohumeral):** the humeri hang in greater abduction (~30–45°) because of the wide hand placement. During the pull the shoulders extend and adduct as the elbows flex and draw outward/downward (the elbows flare out to the sides rather than tucking to the ribs).
- **Elbows:** flex as the reach shortens, but track outward and downward (laterally) rather than straight down beside the thorax. At the top the elbows are bent and drawn down and out.
- **Wrists/hands:** fixed on the bar; the hands are the anchor. Wrists neutral.
- **Muscles (primary):** latissimus dorsi dominates (greater stretch and adduction demand), teres major, posterior deltoid, biceps brachii (less than standard grip), brachialis, rhomboids/middle trapezius (retraction), lower trapezius/serratus anterior (depression).

## 7. Lower Body
- Hips and knees extended or slightly flexed; legs hang with a controlled slight forward pendulum.
- Pelvis neutral; glutes gently engaged to prevent lumbar hyperextension.
- Feet relaxed, not driving the movement.
- The lower body acts as a stable, non-swinging mass.

## 8. Contacts
- **Primary (anterior/upper) contact:** both hands fixed on the horizontal bar, placed wide. The hands are the only external anchor; the body is lifted relative to them.
- **Secondary contact:** none. Feet are free.
- **Contact stability:** the grip is secure and static; hands do not slide or regrip.
- **Bottom reference:** full extended hang with arms straight and shoulders depressed (not shrugged), feet hanging free.

## 9. Range of Motion
- **Shoulder girdle:** full depression and retraction at top; full elevation and protraction at bottom.
- **Glenohumeral:** from high shoulder elevation/abduction in the hang to near-neutral at the top; the wide grip increases the vertical excursion the shoulders must cover.
- **Elbow:** from ~175–180° (extended) at bottom to ~90–110° of flexion at top (elbows flared out).
- **Wrist:** neutral throughout.
- **Spine/trunk:** neutral sagittal alignment; no rotation; minimal deviation.
- **Vertical travel:** chin clears the bar at the top; bottom is the full hang.

## 10. Muscle Intent
- **Initiation:** lower trapezius + serratus anterior depress; rhomboids + middle trapezius retract (larger retraction excursion due to wide grip). Body rises with arms straight.
- **Pull:** latissimus dorsi and teres major drive shoulder extension/adduction across the wider path; posterior deltoid assists; biceps/brachialis flex the elbows (less dominant than standard grip); chest leads up.
- **Stabilization:** anterior core and glutes brace the trunk; forearm flexors and grip maintain the fixed wide contact; scapular upward rotators control the broad path.

## 11. Visual Checkpoints
- [ ] ✓ Hands fixed on the bar, overhand grip, well outside the shoulders (~2× shoulder width).
- [ ] ✓ Arms fully extended at the bottom; shoulders depressed, not shrugged.
- [ ] ✓ The first motion is the scapulae dropping and squeezing together (body rises a few units, elbows straight).
- [ ] ✓ The elbows flare outward and downward as the pull continues (not tucked to the ribs).
- [ ] ✓ The humeri are clearly abducted because of the wide grip.
- [ ] ✓ The chest leads upward; the chin clears the bar at the top.
- [ ] ✓ The trunk stays rigid; no swing, no lumbar arch, neutral pelvis.
- [ ] ✓ The head is neutral; neck long, not jutted.
- [ ] ✓ The legs hang with a slight controlled forward pendulum.
- [ ] ✓ Gaze forward/down at bottom, lifting naturally.
- [ ] ✓ Bottom position is a full hang with depressed shoulders.
- [ ] ✓ No visible upper-trap shrugging at the top.
- [ ] ✓ The path is smooth: scapular set → elbow flex (out/down) → chest to bar.
- [ ] ✓ Descent retraces the same controlled path.

## 12. Common Mistakes
- **Shrugging:** elevating the shoulders toward the ears instead of depressing—worse tendency here because the wide grip challenges scapular control.
- **Elbows collapsing forward/in:** losing the outward flare and turning it into a narrow pull, defeating the wide-grip intent.
- **Half reps / insufficient travel:** the wide grip is harder, so lifters often stop short of the chin-over-bar top or short of a full hang.
- **Swinging/kipping:** using leg and lumbar momentum to compensate for the harder pull.
- **Lumbar hyperextension:** arching instead of bracing.
- **Excessive shoulder abduction pain:** going wider than comfortable can impinge the shoulder; width should remain within a pain-free range (experts vary, but ~1.5–2× shoulder is common; beyond that adds risk without clear benefit).

## 13. Acceptance Criteria
- [ ] Hands are fixed on the bar in an overhand grip well outside shoulder width (~2× biacromial width).
- [ ] At the bottom the arms are fully extended and the shoulders depressed (not shrugged).
- [ ] The pull begins with scapular depression and retraction before elbow flexion.
- [ ] The elbows track outward and downward (flared), reflecting the wide abducted shoulder position.
- [ ] The chest leads up; the chin clears the bar at the top.
- [ ] The trunk remains rigid and neutral; no swing, no lumbar arch.
- [ ] The feet hang free with at most a slight controlled forward pendulum.
- [ ] The descent retraces the same controlled path to a full hang.
