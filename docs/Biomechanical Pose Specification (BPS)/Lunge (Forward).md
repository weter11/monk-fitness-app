# Biomechanical Pose Specification — Lunge (Forward)

> **Canonical reference.** This document defines what a forward lunge should look
> like from a strict biomechanics, anatomy, and strength-and-conditioning
> standpoint. It is implementation-independent and describes human movement only.
> It must remain valid even if the rendering system behind it is rewritten from
> scratch.
>
> Sources: sports biomechanics, physical-therapy literature, strength &
> conditioning practice, and human anatomy. Where expert opinion differs, the
> consensus is stated and the alternatives are noted.

---

## 1. Exercise Overview

**Movement goal.** A forward lunge is a split-stance, closed-chain movement in
which one foot steps forward and the body lowers by flexing the front hip and
knee while the rear leg extends and the rear heel lifts. The torso is held
upright (or with a controlled slight forward lean) over the front foot, and the
weight is carried predominantly on the front leg.

**Training goal.** Develop unilateral lower-body strength and control, with
primary emphasis on the quadriceps, gluteus maximus, and hamstrings of the
front leg, and eccentric demand on the hip extensors and knee stabilizers of the
rear leg. Secondary demand on the trunk for anti-rotation and anti-flexion
stability.

**Intended muscles.**

- Prime movers (front leg): quadriceps (rectus femoris, vastus complex),
  gluteus maximus, hamstrings.
- Rear leg: gluteus maximus and hamstrings (eccentric control of the lowering
  rear hip), gastrocnemius/soleus (rear heel lift, ankle plantarflexion).
- Trunk: rectus abdominis, obliques, erector spinae (upright posture and
  anti-rotation), gluteus medius (pelvic leveling).
- Supporting: tibialis anterior (front-foot dorsiflexion control),
  hip adductors/abductors (stance width and pelvic stability).

**Common execution style.** From standing, the athlete takes a controlled step
forward with one foot, landing the heel first and then the whole foot. The front
knee flexes and the hip descends so the thigh approaches horizontal; the rear
heel lifts as the rear knee lowers toward (but not touching) the floor. The
torso stays tall. From the bottom, the athlete drives through the front foot to
return to standing. Breathing is typically inhale on the descent, exhale on the
drive up.

---

## 2. Pose Purpose

This specification treats the **bottom (deep) position** of the forward lunge as
the canonical audited pose. The bottom position is the mechanically loaded
extreme and the clearest test of alignment.

**Bottom position (deep lunge).** The front foot is planted flat, the front hip
and knee are flexed so the front thigh is roughly horizontal (or as deep as
mobility allows) and the front shin is near-vertical. The rear leg is extended
behind the body: the rear hip is extended/hyperextended relative to standing,
the rear knee is flexed and lowered toward the floor, and the rear heel is
lifted so the rear foot rests on the toe/ball. The torso is upright or carries a
slight forward lean from the hip, and the trunk remains long. Weight is
disproportionately on the front foot.

**Front knee tracking.** The front knee travels forward over the front foot and
in line with the second/third toe — no inward valgus collapse and no outward
varus.

**Torso.** Upright with a controlled, optional slight forward lean generated at
the hip (not by rounding the spine). The rib cage sits over the pelvis.

**Return.** The athlete drives the front foot into the floor, extends the front
hip and knee, and steps the rear foot forward to return to standing. The pose
described here is the bottom hold; the return is the reverse of the descent.

---

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment

**Center of mass.** The whole-body COM sits over the front foot (the loaded
support). It descends vertically as the lunge is entered and rises on the drive
out. It must remain within the support polygon defined primarily by the front
foot, with the rear toe providing a secondary rear anchor.

**Balance.** Load is carried predominantly on the front foot. The rear toe/ball
provides balance and a small share of support but is not the primary driver. The
body is balanced in the sagittal plane: no lateral tipping.

**Symmetry.** Left and right are not mirror-symmetric in a lunge (it is a
split-stance movement), but the loaded side and the trailing side must each be
internally correct. The pelvis is level (no drop on either side) and square
(facing forward, not rotated). The shoulders are level and square to the pelvis.

**Posture.** A line from the crown of the head through the thorax and pelvis
stays upright or leans slightly forward from the hip. The rear leg extends
straight back. The front shin is near-vertical; the front thigh approaches
horizontal at depth.

**Visual appearance.** From the side, the body shows a clear "Z" of stacked
segments: upright trunk, bent front leg, extended rear leg. From the front, the
front knee tracks over the front foot, the pelvis is level, and the shoulders
are square.

---

## 4. Head

**Neck.** The cervical spine is a neutral continuation of the thoracic spine.
There is no excessive flexion (chin to chest) or extension (head craning up).

**Gaze.** The eyes look forward and slightly down, toward the horizon or the
floor a short distance ahead. The gaze is level, not tilted up or down.

**Chin.** The chin is neutral and slightly packed so the ears sit roughly above
the shoulders when viewed from the side. The head does not jut forward.

**Cervical alignment.** The cervical curve is preserved but not exaggerated. The
skull, cervical spine, and thoracic spine form one gentle, unbroken line. No
"gooseneck" (cervical extension) and no "turtle tuck" (cervical flexion).

---

## 5. Spine

**Lumbar.** Neutral-to-slightly-extended. The natural lumbar arch is maintained
without excessive extension (swayback) or flexion (rounded lower back). The
lumbar spine does not round as the athlete lowers; if a slight forward trunk
lean is used, it is produced at the hip, not by lumbar flexion.

**Thoracic.** Neutral-to-slightly-extended. The upper back is flat and broad.
There is no rounding (kyphosis). The rib cage is stacked over the pelvis with the
anterior core engaged so the chest stays open and tall.

**Rib cage.** The ribs are down and the anterior abdominal wall is braced. The
chest is not flared open nor collapsed. The thoracic cage moves as a unit with
the trunk.

**Pelvis.** Neutral and level. The pelvis is square to the front (no rotation)
and level side-to-side (no contralateral hip drop). It is not in excessive
anterior or posterior tilt. The gluteals and anterior core co-contract to hold
the pelvis stable and the trunk upright.

**State summary.** Lumbar: neutral (slight extension acceptable, no flexion).
Thoracic: neutral (slight extension acceptable). Pelvis: neutral, level, square,
no rotation. The spine stays long; depth is created by the legs, not by
rounding.

---

## 6. Upper Body

**Shoulders (glenohumeral).** The shoulders are level, square to the pelvis, and
relaxed away from the ears. The arms hang at the sides or are held in a chosen
position (commonly the hands rest on the front thigh, are held at the chest, or
extend for counterbalance). The humerus is not loaded and does not shrug.

**Scapula.** The shoulder blades sit flat on the rib cage. They do not wing or
excessively elevate. If the arms are held up or out, the scapulae track naturally
without shrugging.

**Clavicles.** The clavicles are level and symmetric, continuing the line of the
shoulders. They do not ride up toward the neck.

**Elbows.** Free. If the hands rest on the front thigh, the elbows are lightly
flexed; if the arms are extended for balance, the elbows are straight but not
locked. No forced position is required — the upper body is a stable, unloaded
counterweight to the lunge.

**Forearms / wrists / hands.** Positioned according to the chosen arm style
(rest on thigh, held at chest, or extended). The hands are relaxed, not clenched,
unless a load is held. There is no pronation/supination deviation that implies
shoulder compensation.

**Note.** The upper body's role is postural and stabilizing. It must not
collapse forward, twist, or tilt the trunk. Any held load (e.g., a weight at the
chest) increases the trunk-stability demand but does not change the lower-body
landmarks below.

---

## 7. Lower Body

**Front hip.** Flexed so the front thigh approaches horizontal at depth. The
front hip is the primary loaded joint: it controls the descent and drives the
return. The femur descends from the pelvis to a ~90° (or deeper) angle at the
knee.

**Front knee.** Flexed to track over the front foot. The knee travels forward in
line with the second/third toe. **No valgus** (inward knee collapse) and no
varus (outward bow). The front shin is near-vertical (roughly 0–20° from
vertical), not pitched sharply forward.

**Front ankle.** Dorsiflexed so the front foot is flat with the heel planted.
The ankle angle reflects the shin staying near-vertical; the heel does not lift
off the floor in the bottom position.

**Front foot.** Flat on the floor — heel and toe both down. The foot points
straight forward (or very slightly outward, ≤15°). It is the primary load-bearing
contact.

**Rear hip.** Extended (the rear thigh is behind the pelvis, in relative
hyperextension compared to standing). The rear glute and hamstring control the
lowering of the rear side.

**Rear knee.** Flexed and lowered toward the floor. The rear knee does not
contact the floor in the standard lunge (it hovers just above).

**Rear ankle.** Plantarflexed so the rear heel lifts; the rear foot rests on the
toe/ball, the dorsal top of the foot often near the floor depending on mobility.

**Rear foot.** Rests on the toe/ball of the foot; the heel is raised. It points
straight back (or very slightly outward) and is the secondary balance contact.

**Weight distribution.** Predominantly on the front foot (roughly 60–80% of
load, depending on depth and coaching model). The rear toe provides balance only.

---

## 8. Contacts

**Front foot–floor contact (×1, primary).**

- *Contact surface:* the whole sole — heel and toe/ball — flat on the floor.
- *Expected pressure:* the primary load; heaviest under the heel/midfoot,
  distributed forward through the toe. Symmetric within the foot.
- *Stability:* fixed. The front foot does not slide or lift; it is the drive
  anchor.
- *Visual appearance:* flat foot, heel down, toes forward, knee tracking over
  the toes.

**Rear foot–floor contact (×1, secondary).**

- *Contact surface:* the toe/ball region (and, depending on ankle mobility, the
  dorsal top) of the foot on the floor; the heel is raised.
- *Expected pressure:* light, balance-only; not a driving load.
- *Stability:* fixed as a rear anchor; the rear foot does not slide, but the
  heel is intentionally lifted.
- *Visual appearance:* rear heel up, rear foot pointing back, rear knee hovering
  near the floor.

No knee, hand, or hip contact with the floor in the canonical forward lunge.
(Modifications that add a rear-knee touch or a front-hand touch are alternative
variants outside this spec.)

---

## 9. Range of Motion

**Expected ROM.**

- Front knee: flexes to roughly 90° (thigh horizontal) at a standard deep
  lunge; deeper is acceptable with mobility.
- Front hip: flexes to ~90° (or more) consistent with the knee angle.
- Front ankle: dorsiflexes to keep the shin near-vertical and the heel down.
- Rear hip: extends/hyperextends as the rear leg trails; rear knee flexes as it
  lowers.
- Rear ankle: plantarflexes (heel lift).
- Spine/trunk: minimal change in joint angle — stays long and upright.

**Acceptable variation.**

- Depth from thigh-parallel to slightly above/below parallel.
- Slight forward trunk lean (from the hip) up to ~20–30°.
- Front foot turned out up to ~15°.
- Rear foot turned out slightly for comfort.
- Hand position (rest on thigh, chest, or extended) per chosen style.

**Unacceptable variation.**

- Front knee valgus (collapsing inward) or varus.
- Front heel lifting off the floor at the bottom.
- Front shin pitching far forward (excessive anterior translation of the knee
  beyond the toe line by a large margin), indicating poor ankle mobility or
  forward weight shift.
- Rear knee touching the floor (standard lunge) or rear foot sliding.
- Pelvis drop, rotation, or tilt (asymmetric or rotated hips).
- Lumbar flexion / rounded back at the bottom.
- Torso collapsing forward or twisting.
- Weight shifting predominantly onto the rear foot (lunge becomes a stumble).
- Asymmetric shoulder tilt.

## 10. Muscle Intent

Muscles that should *visually* appear engaged:

- **Quadriceps (front leg)** — dominant knee extensor under load; visible
  tension on the front of the thigh, especially at the bottom.
- **Gluteus maximus (front leg)** — hip extensor driving the return; engaged to
  keep the front pelvis stable.
- **Hamstrings (front leg)** — co-contract with the quadriceps for knee
  stability; assist hip control.
- **Gluteus maximus / hamstrings (rear leg)** — eccentric control of the
  lowering rear hip; visible tension along the back of the rear thigh.
- **Gastrocnemius / soleus (rear leg)** — rear heel lift (plantarflexion).
- **Gluteus medius (both sides)** — pelvic leveling; visible as hip stability.
- **Rectus abdominis / obliques** — anterior trunk rigidity (upright, no
  rotation).
- **Erector spinae** — posterior trunk rigidity, preserves the upright line.
- **Tibialis anterior (front leg)** — controls front-foot dorsiflexion.

The body reads as "loaded on the front leg, tall through the trunk."

---

## 11. Visual Checkpoints

An auditor should be able to confirm each of the following from the rendered
pose:

1. ✓ Front foot is flat on the floor, heel down, pointing forward (≤15° out).
2. ✓ Front knee is flexed ~90° (thigh near horizontal) at depth.
3. ✓ Front knee tracks over the front foot, in line with the second/third toe.
4. ✓ Front knee shows no valgus (inward collapse) or varus (outward bow).
5. ✓ Front shin is near-vertical; front heel remains on the floor.
6. ✓ Front hip is flexed consistent with the knee angle (thigh down to ~horizontal).
7. ✓ Rear leg is extended behind; rear hip in relative extension.
8. ✓ Rear knee is flexed and lowered near (but not touching) the floor.
9. ✓ Rear heel is lifted; rear foot rests on the toe/ball.
10. ✓ Rear foot points straight back (or slightly out).
11. ✓ Weight is predominantly on the front foot (front leg loaded).
12. ✓ Torso is upright or carries only a slight forward lean from the hip.
13. ✓ Lumbar spine is neutral — no rounding at the bottom.
14. ✓ Thoracic spine is flat/broad — no kyphosis.
15. ✓ Rib cage is stacked and braced; chest open and tall.
16. ✓ Pelvis is level side-to-side — no contralateral hip drop.
17. ✓ Pelvis is square to the front — no rotation.
18. ✓ Neck is a neutral continuation of the spine; chin neutral and packed.
19. ✓ Gaze is forward and slightly down.
20. ✓ Shoulders are level and square to the pelvis, not shrugged.
21. ✓ Scapulae flat on the rib cage — no winging.
22. ✓ No trunk twist; shoulders and hips face the same direction.
23. ✓ Front foot does not slide or lift.
24. ✓ Rear foot does not slide (heel intentionally raised).
25. ✓ No rear-knee floor contact in the standard lunge.
26. ✓ Depth is created by hip/knee flexion, not by rounding the spine.
27. ✓ The loaded (front) leg reads as engaged; the rear leg reads as a stable
     trailing anchor.
28. ✓ The overall silhouette is a clean split-stance lunge with stacked trunk.

## 12. Common Mistakes

**Front knee valgus (caving inward).** The front knee drops toward the midline.
*Why wrong:* increases medial knee-joint stress and indicates weak glute
stabilization. *Visual difference:* the front knee points inward relative to the
foot; the ankle rolls in.

**Front heel lifting.** The front heel rises off the floor at the bottom.
*Why wrong:* shifts load forward, reduces stability, and indicates poor ankle
dorsiflexion or excessive forward weight shift. *Visual difference:* the front
foot rolls onto the toe; the shin pitches sharply forward.

**Pelvis drop / rotate ("hiked hip").** The trailing-side hip drops or the
pelvis rotates. *Why wrong:* indicates weak gluteus medius and reduces trunk
stability. *Visual difference:* the hips are not level; one iliac crest sits
lower; the pelvis may face off-square.

**Rounded lower back (lumbar flexion).** The athlete rounds the spine to reach
depth. *Why wrong:* off-loads the legs and strains the lumbar spine. *Visual
difference:* the lower back bows forward; the chest drops toward the thigh.

**Excessive forward trunk lean / chest collapse.** The torso pitches far
forward. *Why wrong:* changes the movement into a good-morning-style hinge and
reduces quadriceps demand. *Visual difference:* the shoulders travel well ahead
of the front foot; the trunk angle steepens beyond a slight lean.

**Rear foot sliding / rear knee to floor.** The rear foot drifts or the rear
knee touches down. *Why wrong:* breaks the controlled eccentric rear-leg demand
and the balance anchor. *Visual difference:* the rear footprint shifts; the rear
knee contacts the floor.

**Weight shifting to the rear foot.** The athlete leans back onto the rear leg.
*Why wrong:* off-loads the target front leg. *Visual difference:* the COM sits
behind the front foot; the rear heel may lower.

**Twisting torso / uneven shoulders.** The shoulders rotate or tilt. *Why wrong:*
indicates poor anti-rotation control. *Visual difference:* the shoulders are not
square to the hips; one shoulder drops.

---

## 13. Acceptance Criteria

The forward lunge (bottom position) is **accepted only if every criterion is
satisfied**:

- [ ] Front foot is flat, heel down, pointing forward (≤15° out), not sliding.
- [ ] Front knee flexed ~90° (thigh near horizontal) at depth.
- [ ] Front knee tracks over the front foot, aligned with the second/third toe.
- [ ] No front-knee valgus or varus.
- [ ] Front shin near-vertical; front heel stays on the floor.
- [ ] Rear leg extended behind; rear knee lowered near (not touching) the floor.
- [ ] Rear heel lifted; rear foot on toe/ball, pointing back, not sliding.
- [ ] Weight predominantly on the front foot.
- [ ] Torso upright or slight hip-hinge lean; not collapsed forward.
- [ ] Lumbar and thoracic spines neutral; no rounding or kyphosis.
- [ ] Rib cage stacked and braced; chest open.
- [ ] Pelvis level and square; no drop or rotation.
- [ ] Neck continues the spinal line; chin neutral and packed.
- [ ] Shoulders level, square, not shrugged; scapulae flat.
- [ ] No trunk twist; shoulders and hips face the same direction.
- [ ] Gaze forward and slightly down.
- [ ] The rendered pose matches the visual checkpoints in §11.
