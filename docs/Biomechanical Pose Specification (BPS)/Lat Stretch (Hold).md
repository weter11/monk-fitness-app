# Lat Stretch (Hold)

## 1. Exercise Overview
The latissimus dorsi stretch is a mobility/soft-tissue drill that places the latissimus dorsi and the surrounding thoracolumbar fascia under a sustained lengthened tension. It can be performed hanging from a bar (the most loaded version) or on the floor/against a wall with one or both arms elevated overhead (a passive or active-assisted version). The defining feature is an overhead reach that draws the shoulder girdle upward and away from the pelvis, producing a stretch sensation down the side of the torso. It is a stretch, not a loaded pull—there is no concentric pulling effort.

## 2. Pose Purpose
This document specifies the biomechanics of a held latissimus dorsi stretch. The arm(s) are taken overhead and the shoulder girdle is elevated and gently tractioned away from the pelvis, lengthening the lat from its humeral insertion to its spinal/pelvic origin. The emphasis is on the subjective stretch down the side of the torso, maintained with relaxed breathing. It may be done hanging (body weight provides traction) or kneeling/floor-based (arm elevated, gentle side-bend or overhead reach).

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment
- **Hanging version:** the body hangs from one or both hands with the arm(s) overhead; the free side can bend or the legs can cross to increase the side stretch. The trunk may lean slightly to the stretching side to bias one lat.
- **Floor/vertical version:** the individual kneels or stands and reaches one or both arms overhead, gently tractioning the arm upward; a slight side lean enhances the unilateral stretch.
- The non-stretching parts of the body are relaxed and stable; the pelvis can be tilted slightly to increase or decrease tension as needed.
- The midline may show a gentle lateral curve in the stretching direction (this is intentional for the side stretch), unlike the strict vertical pulls.

## 4. Head
- Cervical spine neutral or following the gentle side-bend; the head is not cranked.
- Gaze is forward/down in the hanging version, or forward in the floor version.
- The neck is relaxed; no shrugging of the upper trapezius toward the ears on the stretching side (the elevation here is a controlled traction, not a tense shrug).

## 5. Spine
- The thoracic and lumbar spines are in a relaxed position; in the unilateral version a gentle lateral flexion (side bend) toward the non-stretching side lengthens the lat on the stretching side.
- There is no spinal rotation; the curve is purely coronal (side bend) when present.
- The scapulae are elevated and slightly protracted on the reaching side(s) to maximize the distance between the arm and the pelvis (the source of the stretch).
- The spine's role is passive—it simply accommodates the shoulder-to-pelvis lengthening.

## 6. Upper Body
- **Grip/reach:** in the hanging version the hand(s) are fixed on the bar overhead (typically overhand or neutral); in the floor version the hand is placed on a surface or simply reached into the air.
- **Shoulders (glenohumeral):** the humerus is taken into full elevation (~160–180° of flexion/abduction) overhead. The shoulder girdle is elevated (scapulae up toward the ears, but relaxed) to traction the arm away from the body.
- **Elbows:** extended (straight) to keep the lat lengthened along its full line; a bent elbow would shorten the lat and reduce the stretch.
- **Wrists/hands:** fixed (hanging) or pressing/reaching (floor); wrists neutral.
- **Muscles (target):** latissimus dorsi is the primary tissue under lengthening tension, with the teres major, posterior deltoid, and thoracolumbar fascia as synergists; the stretch is felt down the side of the torso toward the pelvis.

## 7. Lower Body
- **Hanging version:** the legs may be crossed at the ankles and bent, or one knee bent and the other straight, to add a gentle counterweight side pull that increases the stretch on one side.
- **Floor version:** the individual kneels or stands with a stable base; the pelvis may shift slightly away from the reaching arm.
- The lower body is passive and stable; it provides the anchor against which the overhead arm is tractioned.
- The feet are planted (floor) or hanging free (bar), never driving a pull.

## 8. Contacts
- **Primary contact (hanging version):** one or both hands fixed on the bar overhead; the hand(s) are the anchor and the body hangs/tractions from them.
- **Primary contact (floor version):** the hand may press against a wall/ground or simply reach overhead; the lower body (knees/feet) provides the stable base.
- **Secondary contact:** none required in either version; this is a stretch, not a loaded pull.
- **Contact stability:** the grip (hanging) or base (floor) is steady so the stretch can be held and breathed into.

## 9. Range of Motion
- **Shoulder girdle:** elevated and protracted on the reaching side to maximize shoulder-to-pelvis distance.
- **Glenohumeral:** full elevation (~160–180°) overhead; the arm is straight.
- **Elbow:** fully extended to keep the lat lengthened.
- **Spine (unilateral):** gentle lateral flexion (a few degrees of side bend) toward the non-stretching side.
- **Wrist:** neutral.
- **Breathing:** the stretch is held for several calm breaths; the range is static, not cycled.

## 10. Muscle Intent
- **Lengthen (target):** the latissimus dorsi is held under eccentric/lengthened tension from humeral insertion to spinal/pelvic origin; the goal is a sustained, comfortable stretch sensation, not a contraction.
- **Traction:** the shoulder girdle is gently elevated and the arm tractioned away from the pelvis (often with body weight in the hanging version) to increase the stretch without forceful contraction.
- **Relax:** the antagonistic and surrounding muscles (including the upper traps on the reaching side) stay relaxed so the stretch can deepen with breathing; the core is lightly toned only for stable posture, not for pulling.

## 12. Common Mistakes
- **Bending the elbow:** shortens the lat and kills the stretch; keep the arm straight.
- **Tensing the upper trap into a hard shrug:** the elevation should be a relaxed traction, not a clenched shrug that guards the shoulder.
- **Rounding the low back / losing the side bend:** failing to create the shoulder-to-pelvis distance (and the gentle lateral curve) reduces the lat lengthening.
- **Holding the breath:** breath-holding prevents the stretch from deepening; keep breathing.
- **Forcing range / pain:** pushing into sharp pain at the shoulder or rib cage instead of a comfortable tension.
- **Using it as a pull:** contracting the lats/biceps to "do work" rather than relaxing into the stretch.
- **Over-rotating the spine:** adding twist when only coronal side-bend is intended.

- [ ] The trunk has no rotation; the spine is stable.
