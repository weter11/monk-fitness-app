# Biomechanical Pose Specification — Hip Flexor Stretch (Hold)

> **Canonical reference.** This document defines what a half-kneeling hip-flexor
> stretch should look like from a strict biomechanics, anatomy, and physical-
> therapy standpoint. It is implementation-independent and describes human
> movement only. It must remain valid even if the rendering system behind it is
> rewritten from scratch.
>
> Sources: sports biomechanics, physical-therapy literature, strength &
> conditioning practice, and human anatomy (hip-flexor anatomy: psoas major,
> iliacus, rectus femoris, sartorius, tensor fasciae latae). Where expert opinion
> differs, the consensus is stated and the alternatives are noted.

---

## 1. Exercise Overview

**Movement goal.** The half-kneeling hip-flexor stretch is a static mobility
hold performed in a half-kneel: one knee on the floor and the other foot planted
forward with the front knee at ~90°. The pelvis is held neutral or with a gentle
posterior tuck to correct anterior pelvic tilt, and the trunk is upright, opening
the front of the rear (kneeling) hip. Arms may rest at the sides or reach
overhead.

**Training goal.** Lengthen the anterior hip structures of the rear (kneeling)
side — primarily the iliopsoas (psoas major + iliacus) and rectus femoris — and
improve anterior-hip mobility and pelvic positioning. It is a mobility/flexibility
hold, not a strength movement. Relative to the couch stretch, the rear shin is on
the floor (not pinned up a wall), so the rectus-femoris knee-component stretch is
less isolated; the emphasis is the hip-flexor (iliopsoas) lengthening.

**Intended muscles (stretched).**

- Rear side: psoas major, iliacus (iliopsoas), rectus femoris (mild, since the
  rear knee is flexed on the floor), sartorius, tensor fasciae latae.

**Intended muscles (stabilizing / engaged).**

- Gluteus maximus and abdominal wall (gentle posterior pelvic tilt / pelvic
  control).
- Quadriceps of the front thigh (knee extension hold).
- Trunk: erector spinae, obliques (upright posture), gluteus medius (pelvic
  leveling).

**Common execution style.** The athlete drops into a half-kneel: rear knee on the
floor (rear lower leg may be flat on the floor behind, or the rear foot may be
untucked), front foot planted forward with the front knee at ~90° tracking over
the front toe. The pelvis is held neutral or gently tucked; the athlete may
gently "tuck the tail" to increase the stretch. The trunk is tall. A common
progression is to reach the arms overhead to further open the front of the body.
Hold 30–120 s per side, breathing steadily.

## 2. Pose Purpose

This specification treats the **held bottom position** of the half-kneeling hip
flexor stretch as the canonical audited pose.

**Held position.** The athlete is in a half-kneel: the rear knee rests on the
floor, the rear lower leg lies back along the floor (or the rear foot is relaxed
behind), and the front foot is planted forward with the front knee flexed to ~90°
and tracking over the front toe. The pelvis is neutral or with a gentle posterior
tilt to correct any anterior tilt; the rear hip is in extension so the front of
the rear hip is opened. The trunk is upright. Weight is shared between the front
foot and the rear knee contact.

**Pelvic positioning.** The hallmark is a neutral-to-slightly-tucked pelvis
(correction of anterior pelvic tilt). The lumbar spine is therefore neutral (not
arched). Unlike the couch stretch, the rear foot is typically on the floor behind
rather than pinned up a wall.

**Trunk.** Upright and tall, square to the pelvis. Arms rest at sides, at the
chest, or reach overhead.

**Hold.** Static, no bouncing. The stretch is felt at the front of the rear hip.

---

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment

**Center of mass.** The whole-body COM sits over the base formed by the front
foot and the rear knee contact. It is roughly centered, leaning slightly forward
over the front foot. It must remain within this support region.

**Balance.** Load is shared: the front foot bears the larger share, the rear knee
contact bears the rear share and stabilizes the rear-hip extension. Balanced in
the sagittal plane.

**Symmetry.** Not mirror-symmetric (half-kneel). The pelvis is level side-to-side
(no contralateral hip drop) and square (facing forward, no rotation). The
shoulders are level and square to the pelvis.

**Posture.** Upright trunk. The pelvis is neutral/slightly tucked; the lumbar
spine is neutral (not arched). The front thigh is roughly horizontal; the rear
thigh is vertical (below the rear hip, lower leg back along the floor).

**Visual appearance.** From the side, the body shows an upright trunk over a
front shin-vertical leg and a rear knee on the floor with the lower leg trailing.
From the front, the pelvis is level, the front knee tracks over the front toe, and
the shoulders are square.

---

## 4. Head

**Neck.** The cervical spine is a neutral continuation of the thoracic spine. No
excessive flexion or extension.

**Gaze.** The eyes look forward, toward the horizon or slightly down. The gaze is
level, relaxed.

**Chin.** Neutral and slightly packed; ears roughly above the shoulders from the
side. The head does not jut forward.

**Cervical alignment.** The cervical curve is preserved but not exaggerated; one
gentle, unbroken line. No gooseneck, no turtle tuck.

---

## 5. Spine

**Lumbar.** Neutral (or gently flattened by a mild posterior tilt). The natural
lumbar arch is maintained within the individual's neutral range — not excessively
extended (arched) and not rounded into flexion. The lumbar spine is long.

**Thoracic.** Neutral-to-slightly-extended and tall. The upper back is flat and
broad; no rounding. The rib cage is stacked over the pelvis with the anterior core
engaged.

**Rib cage.** Ribs down, anterior abdominal wall braced. Chest open, not flared.
The thoracic cage moves as a unit with the trunk.

**Pelvis.** Neutral or with a gentle posterior tilt ("gentle tuck" to correct
anterior tilt). The pelvis is level side-to-side and square (no rotation). The
gluteals and abdominals co-contract to hold the position.

**State summary.** Lumbar: neutral (slight flattening acceptable, not arched).
Thoracic: neutral/tall. Pelvis: neutral-to-gently-tucked, level, square. The
neutral/slightly-tucked pelvis opens the rear hip flexors.

---

## 6. Upper Body

**Shoulders (glenohumeral).** Level, square to the pelvis, relaxed away from the
ears. Arms rest at the sides, at the chest, or reach overhead (common progression
to open the front of the body). The humerus is unloaded and does not shrug.

**Scapula.** Flat on the rib cage; no winging or excessive elevation. If arms
reach overhead, the scapulae elevate/upwardly rotate naturally but should not
wing.

**Clavicles.** Level and symmetric; do not ride up toward the neck.

**Elbows.** Free per the chosen arm style. No forced position required.

**Forearms / wrists / hands.** Relaxed, not clenched. No deviation implying
shoulder compensation.

**Note.** The upper body is postural; it must not collapse forward, twist, or
tilt the trunk. Overhead arms increase the anterior-chain stretch but do not
change the lower-body landmarks.

---

## 7. Lower Body

**Rear hip.** In extension (the rear thigh is below/behind the rear hip, knee on
the floor, lower leg trailing). The rear hip flexors are lengthened. A gentle
posterior tilt increases this lengthening.

**Rear knee.** Flexed (~90°+) and resting on the floor. The rear knee is the rear
support point, under or slightly behind the rear hip.

**Rear ankle / foot.** The rear lower leg trails back along the floor; the rear
foot may be relaxed (dorsum near the floor) or the rear foot may be untucked.
The rear foot does not bear a driving load.

**Front hip.** Flexed to ~90° so the front thigh is roughly horizontal. The front
hip is in a neutral-to-slightly-open position.

**Front knee.** Flexed to ~90° and tracking over the front foot/toe. No valgus,
no varus. Front shin near-vertical.

**Front ankle.** Dorsiflexed; the front foot flat with the heel planted.

**Front foot.** Flat on the floor — heel and toe both down — pointing forward (or
very slightly outward). Primary load-bearing contact (with the rear knee).

**Weight distribution.** Shared between the front foot (larger share) and the
rear knee contact. The rear foot bears no driving load.

---

## 8. Contacts

**Front foot–floor contact (×1, primary).**

- *Contact surface:* the whole sole — heel and toe/ball — flat on the floor.
- *Expected pressure:* the larger load share; heaviest under the heel/midfoot
  through the toe; knee tracks over this foot.
- *Stability:* fixed. The front foot does not slide or lift.
- *Visual appearance:* flat foot, heel down, toes forward, knee tracking over
  the toes.

**Rear knee–floor contact (×1, primary rear).**

- *Contact surface:* the rear knee (patellar region / prepatellar soft tissue) on
  the floor.
- *Expected pressure:* a meaningful rear share; stabilized by the trailing lower
  leg.
- *Stability:* fixed. The rear knee does not slide.
- *Visual appearance:* rear knee on the floor under/behind the rear hip; lower
  leg trailing back.

No hand contact with the floor in the canonical half-kneeling hip flexor stretch
(hands rest at sides, chest, or overhead). Front-knee or torso contact with the
floor is absent. Unlike the couch stretch, there is no rear-shin prop contact.

---

## 9. Range of Motion

**Expected ROM.**

- Rear hip: extended (thigh behind/below the pelvis) with the knee flexed; the
  iliopsoas is near end-range lengthening. A gentle posterior tilt increases it.
- Rear knee: flexed ~90°+, knee on floor; rear lower leg trails.
- Front hip: flexed ~90° (thigh horizontal).
- Front knee: flexed ~90°, tracking over the toe.
- Front ankle: dorsiflexed, heel down.
- Spine: lumbar neutral (slightly flattened), thoracic tall; little other change.

**Acceptable variation.**

- Front foot further or closer (closer increases rear-hip stretch intensity).
- Rear knee slightly behind the rear hip.
- Arms at sides, at chest, or overhead.
- Gentle posterior tilt vs strict neutral (both acceptable; the key is no
  anterior tilt).
- Slight individual variation in neutral lumbar curvature.

**Unacceptable variation.**

- Anterior pelvic tilt / lumbar arch (defeats the intent; pelvis must be neutral
  or tucked).
- Front knee valgus/varus or front heel lifting.
- Rear knee lifting off the floor.
- Pelvis drop, rotation, or side tilt.
- Torso collapsing forward or twisting.
- Rear foot taking a driving load that lifts the rear knee.
- Asymmetric shoulder tilt.

---

## 10. Muscle Intent

Structures that should *visually* appear lengthened / under stretch (rear side):

- **Iliopsoas (psoas major + iliacus)** — primary hip-flexor stretch at the front
  of the rear hip; tension felt deep in the anterior hip.
- **Rectus femoris** — mild stretch (rear knee flexed, hip extended); less
  isolated than in the couch stretch because the rear foot is on the floor.
- **Sartorius / tensor fasciae latae** — secondary anterior-hip structures.

Muscles that should *visually* appear engaged (to hold the position):

- **Gluteus maximus (rear side)** — co-contracts to maintain pelvic position and
  rear-hip extension.
- **Rectus abdominis / obliques** — gentle pelvic control / abdominal bracing.
- **Quadriceps (front thigh)** — knee extension hold of the front leg.
- **Erector spinae** — upright thoracic posture.
- **Gluteus medius (both sides)** — pelvic leveling.

The body reads as "neutral/tucked pelvis, upright trunk, rear hip opened, front
leg stacked."

An auditor should be able to confirm each of the following from the rendered

3. ✓ Rear hip is in extension (thigh below/behind the pelvis).
4. ✓ Front foot is flat, heel down, pointing forward.
5. ✓ Front knee flexed ~90°, tracking over the front toe.
6. ✓ Front knee shows no valgus or varus.
7. ✓ Front shin near-vertical; front heel on the floor.
8. ✓ Front thigh roughly horizontal.
9. ✓ Pelvis is neutral or gently tucked (not anteriorly tilted).
10. ✓ Lumbar spine is neutral (not arched, not rounded into flexion).
11. ✓ Thoracic spine tall and broad — no rounding.
12. ✓ Rib cage stacked and braced; chest open.
13. ✓ Pelvis level side-to-side — no contralateral hip drop.
14. ✓ Pelvis square to the front — no rotation.
15. ✓ Neck neutral continuation of the spine; chin neutral and packed.
16. ✓ Gaze forward.
17. ✓ Shoulders level and square to the pelvis, not shrugged.
18. ✓ Scapulae flat — no winging.
19. ✓ No trunk twist; shoulders and hips face the same direction.
20. ✓ Front foot does not slide or lift.
21. ✓ Rear knee does not slide; rear knee stays on the floor.
22. ✓ The stretch is visibly at the front of the rear hip (pelvis opening it).

25. ✓ Arms positioned consistently (sides / chest / overhead) without distorting
     the trunk.

     lower leg trailing.
27. ✓ The held position is static (no bouncing).
28. ✓ No anterior pelvic tilt / lumbar arch.
29. ✓ Rear foot not bearing a driving load that lifts the rear knee.
30. ✓ The rendered pose matches the visual checkpoints in §11.

## 12. Common Mistakes

**Anterior pelvic tilt / arched lumbar ("butt out").** The athlete arches the
lower back and tilts the pelvis anteriorly. *Why wrong:* off-loads the hip
flexors; the stretch is lost and the lumbar is stressed. *Visual difference:* the
lower back bows, the butt pokes back, the front of the hip relaxes.

**Front heel lifting.** The front heel rises. *Why wrong:* reduces front-leg
stability and shifts balance. *Visual difference:* front foot rolls onto the toe;
front shin pitches forward.

**Front knee valgus / not tracking the toe.** The front knee caves inward. *Why
wrong:* medial knee stress. *Visual difference:* front knee inside the toe line;
ankle rolls in.

**Pelvis drop / rotate.** The trailing-side hip drops or the pelvis rotates. *Why
wrong:* poor pelvic control; the opening is lost. *Visual difference:* hips not
level/square; one iliac crest lower or rotated forward.

**Rounded upper back / forward collapse.** The torso pitches forward. *Why
wrong:* reduces the anterior-chain stretch and loads the lumbar. *Visual
difference:* shoulders ahead of the hips; upper back rounds.

**Rear knee lifting off the floor.** The rear knee comes up. *Why wrong:* reduces
rear-hip extension and the stretch. *Visual difference:* the rear thigh leaves the
vertical; the body rises.

**Twisting torso / uneven shoulders.** Shoulders rotate or tilt. *Why wrong:*
poor trunk control. *Visual difference:* shoulders not square to the pelvis.

**Rear foot driving load (lifting the rear knee).** The rear foot pushes so the
rear knee leaves the floor. *Why wrong:* defeats the kneeling anchor. *Visual
difference:* the rear knee is off the floor; weight shifts rearward.

## 13. Acceptance Criteria

The half-kneeling hip flexor stretch (held position) is **accepted only if every
criterion is satisfied**:

- [ ] Rear knee on the floor, under or slightly behind the rear hip.
- [ ] Rear lower leg trailing; rear foot not bearing a driving load.
- [ ] Rear hip in extension; rear hip flexors visibly lengthened.
- [ ] Front foot flat, heel down, forward, not sliding.
- [ ] Front knee flexed ~90°, tracking over the front toe, no valgus/varus.
- [ ] Front shin near-vertical; front heel on the floor.
- [ ] Front thigh roughly horizontal.
- [ ] Pelvis neutral or gently tucked (no anterior tilt); lumbar neutral.
- [ ] Thoracic spine tall; no rounding.
- [ ] Rib cage stacked and braced; chest open.
- [ ] Pelvis level and square; no drop or rotation.
- [ ] Neck continues the spinal line; chin neutral and packed.
- [ ] Shoulders level, square, not shrugged; scapulae flat.
- [ ] No trunk twist; shoulders and hips face the same direction.
- [ ] Gaze forward.
- [ ] Rear knee stays on the floor; front foot does not slide or lift.
- [ ] The stretch is driven at the front of the rear hip by the pelvic position.
- [ ] The rendered pose matches the visual checkpoints in §11.
