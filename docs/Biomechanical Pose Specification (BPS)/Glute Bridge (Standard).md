# Biomechanical Pose Specification — Glute Bridge (Standard)

## 1. Exercise overview
The standard glute bridge (hip thrust) is a supine (lying on the back), closed-chain lower-body
exercise in which the shoulders and feet are fixed to the floor and the hips are driven upward into
full hip extension. It isolates the hip extensor chain — principally the gluteus maximus — and trains
the posterior tilt and lifting of the pelvis against gravity. The body forms a straight diagonal line
from the shoulders through the hips to the knees at the top.

## 2. Pose purpose
To strengthen hip extension and teach pelvic control, with emphasis on the gluteus maximus and
hamstrings and on maintaining a neutral lumbar spine (not overarching). It is used for posterior-chain
development, glute activation, and rehabilitation of hip-extension patterning. The canonical pose is
the top, fully-extended hold position.

## 3. Whole-body alignment
- Supine: the torso lies on the floor, knees bent, feet flat and close to the hips.
- At the top, the body forms a straight line from the shoulders through the hips to the knees.
- The pelvis is lifted and neutral — extended at the hip but not excessively tilted (no lumbar
  overarching).
- The center of mass is supported between the shoulder/upper-back base and the two feet.
- Symmetrical: both hips rise equally; the pelvis stays level side to side.

## 4. Head
- Head rests on the floor, cervical spine neutral.
- The chin is tucked slightly, not jutting; the neck is relaxed, not strained.
- No cervical extension (looking sharply up) that would compress the neck.

## 5. Spine
- Lumbar and thoracic spine form a straight, neutral line from shoulders to hips at the top — the
  spine is NOT arched beyond neutral.
- The lift comes from hip extension (pelvis rotating forward on the femurs), not from pushing the
  lower back into the floor or overarching it.
- Abdominal and gluteal co-contraction keeps the pelvis neutral and stable.

## 6. Upper body
- The upper back, shoulders, and head form the fixed posterior base of support.
- Shoulder blades can rest naturally; the arms lie at the sides with palms down or are used lightly
  for balance.
- The chest is open; the upper body is stable and does not lift off the floor (except the naturally
  elevated shoulders at the top of the bridge).

## 7. Lower body
- Setup: lying supine, knees bent ~90°, feet flat hip-width apart, close enough that the shins are
  near vertical at the top.
- Drive: the heels press into the floor and the glutes contract to extend the hips, lifting the
  pelvis.
- At the top: hips fully extended (thighs in line with the torso), knees still bent ~90°, ankles
  near neutral.
- The quadriceps keep the knees tracking straight (no valgus); the hamstrings assist hip extension.
- The pelvis is neutral — not tucked under (insufficient) nor arched (excessive anterior tilt).

## 8. Contacts
- Upper back, shoulders, and head rest on the floor (posterior base of support).
- Both feet flat on the floor, heels driving down; weight shared between upper back and feet.
- No hand contact bearing load (arms are for light balance only).
- Base of support spans from the upper-back contact to the two feet.

## 9. Range of Motion
- Hip extension: from ~90° flexion (setup) to ~0° (full extension at the top).
- Knee flexion: held near ~90° throughout (minimal change).
- Ankle: near neutral to slight dorsiflexion as the heels drive.
- Lumbar spine: neutral; only pelvic rotation (posterior-to-neutral tilt) occurs, no spinal
  articulation beyond the natural curve.
- Shoulder: fixed; minimal movement.

## 10. Muscle intent
- Primary mover: gluteus maximus (chief hip extensor); hamstrings assist hip extension.
- Stabilizers: gluteus medius/minimus (pelvic level); erector spinae and abdominals (neutral pelvis/
  spine); quadriceps (knee tracking, shin stability); calf/foot intrinsics (heel drive).

## 11. Visual checkpoints
- [ ] Supine position, knees bent ~90°, feet flat hip-width.
- [ ] Feet close enough that shins are near vertical at the top.
- [ ] Heels drive into the floor to lift the hips.
- [ ] Hips rise to full extension (thighs in line with torso).
- [ ] Body forms a straight line shoulder–hip–knee.
- [ ] Pelvis is neutral, not arched or tucked.
- [ ] Lower back is NOT over-arched at the top.
- [ ] Knees track straight, no valgus.
- [ ] Glutes visibly engaged (squeeze at the top).
- [ ] Shoulder/upper back stays as the stable base.
- [ ] Head and neck relaxed, neutral.
- [ ] Pelvis level side to side, both hips equal.
- [ ] Arms resting or lightly balancing, not bearing load.
- [ ] Chest open, shoulders down.
- [ ] Breath controlled; exhale on the lift.
- [ ] Hold at the top is steady, not pulsing.
- [ ] Both feet evenly loaded.
- [ ] No cervical strain (no sharp upward gaze).
- [ ] Smooth lift and lower.
- [ ] Symmetry between left and right.

## 12. Common mistakes
- Over-arching the lower back (excessive anterior pelvic tilt) instead of true hip extension — the
  lift should come from the glutes, not the spine.
- Tucking the pelvis under (not reaching full extension).
- Driving through the toes with heels lifting, reducing glute recruitment.
- Letting the knees cave inward.
- Raising the shoulders off the floor or shrugging.
- Straining the neck by looking sharply upward.
- Asymmetric hip rise (one hip higher).
- Using momentum instead of a controlled glute contraction.

## 13. Acceptance criteria
- [ ] Hips reach full extension with a straight shoulder–hip–knee line.
- [ ] Pelvis neutral, no lumbar over-arch and no tuck.
- [ ] Heels flat, driving the lift; feet evenly loaded.
- [ ] Knees track straight, no valgus.
- [ ] Upper back/shoulders form a stable base; head/neck neutral.
- [ ] Glutes engaged; movement controlled and symmetric.
