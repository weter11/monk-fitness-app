# Biomechanical Pose Specification — Push-Up

> **Canonical reference.** This document defines what a push-up should look like
> from a strict biomechanics, anatomy, and strength-and-conditioning standpoint.
> It is implementation-independent and describes human movement only. It must
> remain valid even if the rendering system behind it is rewritten from scratch.
>
> Sources: sports biomechanics, physical-therapy literature, strength &
> conditioning practice, and human anatomy. Where expert opinion differs, the
> consensus is stated and the alternatives are noted.

---

## 1. Exercise Overview

**Movement goal.** A push-up is a closed-chain, prone pressing pattern in which
the body moves as a single rigid plank between a high (top) and low (bottom)
position by flexion and extension of the elbows while the hands and feet remain
fixed on the floor.

**Training goal.** Develop horizontal pushing strength and trunk rigidity, with
primary emphasis on the pectoralis major, anterior deltoid, and triceps brachii,
and concurrent demand on the scapular stabilizers and the entire anterior and
posterior trunk for whole-body stiffness.

**Intended muscles.**

- Prime movers: pectoralis major (sternal head), anterior deltoid, triceps
  brachii (lateral and long heads).
- Scapular stabilizers: serratus anterior, lower trapezius, rhomboids (depending
  on protraction/retraction strategy).
- Trunk: rectus abdominis, external/internal obliques, erector spinae,
  gluteus maximus and medius (for pelvic and hip extension stiffness).
- Supporting: rotator cuff (humeral_head centration), quadriceps (knee
  extension hold).

**Common execution style.** The athlete adopts a prone plank: hands beneath the
shoulders, body straight from shoulders through hips to ankles, core braced,
and performs a controlled descent until the chest approaches the floor, then
presses back to the top. Breathing is typically exhale on the press, inhale on
the descent. Tempo is smooth, with no sag, pike, or snapping at the joints.

---

## 2. Pose Purpose

The push-up is defined by several canonical poses along its range. This
specification treats the two end-range poses (top and bottom) as the audited
canonical poses and describes the transition between them.

**Top position (lockout).** The body is fully extended as a rigid plank; elbows
straight (not hyperextended) and shoulders directly above the hands. This is the
starting and finishing pose.

**Bottom position (chest-down).** The body has been lowered by elbow flexion so
the chest is just above (or lightly touching) the floor, elbows at roughly
45–70° of flexion relative to the torso, while the rigid-plank relationship of
the trunk is preserved.

**Transition.** The descent and ascent are produced **only** by elbow flexion/
extension (and the accompanying scapulohumeral motion). The trunk, pelvis, hips,
and legs remain a fixed straight segment; depth is created by the arms, not by
bending the spine or dropping the pelvis through the feet.

**Scapular strategy.** At the top, the shoulder blades are typically in slight
protraction or neutral; through the descent they allow controlled scapular
motion (retraction at the bottom for most coaching models, or maintained
protraction for a "tight" plank). The consensus model used here: the scapulae
track naturally with the humeral motion and do not wing, elevate excessively, or
shrug the shoulders toward the ears.

---

## 3. Whole-Body Alignment

**Center of mass.** The whole-body COM sits roughly mid-torso and travels
vertically along a near-vertical line between the hands and feet as the body
lowers and rises. It must remain within the support polygon defined by the hand
and foot contacts.

**Balance.** The load is shared between the hands (anterior support) and the
toes/balls of the feet (posterior support). The body is a lever balanced on
these two contact regions; no single contact should bear a disproportionate,
unintended load that tilts the plank.

**Symmetry.** Left and right sides are mirror-symmetric. Both hands press
equally; both shoulders, elbows, and hips are level with their contralateral
counterparts. No rotation or side-bending of the trunk.

**Posture.** A single straight line connects the shoulder, hip, and ankle. The
neck is a neutral continuation of the spine (not arched up or tucked down). The
head, chest, and pelvis neither sag toward the floor nor pike upward.

**Visual appearance.** From the side, the body reads as a straight board tilted
prone, pivoting only at the shoulders and anchored at the hands and toes. From
the front, the shoulders are square, the hands are directly under the shoulders,
and the body forms a clean, symmetric silhouette with no sway or twist.

---

## 4. Head

**Neck.** The cervical spine is in a neutral, elongated position — a natural
continuation of the thoracic spine. There is no excessive flexion (chin tucked
to chest) or extension (head craned up).

**Gaze.** The eyes look down and slightly forward at the floor a short distance
ahead of the hands, or straight down. The gaze is relaxed, not strained upward.

**Chin.** The chin is held in a neutral, slightly tucked (pack-the-neck) position
such that the ears align roughly over the shoulders when viewed from the side.
The head is not jutting forward.

**Cervical alignment.** The cervical curve is preserved but not exaggerated. The
skull, cervical spine, and thoracic spine form one gentle, unbroken line. No
"gooseneck" (cervical extension) and no "turtle tuck" (cervical flexion).

---

## 5. Spine

**Lumbar.** Neutral. The natural lumbar arch is maintained without excessive
extension (swayback) or flexion (rounded lower back). The lumbar region is held
rigid as part of the plank; it does not flex to sag the belly toward the floor
or extend to pike the hips up.

**Thoracic.** Neutral-to-slightly-extended. The upper back is flat and broad.
There is no rounding (kyphosis) of the mid-back. The rib cage is stacked over
the pelvis with the anterior core engaged so the chest does not collapse.

**Rib cage.** The ribs are down and the anterior abdominal wall is braced
("ribs stacked, belly tight"). The chest is not flared open nor compressed. The
thoracic cage moves as a unit with the trunk, not independently.

**Pelvis.** Neutral. The pelvis is level (no anterior or posterior tilt beyond
the individual's neutral standing posture) and square to the floor. It does not
rotate, hike, or tilt side-to-side. The gluteals and anterior core co-contract
to hold the pelvis as a fixed anchor of the plank.

**State summary.** Lumbar: neutral. Thoracic: neutral (slight extension
acceptable). Pelvis: neutral, level, square. None of these segments flex,
extend, rotate, or side-bend to create or fake pressing depth.

---

## 6. Upper Body

**Shoulders (glenohumeral).** The humerus is positioned so the shoulder joint
sits directly above the hand at the top, and travels forward/down relative to the
hand through the descent. The humeral head stays centered in the socket (no
anterior glide or shrugging). The shoulders are depressed and retracted enough to
keep them away from the ears.

**Scapula.** The shoulder blades glide smoothly on the rib cage. They do not
wing (medial border lifting off the thorax) and do not excessively elevate.
Coaching consensus varies between maintained protraction (tight plank) and
retraction at the bottom; both are acceptable provided the scapulae remain flat
and stable rather than winging or shrugging.

**Clavicles.** The clavicles are level and symmetric, continuing the line of the
shoulders. They do not ride up toward the neck (trap-dominant shrug) nor drop.

**Elbows.** At the top, the elbows are fully extended but not hyperextended
(slight soft lock). At the bottom, the elbows are flexed to roughly 45–90°,
tucked at approximately a 45–70° angle relative to the torso (elbows should not
splay wide to 90° nor be glued to the ribs at 0°). Both elbows are symmetric.

**Forearms.** The forearms are vertical (or near-vertical) when viewed from the
front, connecting the elbow to the hand under the shoulder. They are rigid and
bear load in line with the upper arm. No pronation/supination deviation beyond
what the neutral hand placement requires.

**Wrists.** The wrists are in neutral extension/flexion (roughly straight),
aligned under the forearms. They are not bent sharply back (excessive extension)
or folded forward. The load distributes across the palm, not集中在 the heel of
the hand or the fingertips alone.

**Hands.** Hands are flat on the floor, fingers spread for stability, pointing
forward or very slightly outward. The hands are placed directly beneath the
shoulders (slightly wider than shoulder-width is the common standard; the
consensus canonical width is shoulder-width to 1.2× shoulder-width). The whole
palm contacts the floor; the hand does not claw, curl, or lift.

---

## 7. Lower Body

**Hip.** The hips are extended and held in neutral — the body is a straight line,
not a piked (hips-high) or sagging (hips-low) shape. The hip joints are the rear
anchor of the plank.

**Femur.** The femurs run straight back from the hips to the knees, parallel to
the floor, as part of the rigid posterior chain. There is no external or internal
rotation that twists the legs out of the plank plane.

**Knee.** The knees are fully extended (locked but not hyperextended) and held
straight. The lower legs point back to the feet; the knee is a rigid link, not a
bent hinge. No knee valgus (inward collapse) or varus.

**Ankle.** The ankles are in a neutral-to-slightly plantarflexed position so the
weight rests on the toes/balls of the feet. The ankle is not dorsiflexed such
that the heels drop, nor sharply pointed.

**Foot.** The feet are dorsum-down on the floor, resting on the toes/balls of the
feet (the top of the foot may contact the floor depending on ankle mobility). The
feet are close together or at hip width, symmetric. They are the posterior
contact anchor.

**Toe direction.** The toes point straight back (away from the head), parallel to
each other. They do not splay outward or cross inward.

**Weight distribution.** The load is distributed along the length of the body
between the two hand regions (front) and the two foot regions (rear). Within the
feet, pressure is on the toe/ball region. The pelvis/hips carry no disproportionate
shift; the plank remains balanced.

---

## 8. Contacts

**Hand–floor contacts (×2).**

- *Contact surface:* palm (and spread fingers) flat on the floor; fingers
  providing grip and sensory stability.
- *Expected pressure:* distributed across the whole palm, heaviest under the
  heel-of-palm and finger bases; symmetric left/right.
- *Stability:* fixed. The hands do not slide, drift, or lift during the
  movement. They are the anterior pivot.
- *Visual appearance:* flat palms, fingers fanned, wrists straight, hands under
  the shoulders.

**Foot–floor contacts (×2).**

- *Contact surface:* the toe/ball region of the foot (and, depending on ankle
  mobility, the dorsal top of the foot) on the floor.
- *Expected pressure:* on the toe/ball of the foot; symmetric left/right; the
  heel is raised.
- *Stability:* fixed. The feet do not slide or lift; they are the posterior
  anchor of the lever.
- *Visual appearance:* feet pointing straight back, close together, body extended
  off the floor behind the ankles.

No other contacts exist (no knee, chest, or hip contact with the floor in the
canonical strict push-up; light chest-floor touch at the bottom is permissible
only as the lowest acceptable extreme and must not involve the abdomen or pelvis
touching).

---

## 9. Range of Motion

**Expected ROM.**

- Elbow: from ~0° extension (top) to ~45–90° flexion (bottom). A full,
  standards-compliant repetition reaches the bottom where the chest is within a
  few centimeters of the floor (or lightly touches).
- Shoulder: horizontal adduction / humeral depression through the descent; the
  upper arm travels from vertical (top) to ~45–70° from the torso (bottom).
- Scapula: controlled protraction/retraction and depression tracking the humerus.
- Spine/hips/knees: essentially no change in joint angle — they remain rigid
  across the entire movement.

**Acceptable variation.**

- Bottom elbow angle between 45° and 90° (depth preference varies by standard).
- Hand width from shoulder-width to 1.2× shoulder-width.
- Slight individual variation in neutral lumbar/thoracic curvature.
- Light chest-floor touch at the lowest point.
- Minor scapular protraction-vs-retraction strategy difference (see §2/§6).

**Unacceptable variation.**

- Elbow flexion below ~45° (chest not lowered enough) — incomplete ROM.
- Elbow splay to ~90° from torso (wide "T" press) — changes the movement pattern.
- Any sag of the lumbar spine / pelvis dropping below the shoulder–ankle line.
- Any pike (hips rising above the shoulder–ankle line).
- Knee bend or knee/hip drop to the floor.
- Head crane up or tuck down breaking the neck–spine line.
- Hand or foot sliding, lifting, or drifting.
- Asymmetric loading (one side lower, rotated, or tilted).

## 10. Muscle Intent

Muscles that should *visually* appear engaged (tense, defined, contributing to
the rigid-plank shape):

- **Pectoralis major** — primary presser; visible tension across the chest.
- **Anterior deltoid** — co-presser at the front of the shoulder.
- **Triceps brachii** — extends the elbow; visible on the back of the upper arm,
  especially at the top/lockout.
- **Serratus anterior** — protracts/stableizes the scapula; visible along the
  side of the rib cage ("boxer's muscle").
- **Trapezius (lower/middle)** — depressed, retracted scapula stability.
- **Rectus abdominis / obliques** — anterior trunk rigidity (no sag).
- **Erector spinae** — posterior trunk rigidity, preserves the plank line.
- **Gluteus maximus / medius** — hip extension and pelvic stabilization (keeps
  the plank straight and level).
- **Quadriceps** — knee extension hold.
- **Rotator cuff** — humeral_head centration (implied stability, not prominently
  visible).

The body as a whole reads as "switched on" and rigid, not slack.

An auditor should be able to confirm each of the following from the rendered
pose:

1. ✓ Body forms a straight line from shoulders through hips to ankles (side view).

3. ✓ Elbows are fully extended (not hyperextended) at the top.
4. ✓ At the bottom, elbows are flexed ~45–90° and tucked ~45–70° from the torso.

6. ✓ Wrists are neutral (not bent sharply back or forward).
7. ✓ Forearms are vertical / in line with the upper arms from the front.
8. ✓ Shoulders are level and not shrugged toward the ears.
9. ✓ Scapulae are flat on the rib cage — no winging.
10. ✓ Neck is a neutral continuation of the spine (no gooseneck, no turtle tuck).
11. ✓ Gaze is down/forward at the floor, not craned upward.
12. ✓ Chin is neutral and packed, ears roughly over the shoulders.
13. ✓ Lumbar spine is neutral — no sag (belly dropping) or sway.
14. ✓ Thoracic spine is flat/broad — no rounding of the upper back.
15. ✓ Rib cage is stacked and braced, not flared or collapsed.
16. ✓ Pelvis is level, square, and neutral — no anterior/posterior tilt.
17. ✓ Hips are not piked above or sagging below the shoulder–ankle line.
18. ✓ Knees are fully extended (locked but not hyperextended), straight.
19. ✓ Feet point straight back, parallel, resting on the toes/balls.

29. ✓ The head does not jut forward past the hand line.
30. ✓ The overall silhouette is a clean, symmetric prone plank.

## 12. Common Mistakes

**Sagging hips / lumbar drop ("banana with a dip").** The pelvis and belly drop
below the shoulder–ankle line. *Why wrong:* it off-loads the press, strains the
lumbar spine, and breaks the plank. *Visual difference:* the lower back bows
downward; the trunk is no longer straight; the COM shifts rearward/downward.

**Piking / hips-high ("downward dog push-up").** The hips rise above the line.
*Why wrong:* it shortens the lever, off-loads the chest, and changes the
movement into an inclined press. *Visual difference:* the posterior chain angles
upward; the body is no longer horizontal; the shoulders are behind the hands.

**Flared / splayed elbows (90° from torso).** Elbows wing straight out to the
sides. *Why wrong:* increases shoulder-joint stress and shifts load off the
pectorals onto the deltoids; changes the pattern. *Visual difference:* the upper
arms form a "T" rather than a tucked ~45–70° angle; the silhouette is wide.

**Head crane / neck extension.** The head lifts and looks forward/up. *Why wrong:*
breaks the cervical–thoracic line and can strain the neck. *Visual difference:*
the neck arches upward; the forehead leads rather than the chest.

**Chin tuck / head drop.** The head drops toward the chest. *Why wrong:* breaks
the spinal line and misaligns the gaze. *Visual difference:* the neck flexes; the
crown of the head dips below the shoulder line.

**Shrugged shoulders.** The trapezius elevates the shoulders toward the ears.
*Why wrong:* reduces scapular stability and increases neck strain. *Visual
difference:* the clavicles ride up; a "hunched" shoulder appearance; shortened
neck.

**Winged scapula.** The medial border of the scapula lifts off the rib cage.
*Why wrong:* indicates poor scapular control and unstable shoulder mechanics.
*Visual difference:* a visible ridge/bump along the upper back; asymmetry
common.

**Incomplete depth.** The chest stays far from the floor; elbows barely bend.
*Why wrong:* fails the ROM standard. *Visual difference:* the body hovers high;
the elbows remain near-straight.

**Knee bend / knee drop.** The knees flex or the knees/legs drop to the floor.
*Why wrong:* breaks the rigid-plank lever and changes the movement. *Visual
difference:* the posterior chain bends at the knee; the lower legs may contact
the floor.

**Asymmetric loading.** One side lower, rotated, or tilted. *Why wrong:* indicates
uneven press mechanics and core instability. *Visual difference:* the shoulders
or hips are not level; the body rotates about its long axis.

**Hand or foot sliding.** A hand or foot drifts during the movement. *Why wrong:*
the contacts are fixed anchors; sliding invalidates the lever. *Visual
difference:* the hand/foot position relative to the body changes between top and
bottom.

## 13. Acceptance Criteria

The push-up pose (top and bottom) is **accepted only if every criterion is
satisfied**:

- [ ] The body forms a single straight line: shoulder–hip–ankle (side view).
- [ ] The pelvis is level, square, and neutral (no tilt, hike, or rotation).
- [ ] The lumbar and thoracic spines are neutral (no sag, sway, or rounding).
- [ ] The neck continues the spinal line; the chin is neutral and packed.
- [ ] The gaze is down/forward at the floor.
- [ ] At the top: elbows extended (not hyperextended), shoulders over hands.
- [ ] At the bottom: elbows flexed ~45–90°, tucked ~45–70° from the torso, chest
      near the floor.
- [ ] Hands are flat, fingers spread, under the shoulders, not sliding.
- [ ] Wrists are neutral; forearms aligned with the upper arms.
- [ ] Shoulders are level, depressed, not shrugged; scapulae flat (no winging).
- [ ] Knees are fully extended (not hyperextended) and straight.
- [ ] Feet point straight back, parallel, resting on the toes/balls, not sliding.
- [ ] Left and right sides are symmetric (no rotation or side tilt).
- [ ] Depth is achieved by elbow flexion only; no spinal or pelvic compensation.
- [ ] The whole body reads as rigid and braced (engaged trunk and glutes).
- [ ] No knee, hip, or abdomen contact with the floor (light chest touch only at
      the lowest acceptable extreme).
- [ ] The rendered pose matches the visual checkpoints in §11.
